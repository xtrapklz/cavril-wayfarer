# PRIMUS — Arcs A, B, C (Full Treatments)

*Three of the six main side-quest arcs, fleshed for the table. Arc A is the spine;
B and C hang from it. Read alongside `PRIMUS-BIBLE.md`. Canon precedence:
in-code `JOURNEY_THREADS` > the bible > these treatments > improvisation.*

**The one law under all three:** the fey always take, and *grudging payment costs
double*. Generosity is the only safe currency, and it is never quite enough. Every
ending below is measured against that law.

**The braid in one breath.** The forest is calling in a debt (**A**). One mortal broker
has spent a fortune steering the party toward that debt so she can pay hers with theirs
(**B**). One mortal war-band is racing the party to loot the debt as if it were treasure,
and learning it is not (**C**). The Tithe-Warden tallies the party's generosity; the
Glovewright tries to buy them off the tally; Cadoc tries to rob the till. They share NPCs
(the Glovewright, the Collector, Wrenna), an object (the glove), and a thesis the whole
campaign argues: **give gladly, or pay double.**

# Arc A — The Tithe of the Forest

## Premise & themes

The Dreaming Forest is not waking. It is *collecting*. Long ago — the world's first
winter, the one no song remembers the end of — the living world bargained with the forest
for spring, and the price was deferred. It has never stopped accruing. Every thread the
party walks is a line item: the plague's shared dream is the debt leaking upstream; the
river's drowned bell is the debt counting heads; the changeling Wrenna chases is one
payment already made and regretted. The party did not choose to walk the collection route.
They are simply the ones who *notice* the road ordering itself into rows, the butterfly
that casts no shadow, the fellow traveller whose boot-prints number one more than the
party. And the forest has been told they are coming. The Tithe-Warden carries their name
in a ledger bound in bark before they ever speak it aloud.

The theme is **debt as intimacy**. The forest does not hate the world; it *owns* a piece
of it, the way a creditor owns a piece of you, and it is unfailingly courteous about the
collection — funeral courtesy, the same gracious patience the Collector's clerk shows
(Arc E). The party's only lever is the one thing the fey cannot counterfeit: a gift given
*gladly*, before it is asked. The campaign keeps a running tally of that generosity (the
Tithe-Warden's ledger is the diegetic face of it), and at the threshold the tally is read
back. What the party gave freely buys passage and a *voice* in the bargain. What they
hoarded is taken anyway — doubled — out of someone they love. Wrenna is the barometer:
she has walked this route before, and whether she returns with her girl or hangs at the
treeline as a swaying shoe tells the party what their own ledger reads.

## The spine (beat by beat)

### A1 — The Butterfly With No Shadow
- **Where.** *Temperate.* A bluebell meadow over hoof-torn earth — Greywether Common, a
  drovers' grazing-flat with a leaning waystone, half a day out from the first village.
- **Who.** First sighting of **Wrenna**, walking the same direction, a child's shoe on a
  cord at her throat. She never asks the party's name.
- **What they do.** Notice a butterfly the colour of stained glass that casts *no shadow*
  on the bright grass. That night, camping, every party member dreams the same dream — a
  far place of pale trees and a voice singing just ahead — a dream a plague-village will
  describe, word for word, two legs on (ties Arc D).
- **The choice.** Follow the butterfly, kill it, or let it be. **Follow** → it leads to a
  ring of toadstools and a single child's footprint, fresh, too small (first whisper of
  Wrenna's girl). **Kill it** → it crumbles to a pinch of grave-dirt, and Wrenna, if near,
  goes white: *"You should not have. Now it knows your hand."* (A small early debit on the
  tally.) **Let it be** → it lands on the most generous PC and is gone; that PC's dreams
  are clearer all campaign (a foreshadowing boon, not a curse).
- **Downstream.** The party are now *marked carriers* of the dream. Whatever they chose
  here is the Warden's first ledger line.

### A2 — The Bell That Counts
- **Where.** *Water / river.* The crossing at Marrow Ford, where a drowned bell hangs
  offshore with no boat to swing it (the in-code water signature).
- **Who.** The **Ferryman** — or his twin; the party will not be sure which. He poles them
  across before they can hail him and waves off their coin.
- **What they do.** Cross. Mid-river the bell tolls — slow, underwater, *counting* — and
  the current tugs every PC a hand's-width toward it, as if the river leans to read them.
  The Ferryman names it: *"The river remembers the bell, and the bell remembers a debt."*
- **The choice.** Pay the Ferryman anyway (he refuses coin — but accepts a *story*, or a
  kindness done for the next traveller); demand he explain; or say nothing. Accepting his
  free passage *without offering anything back* is a debt incurred — the river "gave you
  the easy water; it will want the favour returned" upstream, at the source. Offering him
  something freely is the campaign's first big *glad-gift* tally credit.
- **Downstream.** The party are now ON the ledger by name. The drowned bell's count is the
  Warden's headcount baseline — and it already reads *party + 1* (Arc F, the One Who
  Follows, is now formally on the manifest).

### A3 — The Sisters' Quarrel Over the Party
- **Where.** *River / temperate edge.* A hazel thicket at a spring-head, dusk.
- **Who.** The **Green-Eyed Sister** and the **River-Grey Sister** — the Two Sisters, fey
  twins, each reaching for the party from opposite directions.
- **What they do.** Green-Eye presses a **hazelnut** into a PC's hand "for a thirst you
  can't yet name" and is gone between steps. River-Grey offers a dipper of water sweet past
  reason and asks them to drink. The party realise, slowly, that the sisters are *fighting
  over who collects them* — one wants them to arrive owing nothing (carry the hazelnut,
  refuse the water), the other wants them to drink the forest into their dreams and arrive
  already hers.
- **The choice.** Take the hazelnut and refuse the water → a credit on the glad-ledger and
  a literal key item (the hazelnut matters at A6 and in the desert, Arc B). Drink the
  water → sweet, then her cold thumb leaves a mark between the brows that *aches toward the
  forest* for the rest of the campaign (a compass, and a lien). Take both / play them
  against each other → a clever party can, but the sisters remember being used.
- **Downstream.** The chosen sister becomes a recurring face; the spurned one becomes an
  obstacle. The hazelnut, if kept, is the single object that can be *spent* at A6 to buy a
  voice in the bargain without paying flesh.

### A4 — The Warden Falls Into Step
- **Where.** *Boreal* (or *jungle*, GM's leg). A spruce corridor where the sound has gone
  dead and the deadfall has begun, subtly, to lie in *rows* — the road ordering itself as
  the boundary nears.
- **Who.** The **Tithe-Warden** — fey assessor in funeral courtesy, mirroring the
  Collector's clerk so exactly that a party who has met the Collector (Arc E) will feel the
  echo in their spine.
- **What they do.** The Warden falls into step, opens a **ledger bound in bark**, and
  reads. *"Confirming the route. You carry more than you packed."* It counts the party —
  and counts *one extra*. It does not threaten. It tallies. If the party have done glad
  gifts (A1–A3, Arc D cures, Arc C restraint), the Warden notes them aloud with something
  almost like approval; if they have hoarded or grudged, it notes that too, in the same
  even voice.
- **The choice.** Argue the count (the one place mortal wit can shave the bill — see Arc E,
  the Collector's terms can be *argued*); ask what is owed; or refuse to engage. Refusing is
  not free — *"A silence is also an answer. I will record it."* A party that asks the Warden
  honestly what the forest wants gets the truth, plainly, which most parties find worse than
  a riddle.
- **Downstream.** This is the reveal that the bark-ledger is the *same ledger* the Collector
  carries (Arc E, connectivity thread 3). The forest's hunger and the mortal world's debts
  are one account. The "one extra" is named here as a *line the party themselves wrote*
  (Arc F).

### A5 — The Convergence Warning
- **Where.** *Any* — the GM seats this in whichever arc the party advanced most.
- **Who.** Whichever NPC the party invested in most sends a messenger or comes themselves:
  the **Huntsman** (if the Hunt is live — his fey-silver arrow points the way home, toward
  the threshold); **Sister Maready** (if the Shared Dream, Arc D); the **Glovewright** (if
  Arc B is hot); **Sir Cadoc Vane** (if Arc C). They have learned, the hard way, *what the
  forest will ask*, and they have come to warn the party before the threshold.
- **What they do.** Deliver the campaign's thesis in the mouth of someone the party trusts.
  **Wrenna's line lands here, the spine of the whole bible:** *give gladly, or pay double.*
  She says it like a woman reading her own gravestone, because she is.
- **The choice.** Heed the warning and spend the last leg *banking generosity* (curing a
  source, paying a toll for a stranger, sparing Cadoc), or press on as they are. There is no
  branch that removes the bargain — only branches that change who pays it.
- **Downstream.** This beat *retunes* the party's behaviour for the final leg, which is the
  point: the Tithe is not a fight to win but a price to prepare for. Every glad act between
  A5 and A6 is double-weighted on the tally (the forest values gifts given *after* the cost
  is known).

### A6 — The Threshold and the Naming of the Tithe
- **Where.** *Void / forest threshold.* A pale ring of mushrooms; an arch of living fallen
  branch that has grown into a doorway; stars in the wrong places, one of them (the Red
  Star, Arc E) close enough now to read by.
- **Who.** The **Courteous Guide**, who steps from a *solid* trunk — *"You are expected. You
  are always expected. This way, or that way; they arrive at the same place."* The Warden, to
  read the final tally. Wrenna, barefoot, at the water's edge, listening to a child's voice
  only the longing can hear. Whatever army or ally the party have earned (Arc C's Company,
  Arc B's Glovewright, Arc E's flock) stands at their back — or doesn't.
- **What they do.** The Tithe is named *in full*: the forest wants back what was taken to
  make the first spring, and the party's accumulated debts — river, sisters, hunt, plague —
  *are* the down payment. The Warden reads the ledger aloud. The hazelnut, if kept, can be
  spent here to buy a *voice* in the bargain without paying flesh. The Guide opens the way.
- **The choice — the campaign's hinge.** *Give gladly* (let the tally pay, offer freely
  what's still owed) → passage, and a say in the terms. *Bargain by force* (if Arc C's
  Company / a prophet's flock / the Glovewright stand armed at the threshold) → the party can
  *demand* rather than beg, and the forest, for once, negotiates. *Grudge or refuse* → the
  forest takes the price anyway, doubled, from someone the party loves.
- **Downstream.** This *is* the campaign's verdict. See Payoffs.

## Read-aloud moments

> **A1 (the butterfly).** "It is the colour of a cathedral window with the sun behind it —
> reds you can taste, a blue that hums. It lands on the bluebells at your feet. And the grass
> beneath it stays bright. No shadow. Not a thin one, not a pale one. None. As if the light
> went *through* the place where it should be, and found nothing there to stop it."

> **A4 (the Warden's tally).** "It walks beside you without your having heard it arrive,
> matching your pace exactly, and it opens a ledger whose pages are bark — pale birch, written
> in a hand like root-hair. It does not look at you. It looks at the page. 'Confirming the
> route,' it says, in a voice as gentle as a clerk telling you the office is about to close.
> 'You carry more than you packed.' Its finger stops on a line. Counts your party. Then moves
> on — to one more line. To one of you who is not there."

> **A6 (the naming).** "The branch overhead is fallen, and it is *alive*, and it has grown in
> the years it has lain here into the shape of a door. The Guide steps out of the trunk the way
> you step out of shade. 'You are expected,' it says, and it is not a threat; it is the kindest
> thing anyone has said to you in a hundred miles. 'The forest gave the world its first spring.
> It has been very patient about the price.' Behind it, far in among the pale trees, a child is
> singing, and you realise you have heard the tune before — in a dream, in a meadow, in a bell
> under the river. It has always been the same song. It has always been counting down to here."

## Interconnections (how A touches B, C, D, E, F)

- **→ B (Glovewright).** The Quiet Hand is trying to buy the party *out* of the Tithe for her
  own ends — every cache, every paid toll, every bought draught of water is her steering them
  onto the route so they can carry *her* debt across at A6. Her **glove** is the same object
  the Collector wears (Arc E), and the Collector's ledger is the Warden's ledger — so the
  Glovewright and the forest's accountant are dealing in the same market, and she knows it. If
  the party honour her debt (Arc B), it *lands inside A6's bargain* as extra weight on their
  side of the scale.
- **→ C (Cadoc).** The Gilded Company thinks the Tithe is treasure. Cadoc's whole arc is the
  refutation of A's thesis: the Company *takes*, and the forest is the one place taking is
  fatal. An allied Company at A6 lets the party *demand* a bargain (force-ending); a dead or
  spurned Cadoc subtracts that option and may put the Company's banners on the *other* side.
- **→ D (Shared Dream).** The plague is the Tithe leaking upstream through the water; curing a
  source (Arc D) literally removes a *tendril* — the forest reaches one biome less far, and the
  Warden's tally credits it. The same dream the party catch at A1 is the dream the sick describe.
- **→ E (Ledger / Red Star).** The Warden and the Collector are one office, two faces, one
  bark-ledger (connectivity thread 3). The Red Star burning over A6 is the sky-level reading of
  the Tithe. Arguing the Collector's terms cleverly (Arc E) shaves the *same debt* the Warden
  reads at A6.
- **→ F (One Who Follows).** The "one extra" the bell counts (A2) and the Warden tallies (A4)
  *is* the follower. Arc A is the impersonal Tithe; Arc F is its personal face — the specific
  someone the party owe, come to be paid at the threshold.

## Payoffs & consequences

- **Gave gladly (high tally).** The forest opens like a held breath let go. The party walk out
  "neither aged a day," as Wrenna does **when she walks out with her girl restored** — the
  bright ending, the one the whole bible is rooting for. The country behind them *eases*: the
  Spreading recedes (Arc D), the Red Star dims (Arc E), the One Who Follows steps into the
  firelight as a *friend* come to stand with them (Arc F). NPCs they were generous to prosper;
  the biomes they healed stay healed.
- **Bargained by force (armed at the threshold).** With the Company (Arc C) or a flock (Arc E)
  at their back, the party *negotiate* rather than supplicate. They get terms — but the forest
  does not forget being strong-armed, and the price is paid in *coin the party choose* rather
  than coin the forest chooses. A harder, colder peace. Cadoc, if he stood with them, may pay
  the bargain-by-force ending's blood-price as a tragic-heroic sacrifice (Arc C payoff).
- **Grudged or refused (low tally).** The forest takes the price anyway, **doubled, from
  someone the party loves** — an ally, a runner child, a cured villager, Wrenna herself. The
  campaign's epilogue is the **forest's edge hung with the party's tokens, swaying in no wind**,
  and Wrenna among them — a child's shoe on a cord, a swaying shoe, the barometer fallen. The
  country sours: merchants raise prices, gates bar, the Spreading advances, and the follower
  (Arc F) becomes the wronged dead come to collect *with* the forest, not for it.

# Arc B — The Quiet Hand and the Open Palm

## Premise & themes

The benefactor who has been smoothing the party's road and the fey reaching for them are the
same impulse wearing two gloves. The **Glovewright** is a mortal broker — the face of the
in-code Quiet Hand — and she has spent a fortune the party never asked her to spend: a cache
left at a hungry crossing, a bridge mended just before they reached it, the King of the Road's
tenth quietly paid in their name, a draught of impossible desert water bought on their behalf
from the Thirst-King's emissary. A friend goes ahead of them, and the friend has a motive. A
generation ago the Glovewright made her own bargain with the forest and never delivered on it
— she promised a child she could not, in the end, give up — and the debt has compounded for
twenty years. She cannot pay it alone anymore. So she has been *grooming a substitute*: easing
the party onto the collection route (Arc A) until they owe enough that the forest will accept
*them*, or someone they love, in place of the child she kept.

The theme is **the price of unasked kindness**. Every gift the Glovewright gives is real, and
every gift is a *claim* — the same law the fey live by (a gift accepted is a claim; the
Glovewright learned the rules from the creditor she's hiding from). The party feel one
benefactor's reach across a third of the map before they ever see her face, and it feels like
luck. It is a leash. The arc's gut-punch is that she is not a villain: she is a woman who has
been *holding a line for twenty years* — her wards are the only thing slowing the Spreading
(Arc D) — and she is so tired, and the thing she will ask is so reasonable in her own grief,
that refusing her costs the party something real. The Glovewright is the human cost of Arc A's
arithmetic: someone already inside the doubled price, trying not to be the one who pays it.

## The spine (beat by beat)

### B1 — A Friend Goes Ahead of You
- **Where.** *Temperate / road.* A lean stretch past Greywether (shared geography with A1) —
  a washed-out ford with, impossibly, a fresh-laid plank bridge, and beside it a cache: dry
  wood, hard bread, a stoppered flask, no note but a chalk mark.
- **Who.** A **runner child** — barefoot, quick, wearing a single leather **glove a size too
  big**, the Glovewright's mark. The child hands a folded scrap and bolts.
- **What they do.** Find the cache exactly where they needed one. The scrap reads, in a fine
  hand: *"A friend goes ahead of you. Mind the third milestone."* (The third milestone is the
  King of the Road's — see B2.)
- **The choice.** Take the cache freely and move on; refuse it; or chase the child. **Take** →
  the road keeps being kind, and the kindness keeps compounding (silently raising what they
  owe her). **Refuse / leave payment** → a rare early *out*; the Glovewright notes a party that
  won't be bought, and recalibrates. **Chase the child** → it knows the country too well to
  catch, but drops, deliberately, a second glove — child-sized, half-finished — the first hint
  she *makes* these, and gives them to children who can't refuse the work.
- **Downstream.** The party are now "a friend's project." Every convenience for the next two
  legs is hers.

### B2 — The Tenth Paid in Your Name
- **Where.** *Road into desert.* The third milestone — a toll-stone where the **King of the
  Road** takes his tenth (Feature arc; connectivity thread 7) — and, a leg on, a desert well
  where the **Thirst-King's veiled Emissary** trades sweet water for memory.
- **Who.** The **King of the Road**, unseen, his toll already *settled* — the milestone-keeper
  waves the party through: "Your tenth's been paid. The lady in the grey gloves." Then the
  **Emissary**, who offers the party water already bought: "A draught is reserved against your
  thirst. The account is open."
- **What they do.** Discover their road has been *pre-paid* — the toll that loops travellers
  forever if cheated (thread 7) is, for them, quietly covered; the desert water that costs a
  name is, for them, already purchased on credit. They benefit. The cost is deferred, and it is
  *theirs* — a debt of memory incurred in their name without consent.
- **The choice.** Drink the reserved water (gaining the desert leg, losing a memory the
  Emissary "promises you will not miss" — but the party choose nothing about *which*); decline
  and pay the Emissary themselves in honest coin/riddle (Arc E crossover — his terms can be
  argued); or refuse water entirely and brave the thirst-hazard. Accepting the Glovewright's
  pre-payment is the moment the party's *memories* go onto a ledger they didn't open.
- **Downstream.** The Emissary "keeps a tally of yours" — and that tally, the party will learn,
  is *fungible with the Collector's* (Arc E) and the Warden's (Arc A). The Glovewright has been
  paying the party's debts into the *same account the forest collects from*.

### B3 — The Colder Note
- **Where.** *Any* — wherever the party next make camp after the desert leg.
- **Who.** A runner child again — older this time, or a different child, same too-big glove —
  with a wax-sealed note. **The seal is a glove**, pressed in wax, and the party have *seen it
  before*: on a corpse two biomes back, or on a king's herald, or — chillingly — under the
  Collector's arm (Arc E).
- **What they do.** Read the warmth go out of the correspondence: *"I have spent much on you.
  Soon I will ask one thing, and you will want to say yes. I am sorry it must be you. There was
  no one else left who owed enough."*
- **The choice.** Seek her out now (she is hard to find before she wants to be — but a party who
  pushes can reach B4 early); send a reply (she answers, honestly, which is worse); or wait. A
  party that *investigates the glove* here can connect it to the Collector and start to guess the
  shape of the trade before B5 lands.
- **Downstream.** The glove is confirmed as the bible's recurring object (connectivity thread 2):
  the same mark on the broker and the Collector, hinting the two of them trade in the same debts.
  Cadoc (Arc C), if the party know him, recognises the seal — he owes her too — and his reaction
  tells them she is real, powerful, and resented.

### B4 — The Failing Ward
- **Where.** *Tainted.* The Spreading's edge — a line of dead grass advancing toward (or out
  from) a seed, ringed by old salt-and-iron **wards** that are visibly *failing*, one post
  guttering at a time.
- **Who.** The **Glovewright herself**, in person at last — older than her notes sounded, grey
  to the wrists in the gloves she's named for, holding a line she has held for twenty years.
  Possibly **Sister Maready** (Arc D) is here too, and they know each other.
- **What they do.** Find their invisible benefactor *exhausted and bleeding favour* — every
  ward she maintains costs her, and she has been paying for two decades. She is the only reason
  the Spreading (Arc D) hasn't reached the river-villages. She does not hide anymore. *"You see
  what I've been doing with the time you bought me. Now you see why I bought it."*
- **The choice.** Help her shore the ward (a credit on Arc A's glad-tally, and her undying
  gratitude); demand answers first; or walk away and let her hold it alone. A party that *helps*
  her here even before knowing the ask earns a softer version of B5 — she may offer them an *out*
  she'd never have offered a party that bargained.
- **Downstream.** Her wards are now explicitly the gate on the Spreading (connectivity thread:
  her wards slow Arc D). If she falls (B6 refuse/kill), the Spreading worsens *here*, visibly,
  and the river-villages downstream start dying.

### B5 — The One Thing She'll Ask
- **Where.** *Forest threshold* (or the last leg before it), private — she draws the party
  aside from whatever else is happening at the edge (this beat dovetails into Arc A's A5/A6).
- **Who.** The **Glovewright**, and — if the party have met him — the **Collector**, who holds
  *her* overdue note and has come, courteous as ever, to watch the account settle one way or the
  other (Arc E crossover).
- **What they do.** Hear the ask, finally, plainly. Twenty years ago she promised the forest a
  child and kept the child instead. The debt is now hers and *compounding past what one life can
  pay*. She asks the party to **carry her debt across the boundary and offer the fey a
  substitute** — themselves, or someone they love — in place of the child she never delivered.
  She is not commanding. She is begging, with a fortune of spent kindness behind the plea.
- **The choice — the arc's hinge.**
  - **Honour the debt (pay her back).** Shoulder the fey claim. It lands *inside Arc A's
    bargain* at A6 as extra weight — the party walk into the forest already owing more, raising
    the Tithe's stakes, but earning the Glovewright as a powerful, *grateful* ally on the far
    side.
  - **Refuse.** Decline to be her substitute. She does not rage — she *withdraws*, and the
    party learn "what a smoothed road becomes when it turns against you": every prior kindness
    reverses. The mended bridge (B1) fails *behind* them; the patrol her coin turned aside turns
    *back*; the toll she paid (B2) is "found unpaid" and the milestones begin to loop.
  - **Expose her.** Reveal her to those she manipulated — Cadoc (Arc C), the villages whose
    patrols she diverted into danger to clear the party's road — and she *falls*. Her wards lapse.
  - **Kill her.** The unpaid fey debt does not vanish. It **transfers to her nearest creditor:
    the party.** They inherit, in full, the thing she spent twenty years not-paying.
- **Downstream.** Whichever branch, the Glovewright's fate ripples straight into A6.

### B6 — Settlement
- **Where.** Folded into Arc A's threshold (A6) — the Glovewright's resolution is *part of* the
  Tithe's naming, not a separate scene.
- **Who.** Glovewright, Warden/Collector, the party, and whoever the substitute turns out to be.
- **What they do.** The account closes. See Payoffs.

## Read-aloud moments

> **B1 (the cache).** "The ford is washed out — and across it, where there was no bridge on any
> map, lies a plank walkway, the cut ends still pale and weeping sap. Beside it, set on a flat
> stone out of the wet: dry kindling, a loaf wrapped in waxed cloth, a stoppered flask. No name.
> Only a chalk mark on the stone — the outline of an open hand, fingers spread, as if someone
> pressed a glove there and lifted it away."

> **B3 (the seal).** "She breaks the wax to read it, and stops. The seal is not a crest. It is a
> *glove* — five fingers and a palm, pressed deep into the red wax, every stitch and seam come
> through clear. And you have seen it before. On the back of a dead man's hand at a crossing two
> weeks gone. Or under the arm of a pale clerk in funeral black, who told you he was only
> confirming the route."

> **B5 (the ask).** "She pulls the gloves off — the first time you have seen her hands. They are
> a young woman's hands on an old woman's arms; the years went somewhere else. 'I promised them a
> child,' she says. 'And when the time came I could not give her up, so I gave them everything
> *but* her, for twenty years, and it was never enough, because grudging payment costs double and
> I have been grudging it her whole life.' She folds the empty gloves and lays them in your palm.
> 'I bought your road so you would owe enough to stand in for me. I am sorry. There was no one
> left who owed as much as you do now.'"

## Interconnections (how B touches A, C, D, E, F)

- **→ A (Tithe).** B *is* the Quiet Hand thread given a face and a motive that plugs straight into
  the Tithe. The Glovewright has been steering the party onto Arc A's collection route on purpose;
  her ask (B5) resolves *inside* A6's bargain. She is the human being already standing in the
  doubled price.
- **→ C (Cadoc).** Cadoc owes the Glovewright too, and resents it (he hates that his legend was
  bought). The party can **turn him against her** (a lever to expose her, B5) or **rally him to
  her** (if they want her saved). Cadoc's recognition of her seal (B3) corroborates her power.
- **→ D (Shared Dream).** Her wards (B4) are the only thing slowing the Spreading; if she falls,
  the Spreading and the river-plague both worsen. Sister Maready may be at the ward with her.
- **→ E (Ledger / Red Star).** Her **glove** appears on the Collector (the recurring object,
  thread 2), and the Collector holds her overdue note — so **exposing one threatens the other**.
  The water she buys (B2) is paid into the Emissary's tally, which is the same market as the
  Collector's and the Warden's. Argue the Collector down (Arc E) and you may ease *her* note too.
- **→ F (One Who Follows).** If the party let the **Glovewright's runner child** go unpaid — used
  and discarded — that child is a strong default candidate for the follower (Arc F): "paid at last
  in the only coin left."

## Payoffs & consequences

- **Honour the debt.** The party shoulder her fey claim and carry it to A6. They walk into the
  forest already owing — Arc A's stakes spike — but the **Glovewright survives, grateful, and
  becomes a powerful ally on the far side**: her network, her wards, her decades of fey-dealing
  expertise, now in the party's corner. The Spreading stays held. Her runner children are freed
  and paid.
- **Refuse.** She withdraws her hand, and the smoothed road sours behind the party: the mended
  bridge fails, the turned-aside patrol returns hostile, the King of the Road's toll "comes due
  unpaid" and the milestones loop (thread 7 weaponised against them). She does not die — she
  *survives as a cold, capable enemy* who knows the country's every favour and how to revoke it.
- **Expose her.** She falls. Her protections lapse all at once: **the Spreading advances** into
  the river-villages (Arc D worsens), **the King of the Road turns on the party** (thread 7), and
  the network of kindnesses that was carrying half the map collapses. The party are *right* about
  her — and the country pays for their being right. Cadoc, if he was the lever, is freed of his
  debt to her and may become an ally (Arc C).
- **Kill her.** Cleanest in the moment, worst in the ledger. **Her unpaid fey debt transfers to
  the party in full** — they inherit twenty years of compounding, and arrive at A6 owing what she
  owed, with no one left to share it. The forest is, if anything, *pleased*: the account simplified.

## Hooks to seed (random-event table feed)

- A child on the road wears a leather glove three sizes too big and won't say who gave it to her,
  only that "the lady pays in work, and you can't say no to work." `(narrative)`
- The milestone-keeper at the third stone waves you through: "Your tenth's settled. The lady in
  the grey gloves saw to it. Generous, her. Generous folk always want something." `(narrative)`
- A dying man at a desert well keeps a wax seal in his fist — a glove pressed in red — and rasps,

- Along the Spreading's edge, salt-and-iron ward-posts stand in a failing ring, re-set so many
  times the ground is white with old salt. Someone has been holding this line for *years*. `(narrative)`
- A Gilded Company outrider spits at the name "Glovewright": "Sir Cadoc'd cut his own debt to her
  out with a knife if he could find the vein. We all would." `(narrative)`
- The pale clerk in funeral black is asked who's overdue on his ledger and answers, mildly, "A

# Arc C — Glory and the Gilded Company

## Premise & themes

**Sir Cadoc Vane** and his Gilded Company treat the entire pilgrimage as a trophy hunt. They
race the party for every bounty, buy first read of every board, take the best kills and the
loudest names — a glittering, well-geared war-band selling a legend, with Cadoc's smirk on the
front of it. He is the party's recurring rival: the man who always got there first. The arc is
the long, expensive education of Cadoc Vane, who learns — the hard way, with his company dying
around him — that the forest is **not a dungeon to be looted**. Something he chases finally
chases back, and the legend he sells collides with the survival of his thinning band. By the
boreal wall, the smirk is gone and the proud knight is asking the party for help he never
thought he'd need. His sister, **Quartermaster Ilse Vane** — the Company's real brain — has seen
this coming for a hundred miles and will trade everything the Company has to get her brother out
*alive* before his pride finishes them all.

The theme is **taking, in a country whose only law is give**. Cadoc is the perfect foil to Arc
A's thesis. The Company *takes* — the trophy, the bounty, the god-hoard — and the forest is the
one place taking is fatal. Every leg, Cadoc's greed extracts a price the party watch him not-pay,
until the bill comes due all at once at a volcanic forge with his men screaming. But the arc is
not a morality lecture with a punchline; it's a tragedy with an exit. Cadoc is *brave*, genuinely,
and the legend he sells has kept frightened villages fed on hope for years. Ilse loves him and is
clear-eyed about exactly how he will die. The party hold the lever: make of Cadoc an **ally, a
debt, or a corpse** — and the Company is the campaign's variable army, the banners that decide
whether the party can *demand* a bargain at the threshold (Arc A) or must beg one.

## The spine (beat by beat)

### C1 — Leave Some Glory For the Rest of Us
- **Where.** *Temperate / road.* A bounty-board outside a market town — the same starter country
  as A1/B1, so all three arcs open in one region.
- **Who.** **Sir Cadoc Vane**, overtaking the party at a canter, gilded and grinning. The local
  posting-clerk, apologetic. Possibly a first glimpse of **Ilse**, already counting their coin.
- **What they do.** Reach the board and find every worthwhile bounty pre-claimed, stamped *the
  Gilded Company*. A local explains: "They pay for first read. Pay the clerk, see the board a day
  before anyone else." Cadoc tips an imaginary hat: *"Leave some glory for the rest of us."*
- **The choice.** Outbid them for first read (an arms race that drains coin); race them to a
  contested bounty on foot/wits; or let them have it and take the scraps. How the party play this
  *first* meeting sets Cadoc's read of them — rival to respect, prey to mock, or beneath notice.
- **Downstream.** Establishes the Company as the always-first rival (connectivity thread 4) and
  plants Ilse as the one doing the actual thinking.

### C2 — The Wrong-Beast's Head
- **Where.** *Savanna.* The Great Hunt's gathering-ground — many peoples under truce, grass moving
  in a wind you can't feel — and at its edge, the **Bone-Singer's** camp.
- **Who.** Both bands, drawn to the same prize: a **wrong-beast** the Great Hunt has gathered to
  take (regional savanna arc), worth a *name*. The **Bone-Singer**, who buys what you killed and
  sells you its courage — and who weighs Cadoc's ambition with a cold ear.
- **What they do.** Compete (or cooperate) for the wrong-beast. Cadoc wants its head and the name
  it carries — he'll take the kill *and the cost*, the Bone-Singer notes, "and never feel the
  second one until it's due." The party can take the beast themselves, let Cadoc have it, or hunt
  it *together* and split the legend.
- **The choice.** **Let Cadoc have the kill** → he takes the glory *and* whatever the wrong-beast's
  death costs the taker (the Bone-Singer's quiet warning pays off at C3). **Take it yourselves** →
  the party shoulder the cost instead, and the name; Cadoc's resentment sharpens. **Together** → a
  rare alliance this early; the Bone-Singer marks the party as people who *share*, which she finds
  more interesting than people who win.
- **Downstream.** The Bone-Singer's reading of Cadoc — ambition unweighed, a man who takes the
  kill and not the price — is the arc's diagnosis. The wrong-beast's "cost," if Cadoc took it,
  begins quietly draining the Company before C3.

### C3 — Whatever He Chased, Chased Back
- **Where.** *Volcanic.* The **Cooling Forge** — ground that ticks with heat, the smith-god's anvil
  ringing somewhere ahead, a god-hoard guarded by something old (regional volcanic arc).
- **Who.** Not Cadoc — *the aftermath* of Cadoc. The party arrive to a struck-haste camp: a dropped
  sword, a fire still smoking, a name **panic-carved** into a tent-pole. The **Last Priest of the
  Forge**, half turned to stone, watching the flame gutter.
- **What they do.** Read the disaster. Cadoc looted the Cooling Forge's god-hoard and **woke what
  guards it.** Whatever he chased through three biomes chased *back*, and the gilded, grinning
  Company is suddenly bleeding and scattered. The party can track the survivors, take what Cadoc
  dropped (the same greed that doomed him, offered to them now), or help the Last Priest re-light
  the forge before the smith-god "forges nothing."
- **The choice.** **Loot Cadoc's loot** → the party become, for a beat, the thing the arc is
  warning against (and the guardian may turn its attention to *them*). **Help the Priest** → a
  glad-act tally credit (Arc A) and the forge stays lit, which matters regionally. **Chase the
  survivors** → leads straight to C4.
- **Downstream.** The Company is *broken* here. This is the inciting "someone got here first" of the
  volcanic regional arc (connectivity thread 4) — and the "first" was Cadoc, and it cost him.

### C4 — The Smirk Gone
- **Where.** *Boreal.* The **Wolf-Winter's wall** — a place where the pack runs and the cold has a
  glassy creak, and a thing waits ahead "that doesn't care how fine your gear is."
- **Who.** **Cadoc Vane, alone** — or nearly: his company thinned to a handful, his gilding
  scratched, the grin entirely gone. He has lost men at the Forge and more on the road since.
- **What they do.** Find their rival *humbled and afraid*, holding at the Wolf-Winter's wall
  because he dares not go forward and cannot, in pride, go back. He says the hardest sentence of his
  life: *"There's a thing up ahead that doesn't care how fine your gear is. I'd take help. I never
  thought I'd say it."*
- **The choice.** **Help him** (fight through the Wolf-Winter beside him → begins his redemption,
  earns the Company's loyalty); **bargain** (name a price for help — a debt Cadoc will owe, which
  changes the threshold math); **refuse / mock** (the man who said "leave some glory for the rest of
  us" eats those words, and remembers who fed them to him). How the party treated him at C1 colours
  what he expects here.
- **Downstream.** This is the pivot from rival to *ally, debtor, or enemy*. A party that helps him
  freely banks an enormous Arc A credit (generosity to the man who embodies *taking*).

### C5 — Ilse's Bargain
- **Where.** *Boreal / threshold.* The last camp before the forest, where the Company — what's left
  of it — must decide whether to follow Cadoc one more step.
- **Who.** **Quartermaster Ilse Vane**, more clear-eyed than her brother and done watching him die
  slowly. She draws the party aside.
- **What they do.** Hear Ilse's offer: the Company's **maps and muscle for the forest** — a war-band
  at the threshold, exactly what Arc A's force-ending needs — **IF** the party help her get **Cadoc
  out alive** instead of letting his pride finish them all. She does not want him to die a legend;
  she wants him to live a man. She knows about his debt to the Glovewright (Arc B) and will trade
  that intel too.
- **The choice — the arc's hinge.**
  - **Take the deal (save Cadoc).** Help Ilse extract her brother — talk him down, share the
    threshold's burden, refuse to let him charge. The **Company allies with the party for A6**,
    banners and all, and Cadoc lives to make a *choice* at the finale rather than a corpse.
  - **Refuse / let Cadoc's pride run.** He charges the forest as a legend and **dies** (or is
    taken). Ilse becomes either a **grateful new ally** (if the party at least *tried*, and it was
    his pride that killed him, not their neglect) or a **vengeful enemy** who **blames the party for
    not saving him** and turns the Company's remnant against them.
  - **Make a corpse of Cadoc yourselves.** If the rivalry curdled to blood, the party can end him —
    and inherit Ilse's enmity, and lose the Company army for A6 entirely.
- **Downstream.** The Company's banners (or their absence) at A6 decide whether the party *demand*
  or *beg* the bargain. Ilse's allegiance ripples through the finale.

### C6 — The Banners At the Threshold
- **Where.** Folded into Arc A's A6 — the Company's resolution is *part of* the Tithe's naming.
- **Who.** Cadoc (allied, dead, or enemy), Ilse, the party, the Warden.
- **What they do.** The Company's strength is spent for the party, against them, or not at all. See
  Payoffs.

## Read-aloud moments

> **C1 (the overtaking).** "Hooves behind you, and then they're past — six riders in gilt-chased
> harness that's a little too clean for this road, pennants snapping, and at their head a knight
> who reins in just long enough to look you over and find you wanting. He touches two fingers to a
> helmet he isn't wearing. 'Leave some glory for the rest of us,' he says, and he's already
> spurring on, and the bounty-board ahead already has his company's stamp on every notice worth
> reading."

> **C3 (the aftermath at the Forge).** "The camp was struck in a hurry — a tent half-collapsed, a
> cookfire still ticking, a good sword dropped in the ash and left. On the centre pole someone has
> cut a word with a knife, fast and deep and crooked: a name. Below it the rock is scored with five
> long grooves, as if something with a hand the size of a door took hold to pull itself up out of
> the mountain. The half-stone priest does not turn his head. 'He took what the smith-god kept,' he
> says, and his voice is going to gravel. 'And the smith-god woke to keep it.'"

> **C4 (the smirk gone).** "You almost don't know him. The gilt is scratched to the steel, there's
> frost in three days' beard, and the men at his back you could count on one hand. He sees you, and
> the smirk that's been on his face since the first milestone is simply *gone*, like a thing he
> pawned. 'There's a thing up ahead,' he says, 'that doesn't care how fine your gear is.' He
> swallows. 'I'd take help. I never thought I'd say it. I'm saying it.'"

## Interconnections (how C touches A, B, D, E, F)

- **→ A (Tithe).** Cadoc is the **foil to Arc A's whole thesis**: the Company *takes*, and the
  forest punishes taking. His looting (C3) is what the forest does to a grudger, dramatised early so
  the party watch it happen to someone else before it can happen to them. An **allied Company at A6
  is the force-ending's army** — the banners that let the party *demand* a bargain. Cadoc redeemed
  is a **tragic-heroic sacrifice** option in the finale; Cadoc spurned **defects to whoever opposes
  the party** (the Scavenger-Prophet, the Red Star's flock — Arc E — even the fey).
- **→ B (Glovewright).** **Cadoc owes the Glovewright** and resents it bitterly. He is the **lever
  that exposes or saves her** (Arc B, B5): turn him against her and he'll help bring her down; rally
  him and he'll stand at her ward. Ilse knows the shape of his debt and trades the intel (C5).
- **→ D (Shared Dream).** If the party let villages fall to the Spreading/plague to chase bounties
  *with* Cadoc's mindset, the soured country (Arc D's moral ledger) raises Cadoc's prices and bars
  his welcome too — the Company feels the rot they helped ignore.
- **→ E (Ledger / Red Star).** A **spurned Cadoc defects to the Red Star's flock or the
  Scavenger-Prophet** (Arc E), arriving at the finale on the *other* army's side — a darker mirror
  of his allied-banners role.
- **→ F (One Who Follows).** **Cadoc, if spurned**, is a strong default candidate for the follower
  (Arc F): the rival the party left to die, come to the threshold to be present when *their* debt is
  paid.

## Payoffs & consequences

- **Allied Company (saved Cadoc, took Ilse's deal).** A **war-band at the forest's edge** — huge in
  Arc A's bargain-by-force ending. The party arrive able to *demand* terms. **Cadoc, redeemed,**
  becomes a man who can *choose* at the finale: stand down, stand with them, or offer himself as a
  **tragic-heroic sacrifice** to weight the bargain. The legend he sold becomes, at last, *true* —
  a knight who learned the country's law and paid it gladly. Ilse is a steadfast ally; the Company's
  maps open the threshold.
- **Dead/shamed Cadoc, Ilse grateful.** If the party tried and his *own pride* killed him, Ilse
  inherits the Company clear-eyed and **allies with the party out of grief and respect** — a
  competent, sober war-band minus its reckless head. The legend dies with Cadoc; Ilse builds
  something quieter and more durable in its place.
- **Dead/spurned Cadoc, Ilse vengeful.** If the party *let* him die — refused, mocked, or made a
  corpse of him — **Ilse blames them** and turns the Company's remnant against them. At A6 the
  banners are on the *wrong* side, or absent when needed; the force-ending is off the table. Worse,
  a **spurned-but-living Cadoc defects** to the prophets or the fey (Arc E), and the party meet their
  old rival at the threshold leading the opposition.

# PRIMUS — Side-Quest Arcs D, E, F (full treatments)

*The braided middle of the pilgrimage. Three arcs that look like three things — a plague, an
omen, a stalker — and turn out to be one debt read three ways. All three converge on the
Dreaming Forest threshold, where Arc A (the Tithe) calls everything home.*

**Canon precedence:** in-code `JOURNEY_THREADS` text > `PRIMUS-BIBLE.md` > this document >
improvisation. Where this file names a fey-face and a mortal-face of the same office (the
Tithe-Warden / the Collector), keep them ambiguous on purpose — the reveal is that they are one
account.

**The braid, stated once so all three arcs can lean on it:**
- **The water** carries the Dream (Arc D) downstream from the forest. Trace it up and it ends at the threshold.
- **The sky** shows the same debt as the Red Star (Arc E); the prophet is the human stampede the debt creates.
- **The ground** keeps the count: boot-prints, your number plus one (Arc F). The extra is the Tithe's interest, given a face.
- **The bark-bound ledger** is the shared object. The Tithe-Warden carries it in the deep wood (Arc A); the Collector carries it on the road (Arc E). Same pages. Same hand, some say.
- **The threshold** — pale ring of mushrooms, the arch of living fallen branch — is where all three arrive at once. Whoever the party brought (a cured Maready, a redeemed Cadoc, a settled flock) or failed to (a Follower owed a death) stands there with them.

# Arc D — The Shared Dream

*the plague that isn't*

## Premise & themes

A sickness runs ahead of the party up every road, and everyone it touches dreams the same dream
of the same far place. There is no contagion to find, no humour to balance, no bad air to flee —
the fever moves on **water**, downstream, and its victims wake describing a forest none of them
have seen. It is not a plague. It is the Tithe of the Forest (Arc A) leaking backward through the
watercourses, the forest's hunger reaching down the rivers to taste who is coming. The cruel joke
the arc turns on: **the cure is upstream, at the source, which is the forest** — so to end the
Dream the party must carry medicine *toward* the very thing causing it, and every mile closer to a
cure is a mile deeper into the cause. This is the campaign's moral ledger for *the people you will
never meet* — the downstream villages whose lives turn on a choice made days away, by strangers,
about a well they'll never see. Its tone is folk-horror domestic: shuttered doors and white
crosses, a healer out of everything, a child breathing in time with a tune nobody hummed. The
horror is not the dying. It is how *peaceful* the dying look, and that they are all dreaming
somewhere lovelier than here.

## The spine

### 1. The white-crossed doors
- **WHERE.** *Temperate*, river-feature — **Ashford-on-Wend**, a milling village where the road first runs alongside the water.
- **WHO.** The miller's wife (door-barring, plain-spoken); a feverish child at an upper window.
- **DOES.** The party arrive to find half the doors chalked with white crosses and a hand-lettered board at the ford: *DON'T DRINK DOWNSTREAM OF US.* No one will let them in. The miller's wife will talk through a shutter: it came up the road three days back, and "it's going your way too — it always goes the way the water doesn't."
- **CHOICE.** Press in and risk it (and learn the sick aren't suffering — they're *smiling*), or honour the quarantine and move on with only the rumour. **Branch:** forcing entry to help earns the village's trust later (they remember a kindness) but exposes the party to the first shared dream that night; respecting the line keeps them clean but blind one beat longer.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** That night — whether they entered or not, if they drank from the Wend — the party dream the dream: *a forest with no horizon, a tune, a sense of being expected.* This is the same dream a plague-village two legs on will describe word for word. The Tithe has tasted them.

### 2. A day behind the fever
- **WHERE.** *Temperate*, river-inn — **The Drowned Lantern**, a coaching inn one day downstream.
- **WHO.** **Sister Maready** (Open Order field-healer, out of everything, still working); the **Apothecary** stocking the inn's back room; the **Plague-Doctor Pedlar** if the party need a darker option.
- **DOES.** The party catch the fever's wake. Maready is treating a common room of the sleeping-smilers with nothing but well-water and exhaustion. She's the first to say it plainly: *they all dream the same place, and lately so does she.* Herbalist's simples, an apothecary's tinctures, clean upstream water — these are worth more than gold here, and the party can spend or hoard them. The Pedlar offers a faster "cure" that is really a curse (it stops the dream by stopping the dreamer).
- **CHOICE.** Resource the healer (give freely from packs / buy out the apothecary on the village's behalf), profiteer (sell your simples at fever-prices), or take the Pedlar's shortcut. **Branch:** generosity here is *literally banked* — see Arc A's Tithe-Warden, who tallies it. Profiteering sours the country (later merchants price-gouge the party in turn). The Pedlar's cure plants a seed of Arc F (a person you "cured" to death follows you).
- **DOWNSTREAM.** Maready, if helped, becomes a recurring ally — she'll reappear, and her fate at the forest's edge (cured, or claimed) becomes a finale beat. If the party showed her only their backs, she keeps walking the road anyway, losing, and the next time they meet her she's further into the dream herself.

### 3. The tell — one dream, many mouths
- **WHERE.** *Any* biome the party are passing through when this lands — the arc is patient. Best at a roadside camp or a second afflicted hamlet.
- **WHO.** Two or three of the sick, lucid for a moment; **Wrenna** if she is travelling near (her grief is the dream's purest carrier — she has dreamed it for seven years).
- **DOES.** The party compare what the sick say and realise it is not *like* a shared dream — it is *the same* dream, down to the tune and the order of the trees. If Wrenna is present, she finishes the sick people's sentences before they speak them, calmly, like reciting a route she's walked. She says the line that reframes the whole arc: *"It isn't a sickness. It's a homesickness. They just don't know whose."*
- **CHOICE.** Treat it as illness (and keep failing) or treat it as a *message* and start tracing the water upstream. **Branch:** the party who pivot to "follow the water" advance the arc toward its source; the party who keep looking for a contagion stall, and more villages fall behind them.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** From here the arc has a direction: upstream, against the current, toward worse and toward the cure at once. The fey-silver arrow (Arc A, the Hunt) — if the party carry it — and the river both point the same way: home.

### 4. The Spreading — the dream made into land
- **WHERE.** *Tainted* — **the Spreading**, where a line of grey, dead grass advances across the country a hand's-width a day, fanning out from (or in toward) a single point.
- **WHO.** The **Glovewright** (the Quiet Hand, Arc B) in person — older than her notes sounded, holding a failing line of wards she set years ago; her runner child.
- **DOES.** The party reach the place where the Dream has stopped being a fever and become geography — the wrongness is in the soil now, in flies that move in watching patterns, in shadows a beat slow. The Glovewright is here, paying visibly for something, keeping the Spreading from reaching the river-villages downstream. She lets slip more than she means to. A journal is found — hers, or a predecessor's: *"It is not a sickness. It is a seed. And someone planted it."*
- **CHOICE.** Help the Glovewright hold the line (and incur a debt to her — Arc B's hook lands here), demand answers and threaten to expose her wards as the cause, or push past her toward the seed against her warning. **Branch:** aiding her opens Arc B in earnest (she will *ask one thing* later); confronting her cracks her composure and reveals the Tithe connection early; pushing past loses her as an ally but gets the party to the source faster and angrier.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The Spreading is the hinge: from here the party know it is *planted*, *aimed*, and *connected to a benefactor they thought was kind*. The Glovewright's involvement ties this arc permanently to Arc B and, through her overdue note, to the Collector of Arc E.

### 5. The source in the dead town
- **WHERE.** *Wasteland* — **Hollow Mereck**, a town already entirely dead of the Dream, downstream of nothing and upstream of everything that's now sick. A fouled well at its heart.
- **WHO.** The **Scavenger-Prophet** and his **immune flock**, picking the dead town clean and unafraid of the water (their immunity is a horror-reveal, not a mercy — see Interconnections); the thing *in* the well.
- **DOES.** The party trace the water to its poisoned head: a well, a buried relic, a drowned thing — a tendril of the forest pushed up through the earth into the watershed, *seeding* the Dream into the country's veins. The Scavenger-Prophet's people are immune because they've already given the relic what it wants (they've stopped dreaming because they've stopped *wanting* — they are hollowed, content, wrong). The source can be **closed** (capped, salted, the relic drawn and destroyed), **left and carried** (a cutting taken, knowingly), or **harvested** (the Spreading's standing offer — take a piece of the seed and become its gardener).
- **CHOICE — the arc's keystone.** Three branches, each a different ending to Arc D *and* a different opening to Arc A:
  - **Close it.** End the Dream. Every downstream village stops dying. Arc A's Tithe *loses a tendril* — the forest reaches one biome less far, and the Tithe-Warden's ledger notes the party paid down a debt instead of incurring one.
  - **Carry it.** Take the seed onward knowingly. The party become the **Tithe's courier** — the dark Arc A ending, where they personally deliver the forest's hunger to the threshold, and arrive as its instrument rather than its debtor.
  - **Harvest it.** Take a cutting. A slow promise. It grows in the party's pack, in their dreams, and roots properly only at the forest itself — a living thread pulling them in, sweet and patient.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** Whatever they choose ripples to *every settlement on that watercourse*. Closing it is the single largest "give gladly" the party can bank before the threshold. Carrying or harvesting it bends Arc A's finale dark and seeds Arc F (the dead of the Dream get a face, and that face follows).

### 6. The frozen smile (optional northern branch / escalation)
- **WHERE.** *Frozen* and *tundra* — the **Herd-folk's fire** on the white flats, where the Dream takes its winter form.
- **WHO.** The **Reindeer-Herder** (shares his fire on one rule: *never step beyond it in the fog; it takes one, always one*); the smiling frozen sleepers half-buried in blue ice, breathing in slow time.
- **DOES.** If the party went north before they went to the source — or if they let the Dream spread unchecked — they find it has reached the cold, where it doesn't fever, it *freezes*: people stopped mid-smile, breathing in unison, kept alive and dreaming by the ice. What Walks in the Fog is the Dream wearing winter, and the fog *counts* the taken. The Herd-folk's "owe a death" custom is the Tithe in a colder dialect.
- **CHOICE.** Honour the fog's counting-rule to pass safely (Arc E's "the terms can be argued" logic applies — knowing the rule is half the win), or break it and lose one of your own to the count. **Branch:** respecting the rule earns the Herd-folk as allies and a clean crossing; breaking it adds a name to the Follower's tally (Arc F).
- **DOWNSTREAM.** This beat proves the Dream is *the same phenomenon everywhere* — fever in the south, freeze in the north, all of it the forest reaching downstream. It also hard-links Arc D to the Tithe's "owe a death" logic that Arc A pays out at the threshold.

### 7. The threshold — what the Dream was for
- **WHERE.** *Void* / forest threshold — the pale ring of mushrooms.
- **WHO.** The **Courteous Guide** (steps from a solid trunk); **Sister Maready** if she survived and followed (cured, or by now claimed and smiling); the **Tithe-Warden**, ledger open.
- **DOES.** At the boundary the Dream resolves: it was never an attack, it was an *invitation list*. Everyone who dreamed it was being *counted as coming*. The party learn whether they are on that list as guests (debt paid down) or as freight (the Tithe's courier). If they closed the source, Maready meets them here cured, on her feet, an ally at their back. If they carried or harvested it, the seed in their pack answers the forest, and something steps out to receive it.
- **CHOICE.** Hand back what they carried (refuse the courier role at the last, at a price), or complete the delivery. **Branch:** refusal at the threshold is a costly grace that ties straight into Arc A's bargain; completion makes the party the forest's chosen instrument and bends the finale toward the dark.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** Arc D's resolution is one of the largest single inputs to Arc A's final tally and to who the Follower (Arc F) turns out to be.

> wash away — so they've been redrawn, again and again, the same crosses thicker each day. At the
> ford a board has been nailed crooked to a post. The lettering is careful, a child's best hand:
> **DON'T DRINK DOWNSTREAM OF US.** Below it, smaller, as if added later by someone who'd stopped

> Sister Maready has not slept. You can see it in the way she moves between the cots — economical,

> she says, not looking up. "That's the part no one believes till they see it. Listen." She stills.
> In the dim of the common room, a dozen sleepers breathe — and they breathe *together*, in, and
> out, slow as a tide. "They're somewhere better than here. All of them. The *same* somewhere."
> She finally looks at you, and there's a question in her face she's afraid to ask out loud:
> *how would she know that, unless she'd started going there too.*

> You salt the well-mouth and the dead town does not thank you, because the dead town is past
> thanking. But far downstream, in a hundred kitchens you will never stand in, a fever breaks. A
> child opens her eyes to a low ceiling and a worried mother instead of a forest with no horizon,
> and is, for one furious confused moment, *disappointed* — before she remembers that she is home,
> and that home is where the people who love her are. She will never know your name. The ledger does.

## Interconnections

- **Arc A (the Tithe — the spine).** Arc D *is* the Tithe seen from downstream, at mortal scale — the same phenomenon, the same forest, the same hunger, met as a fever instead of a fae assessor. Closing the source pays down Arc A's debt; carrying it makes the party Arc A's courier; the "owe a death" fog-count is Arc A's logic in a winter dialect. The Tithe-Warden literally tallies what the party did here.
- **Arc B (the Quiet Hand).** The **Glovewright's** failing wards are the only thing slowing the Spreading — so Arc D is where the party first meet her in the flesh and first owe her. Her overdue note (held by the Collector) bridges to Arc E. Aid her here and she will *ask one thing* later; expose her wards as part of the seeding and Arc B turns adversarial.
- **Arc C (the Gilded Company).** Cadoc treats the dead town of Hollow Mereck as a salvage prize and the relic in the well as loot — a low-stakes echo of his Cooling-Forge mistake. He can blunder the source open at the wrong moment, forcing the party's hand, or be talked into helping cap it for a cut of nonexistent treasure.
- **Arc E (the Ledger and the Red Star).** The **Scavenger-Prophet's immune flock** at Hollow Mereck is the reveal that ties D to E: they're immune because they've already paid (been hollowed) — salvage-faith and the Dream are the same surrender. The Red Star burns over every white-crossed door; the prophet's terrified flock and the fever's victims are one frightened country.
- **Arc F (the One Who Follows).** If the party let a village fall, took the Pedlar's killing "cure," broke the fog-count, or carried the seed — **the person who died of it becomes the Follower.** Arc D is the single most common source of Arc F's antagonist. A cured Maready, by contrast, can be the Follower-as-friend.

## Payoffs & consequences

- **Closed the source (gave gladly).** The Dream recedes across the whole watercourse. Downstream villages live. Maready survives and stands with the party at the threshold. Arc A's forest reaches one biome less far; the Tithe-Warden's tally swings toward "guest." The country *remembers a mercy*: gates open, merchants deal fair, the road is kinder. This is the cleanest banked goodwill in the campaign.
- **Carried it knowingly (the courier).** The party deliver the forest's hunger themselves. Arc A's finale opens dark — they arrive as the Tithe's instrument, and the bargain at the threshold treats them as freight that walked itself in. The Follower (Arc F) is very likely one of the Dream's dead, come to be paid in front of the fey.
- **Harvested a cutting (the slow promise).** A living thread roots in the party's pack and dreams, pulling them toward the forest, sweet and inevitable. They arrive *wanting* to be there — which is its own kind of loss. The cutting blooms at the threshold and gives the forest a claim that lands in Arc A's bargain.
- **Let villages fall / profiteered / weaponised the Dream.** The country sours measurably: later merchants raise prices, settlements bar their gates, allies grow scarce. The dead accumulate into Arc F. Wrenna, if she watched the party choose coin over the sick, stops finishing their sentences — and stops meeting their eyes.

# Arc E — The Ledger and the Red Star

*the Collector, the Barefoot Prophet, the omen*

## Premise & themes

A red star has appeared low in the evening sky, and no two people read it the same: a wound, a
wedding-lamp, a coal, the eye of something turning to look. Beneath it, town to town, a barefoot
prophet preaches the end and gathers a flock — and on the same roads walks a pale clerk in funeral
black, the Collector, who is not preaching anything, only *confirming a route* and noting, in a
ledger bound in bark, a debt "incurred long ago, by blood or by bargain." The star, the prophet,
and the clerk are three readings of one event: the Dreaming Forest's bargain (Arc A) coming due on
the **whole country at once**, and someone — something — deciding who pays. The arc's engine is
**fear curdling toward a target**: a frightened country wants a face to blame or a hand to follow,
and the prophet offers both. Its quiet revelation is mechanical and chilling — the Collector's
ledger is *bark*, the same pages the fey Tithe-Warden carries in the deep wood (Arc A). The
forest's hunger and the mortal world's debts are **one account**, and the Collector is its
accountant in the world of the living. The arc's grace note, and its only real weapon, is folk-
horror's first rule: **the terms can be argued.** The fey always take, but a clerk keeps books,
and books can be read, contested, and — by the clever — amended. This is the one place mortal wit
beats the fae.

## The spine

### 1. The star rises; the clerk falls into step
- **WHERE.** *Temperate*, road — open country at dusk, a milestone, a thin column of travellers all glancing at the same point in the sky.
- **WHO.** The **Collector** (pale, funeral-black, a bark-bound ledger under one arm); ordinary travellers, each reading the star differently.
- **DOES.** The party notice the new red star — low, steady, *wrong* in a way the eye keeps returning to. Other travellers volunteer their readings, no two alike. As dusk deepens, a thin man in clerk's black is simply *there*, walking at their pace though no one saw him approach. He is courteous, incurious, terrible. He says: *"Not here for you. Not yet. Only confirming the route."* He notes something, closes the ledger, and is gone at the next bend.
- **CHOICE.** Engage the Collector (and learn nothing yet but his manner), ignore him, or try to look at the ledger. **Branch:** politeness gets a polite non-answer and a name remembered; aggression gets the first taste of "the interest the ledger charges"; a glimpse of the ledger shows *bark, written in a hand like root-hairs* — a seed of the Arc A reveal, dropped early.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The Collector is now a recurring presence — ungated, like the star — and the party know a debt is being *confirmed* against them or someone near them. The bark detail is planted for the void payoff.

### 2. The crossroads sermon
- **WHERE.** *Temperate*, road / a named crossroads — **Tern's Cross**, where four roads and a market meet.
- **WHO.** The **Barefoot Prophet** of the Red Star; in his crowd, *faces the party have seen before*, each a little nearer him than the last town.
- **DOES.** The party come on the Prophet preaching beneath the star — barefoot on the cold stones, plain, magnetic, naming the star as the sign that the world's long debt is finally due and that *someone* among the living invited it. He does not (yet) name who. The party recognise faces in the flock from earlier legs — a drover, an innkeeper's son, a grieving widow — standing closer to him each time, fear curdling toward devotion. He may single the party out, warmly or coldly, depending on what they've done.
- **CHOICE.** Argue him down publicly (a contest of words — expose the fear-merchant), feed the crowd's fear (use him), or pass quietly. **Branch:** a successful public rebuttal scatters a few from the flock and marks the party as the Prophet's rivals (dangerous later); stoking the crowd wins a frightening, useful momentum and bends Arc E toward "arrive with a mob"; passing keeps the party clean but lets the flock swell unopposed.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The Prophet's flock is now a tracked, growing force. Whether it grows toward *blame* (a witch-hunt, possibly aimed at the party) or *exodus* (a pilgrimage to the forest) depends on the party's handling here and in beat 4.

### 3. The same coin — memory, years, names
- **WHERE.** *Desert* (and/or *wasteland*) — a shaded caravanserai, the **Thirst-King's well**, a riddle-marked door.
- **WHO.** The **Thirst-King's veiled Emissary** (mummified herald, gracious, keeps a tally of names); people the **Collector** has already visited — *"a man who gave up his name, a woman who pays in years."*
- **DOES.** In the dry country the party meet the Collector's *clients* — those already on the ledger — and learn the terms in detail: the debt is paid in **memory, years, or names**, and crucially, *it can be argued.* The man who gave up his name negotiated to keep his children's; the woman paying in years bargained the *order* of them. In the same breath the party meet the Thirst-King's Emissary, who trades sweet water for "memories you will not miss" — the *exact same currency*. The reveal lands: the fey and the ledger deal in one market. The Emissary and the Collector may even nod to one another.
- **CHOICE.** Learn the negotiation rules and bank them for the threshold, spend their own memories/years for the Emissary's water (a real, deferred cost), or try to free a client from the ledger. **Branch:** studying the terms is the single most valuable thing the party can do for Arc A (it lets them *argue the Tithe* later); trading with the Emissary mortgages their own pages; "freeing" a client teaches that the ledger does not forgive a broken contract — it *charges interest* (a shadow that escalates toward Arc F).
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The party now *understand the ledger's rules* — the campaign's only lever against the fae bargain. This is the knowledge that, brought to the threshold, lets a clever party shave Arc A's debt.

### 4. The two prophets bleed together
- **WHERE.** *Wasteland* — the **Scavenger-Prophet's scrap-town** ("the Heirs"), where a salvaged relic is waking.
- **WHO.** The **Scavenger-Prophet** (salvage-faith); the **Barefoot Prophet's** flock, arrived here too; the immune/hollowed people of Arc D's Hollow Mereck nearby.
- **DOES.** The fearful tide finds its second prophet. The Scavenger-Prophet preaches that the old world died of *wanting* and that salvation is taking only what the dead no longer need — and one relic he's enshrined is *waking* under his feet (the regional wasteland arc's inciting trouble). The Red Star's flock and the salvage-flock mix into one panicked mass with two heads, looking for someone to **follow** or someone to **blame**. The waking relic is, the party may realise, kin to the seed in Arc D's well — another tendril of the forest pushed into the world.
- **CHOICE.** Unite the prophets (terrifyingly effective — one mob, one direction), turn them against each other (scatter the tide, but create leaderless chaos), or quiet the relic and deflate both faiths at once. **Branch:** uniting them bends toward "arrive at the forest with a stampede" (a dark mirror of Arc C's allied army); dividing them leaves the country *leaderless and frightened*, easy for the Tithe to pick off; quieting the relic is the "give gladly" path and ties Arc E's resolution to Arc D's source.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** Resolve one prophet and the other shifts (per the bible's "two prophets are one tide" thread). Let both swell and the finale opens on a country *already half-given* to the forest before the party arrive — the Tithe half-collected by panic alone.

### 5. The closer star, the closer terms
- **WHERE.** *Tainted* or back on the *road* — wherever the party are when the star is visibly *closer* and the Collector returns less patient.
- **WHO.** The **Collector** (now naming a date, or a name); the **Glovewright** (Arc B) — her overdue note is in this ledger.
- **DOES.** The star has climbed and brightened; even skeptics are unsettled. The Collector finds the party again and this time is *specific* — he names whose debt he's confirming, and the party may learn it includes the Glovewright's unpaid promise (Arc B), or even a debt incurred in the party's own name without their consent (by the Glovewright's "kindnesses"). He makes clear the route ends at the forest, where the account settles. If the party have been clever (beat 3), they can begin to *negotiate* here.
- **CHOICE.** Open negotiation (deploy the rules learned in the desert), refuse to engage ("learn what interest the ledger charges on a broken word"), or use the Glovewright's note as leverage against the Collector. **Branch:** skilled negotiation shaves debt and earns the Collector's professional respect (he'll deal fairer at the threshold); flat refusal attaches a growing shadow-debuff that escalates directly into Arc F's Follower; leveraging the Glovewright's note threatens *her* (Arc B) — exposing one broker endangers the other.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The party's posture toward the ledger — negotiate / refuse / leverage — is now set, and it determines how the Collector behaves in the finale and whether the refusal-shadow becomes the Follower.

### 6. The star at zenith; the ledger laid open
- **WHERE.** *Void* / forest threshold — beneath the Red Star at its highest, the boundary in sight.
- **WHO.** The **Collector**; the **Barefoot Prophet** and whatever flock he's brought; the **Tithe-Warden** of Arc A, whose ledger the party now see *matches*.
- **DOES.** Under the star at zenith the Prophet's flock gathers at the edge of the world, and the Collector lays the ledger open and names the price aloud. The party see the pages plainly: **bark**, written in root-hair script — *the same ledger the Tithe-Warden carries.* The Collector is the forest's accountant in the mortal world; the star is the debt made visible in the sky; the Prophet's flock is the down payment fear has already gathered. Everything the party did across Arcs D, E (and A) is itemised.
- **CHOICE — the arc's keystone.** Three postures, each setting the finale:
  - **Defuse / expose.** Reveal the Prophet as a fear-merchant and the star as an omen, not a command. The flock disperses; the country *calms*; the Tithe arrives at a steady, unpanicked people the party can help bargain for.
  - **Join / crown.** Let the Prophet crown the star's meaning and lead the flock across. The party arrive *with a mob* — power, and horror: a country half-given before the bargain even opens.
  - **Argue the price.** Use the ledger's own rules (beat 3) to contest the debt line by line. The one place mortal wit beats the fae — a clever party can *shave the Tithe* here.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** Whatever the party do, "the star sets on a changed country either way." Their handling decides who marches *with* them or *against* them to the forest, and feeds directly into Arc A's bargain and Arc F's Follower.

> The clerk is already walking beside you, matching your pace, and you did not see him join the
> road. He is pale in the way of paper, and dressed in the black that funerals wear. Under one arm
> he carries a ledger, and where its cover should be leather it is **bark** — grey, ridged, with
> the small pale flecks of lichen still on it. "Not here for you," he says, pleasantly, before you
> have asked anything at all. "Not yet. Only confirming the route." He makes a small mark you
> cannot see, and closes the book, and the sound it makes is the sound of a branch settling in a
> wind you cannot feel.

> The Prophet stands barefoot on the cold crossroads stones, and you know two of the faces in his
> circle. The drover who shared his trough four days back. The innkeeper's grieving son from the
> Drowned Lantern. They were strangers to this man a week ago. They stand at his shoulders now like
> old friends, like family, like converts — close, and closer than last time, fear worn smooth into
> something that looks almost like peace. "You feel it too," the Prophet calls — to the whole
> crowd, but his eyes find *you*. "The debt is due. The only question left is the one you're all
> afraid to ask out loud: *which of us invited it.*"

## Interconnections

- **Arc A (the Tithe — the spine).** The **Collector's ledger and the Tithe-Warden's ledger are the same bark book** — the campaign's quiet master-reveal that the forest's hunger and mortal debt are one account. The Red Star is the *sky-level* form of Arc A's Tithe; the Prophet's flock is the human stampede the forest's hunger creates. Arguing the Collector's terms is the one mortal lever on Arc A's bargain.
- **Arc B (the Quiet Hand).** The **Glovewright** holds an *overdue note in the Collector's ledger* — so exposing the Collector threatens her, and vice versa. The two brokers trade in the same debts (her glove appears on the Collector); they are mortal mirror-images of the fae market.
- **Arc C (the Gilded Company).** A frightened country is recruiting ground. The Prophet's flock and Cadoc's war-band are opposite answers to the same fear: *follow a faith* vs. *buy a legend*. A spurned Cadoc (Arc C) can defect to the Prophet's flock; an allied Cadoc can be the muscle that breaks it up.
- **Arc D (the Shared Dream).** The **Scavenger-Prophet's immune flock** is the bridge — immune because hollowed, the same surrender as the Dream. The waking salvage-relic (beat 4) is kin to the seed in Arc D's well; quieting one informs the other. The star burns over every white-crossed door.
- **Arc F (the One Who Follows).** Refusing the Collector's terms outright attaches a *shadow-debt that escalates into the Follower* — the ledger's "interest on a broken word" given a face. A client the party freed, or failed to free, can also become the Follower. The Collector may even be the one who *delivers* the Follower to the threshold, "as the contract requires."

## Payoffs & consequences

- **Defused / exposed the Prophet (gave gladly).** The flock disperses; the country calms; the star is read as omen, not command. The Tithe (Arc A) arrives at a steady people the party can *help* bargain rather than a stampede. Banked goodwill; the Collector, treated with wit and respect, deals fair at the threshold.
- **Joined / crowned the star (the mob).** The party arrive at the forest *leading a frightened multitude* — formidable, and damning. Arc A opens on a country *half-given before the bargain*, the Tithe partly collected by panic. A dark mirror of Arc C's allied army: power bought with surrender.
- **Argued the price (mortal wit).** Using the ledger's rules, the party *shave the Tithe* — the single cleverest reduction of Arc A's debt available. The Collector respects a well-kept book; he'll honour an amended line. This is the "win on the rules" ending the folk-horror logic rewards.
- **Refused the ledger outright.** "Learn what interest the ledger charges on a broken word." A growing shadow follows the party — escalating debuffs, ill omens, and finally a face: the Follower (Arc F). The forest sides with the creditor.
- **Let both prophets swell unchecked.** The finale opens on a country already half-given; allies are scarce, the road hostile, and the Tithe arrives most of the way collected. The party bargain from weakness.

## Hooks to seed *(droppable during travel; feed the random-event tables)*

- A pale man in clerk's black asks, with terrible courtesy, whether this *is* the road that goes all the way to the forest — then thanks you and makes a small mark in a book bound in bark. `(narrative)`
- You pass a barefoot man preaching at a crossroads to a crowd of twenty; you'd swear the crowd was twelve, the same twelve, two towns ago. `(narrative)`
- A veiled trader offers water "sweet past reason" for "a memory you won't miss" — and keeps, you notice, a long tally already, in a hand like root-hairs. `(narrative)`
- A woman tells you, matter-of-fact, that she pays her debt "in years" and was allowed to choose the order — "so I'll be old for the bad ones and young again at the end. You can *argue* with him, you know. Most folk never try." `(narrative)`
- A scrap-prophet's banner flutters over a town built of dead machines: THE OLD WORLD DIED OF WANTING — and beneath the floor of his shrine, something is starting to want. `(narrative)`

# Arc F — The One Who Follows

*the follower assembled from the party's own road*

## Premise & themes

Someone walks behind the party. Not stalking — *following*, in the oldest sense: boot-prints that
are the party's number **plus one**, learning to walk inside theirs; small gifts left where they'll
be found; a figure on the dusk ridge who raises one hand, greeting, never threat, a little nearer
each evening. It is not a monster the party haven't met. It is a **return** — a debt, a rival, or a
grief that the party themselves made earlier on the road, shaped entirely by what they did or
failed to do, come to be present at the forest, *where all debts are paid*. Arc F is the campaign's
mirror and its verdict: it has no fixed antagonist because **the party write it**. Its only laws are
fixed — *it arrives, it is owed something, and it is paid at the threshold* — and everything else is
seated by the GM in the party's real history. The themes are intimacy and consequence: the gifts
are kind, which is worse; the hand is raised in friendship, which is worse; the thing that comes is
*known to you*, which is worst of all. If the party gave gladly across the journey, the Follower is
a friend come to stand with them. If they took and grudged, it is the person they wronged, come to
collect in front of the fey — and the forest sides with the creditor.

## The spine (and the assembly procedure)

> **GM procedure — assembling the Follower (do this before beat 1, revisit at beat 4).**
> Arc F's antagonist is *generated*, not authored. Work it in three steps:
>
> 1. **Score the arcs.** Look back across Arcs A–E and rank them by **how carelessly the party
>    handled each** — broken promises, people left to die, bargains grudged, allies spurned,
>    sources weaponised. The *worst-handled* arc supplies the Follower. (Ties: pick the one with
>    the most *intimate* wrong — a named person beats an abstract failure.)
> 2. **Pick the face.** From that arc, choose the specific person/thing the party wronged. See the
>    four variants below; they are templates, not a closed list — a real campaign almost always
>    offers a better, more personal candidate. Prefer the one whose name a player would *wince* to
>    hear.
> 3. **Decide the debt.** Name what the Follower is owed — a death, a daughter, a rescue, a word
>    kept, a forgiveness. This is what gets *paid at the threshold*, and how the party respond
>    (pay / refuse / be forgiven) is the finale's emotional hinge.
>
> **If the party gave gladly across the board** (rare, earned), invert the whole procedure: the
> Follower is drawn from the arc they handled *best*, and arrives as a friend — Wrenna returned,
> Maready cured, Cadoc redeemed — to stand with them, not against them.

### The four default variants

> Use these when the campaign hasn't produced something more personal. Each names the source arc,
> the face, the debt, what the gifts mean, and what they say at the fire.

- **Variant 1 — The Dream-Dead (from Arc D).** *Worst-handled arc: the Shared Dream.* The Follower
  is **a villager the party let die** — or "cured" with the Pedlar's killing draught, or doomed by
  letting a source spread. **Owed:** a death witnessed, a name spoken aloud, the dignity the party
  denied them. **The gifts** are domestic and unbearable — clean water left in a cup, a fire banked
  the way a parent does it, a single flower from a garden that's now grey grass. **At the fire** they
  do not accuse; they *thank* the party, for something the party didn't do, which is worse, and then
  they ask, quietly, to be *counted* — to be added to the forest's list now that they have no other.
  *The forest accepts them as the party's unpaid tithe.*

- **Variant 2 — The Spurned Rival (from Arc C).** *Worst-handled arc: the Gilded Company.* The
  Follower is **Sir Cadoc Vane**, left to die or shamed at the Wolf-Winter's wall, now something
  colder than a man — pride that outlived its body, a legend that refuses to end unwitnessed.
  **Owed:** the rescue the party withheld, the glory they took from him, an acknowledgement. **The
  gifts** are martial and proud — his own polished blade laid across the path, a trophy re-claimed
  and left as a calling-card, the Company's banner planted on the dusk ridge. **At the fire** he is
  terrible and almost gentle: *"You could have pulled me out. You chose the bounty. I only came to
  watch you choose again — at the one door where there's no glory in it."* If Ilse survived, she may
  walk with him, grieving, or against him, to stop what he's become.

- **Variant 3 — The Claimed Healer (from Arc D/A).** *Worst-handled: Maready left to the Dream.*
  The Follower is **Sister Maready**, who kept walking the road the party abandoned her on and was
  *claimed* — she dreams the forest now, fully, and follows it through the party. **Owed:** the help
  the party didn't spare, one more village she couldn't save without them. **The gifts** are a
  healer's reflexes turned wrong — a wound dressed in your sleep, a fever-sweat wiped from your brow
  by a cold hand, simples left in your pack that smell of leaf-mould. **At the fire** she is serene
  and lost: *"I found the place we were all dreaming of. It's lovely. I came back the only way it
  let me — to bring you in. You'll like it. Everyone does, in the end."*

- **Variant 4 — The Grown Changeling (from Arc A).** *Worst-handled: the spine itself — debts
  grudged, generosity refused, Wrenna failed.* The Follower is **Wrenna's stolen daughter, grown**
  — the fey-thing that wears her, sent ahead as the Tithe's collector-in-miniature, the "one extra"
  the Tithe-Warden counted made flesh. **Owed:** the price the party refused to pay gladly, now come
  due with a child's face and a fey's patience. **The gifts** are the cruelest — a child's shoe set
  by the fire (Wrenna's cord, completed), a tune hummed just at the edge of sleep, a hand-picked
  posy of the exact flowers Wrenna's daughter loved. **At the fire** she calls the party what she
  calls Wrenna now — *"sister"* — and means it, and that is the whole horror: the debt has adopted
  you. *She is the Tithe's interest, walking.*

### 1. The number plus one
- **WHERE.** *Any* biome — first in soft ground (mud, snow, dust, ash) where prints take.
- **WHO.** No one visible. The Follower (per assembly) is implied only.
- **DOES.** The party notice, crossing soft ground, that the boot-prints behind them number *their party plus one* — and the extra set is learning to walk inside theirs, stepping where they step, a beat behind. No one is there when they turn. The Tithe-Warden's "you carry more than you packed" (Arc A) lands retroactively as literal.
- **CHOICE.** Investigate (backtrack, set a watch) or press on uneasy. **Branch:** backtracking finds the prints simply *stop*, or double, never a body; pressing on lets the Follower close distance faster. Neither dispels it — Arc F cannot be outrun, only met.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The party know they are accompanied. Dread is seeded; the gifts begin.

### 2. The kindnesses, but wrong
- **WHERE.** *Any* — at camp, at a wayside, wherever the party leave and return.
- **WHO.** Still unseen. Cross-reference the **Quiet Hand** (Arc B): these are *her* kind of small mercies, but intimate, uninvited, *known to you*.
- **DOES.** Things go missing and return changed; a trinket vanishes and reappears mended; the fire is banked exactly the way someone the party knew used to do it; a single fresh flower is laid where they'll find it, in a biome where nothing blooms. The gifts are *specific to the Follower's identity* (see variants) — and a sharp party will start to guess *who*.
- **CHOICE.** Accept the kindnesses (and owe, a little, each time), reject them (leave the gifts, scatter the banked fire), or try to bait the giver. **Branch:** accepting deepens the debt the Follower will name; rejecting is noticed, and the gifts turn from comfort to *reminder* (the missing trinket comes back broken, pointedly); baiting almost works — the party glimpse a shape, and recognise the gait.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The Follower's identity moves from mystery toward dread-certainty. The gifts are the arc telling the party *who they failed*, gently, before it asks them to answer for it.

### 3. The hand raised at dusk
- **WHERE.** *Any* — always at the edge: the far ridge, the treeline, across a frozen lake, at the rim of firelight.
- **WHO.** The Follower, now *seen* — at distance, never closer than the edge, until the threshold.
- **DOES.** Each dusk now, a figure stands at the far edge of the day and *raises one hand* — unmistakably a greeting, not a threat — and is nearer every evening, though never seen to approach. The party can make out enough to *confirm* the identity the gifts implied. The figure never speaks across the distance; it only waits to be let near, and the closing distance is the campaign's clock.
- **CHOICE.** Approach it (it withdraws, always one ridge further — it will not be met until the threshold), signal back (it pauses, as if grateful, then resumes), or ignore it (it comes faster). **Branch:** acknowledging the Follower — a wave returned — is *remembered* at the threshold as a small grace; refusing all contact makes the firelight meeting colder.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** By the time the party reach the void, there is no doubt who follows — only the question of what is owed and whether the party will pay it.

### 4. (Optional) The mirror moment
- **WHERE.** *Any* late biome — a still pool, a polished shield, a stretch of black ice, the Collector's open ledger.
- **WHO.** The Follower; possibly the **Collector** (Arc E) or **Tithe-Warden** (Arc A), who "knows the name on the account."
- **DOES.** A beat where the party are forced to *say the name* — see the Follower's face clearly, or read it in the ledger, or have an NPC name it for them. This is the GM's chance to confirm the assembly (step 2 of the procedure) on-screen and to let the party feel the full weight of *which* failure walked all this way. The Collector, if used, treats the Follower as a line-item: *"Ah. The one you left. The contract does require their presence."*
- **CHOICE.** Own it (accept that this is theirs to answer) or deny it (insist it isn't their fault). **Branch:** ownership unlocks the *reckoning-as-reconciliation* path at the threshold; denial hardens the meeting into pure debt-collection.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The finale's emotional register — reunion, reckoning, or reconciliation — is set here.

### 5. The firelight — the Follower steps in
- **WHERE.** *Void* / forest threshold — the night before the boundary, at the party's own fire.
- **WHO.** The Follower, at last within the light; the **Courteous Guide** waiting beyond; whatever allies the party banked (Maready, Cadoc, Wrenna) present or absent per Arcs A–E.
- **DOES.** The Follower finally crosses into the firelight and is *someone tied to a road already walked* (per assembly). What they came to say bends the finale: a debt to pay, a warning to heed, a forgiveness to accept or refuse. They do not attack. They *settle in*, like family, and speak the thing they walked the whole country to say.
- **CHOICE — the arc's keystone, and the campaign's mirror.**
  - **Pay / make right.** Give the Follower what is owed — a death witnessed, a name spoken, a daughter mourned, a glory acknowledged. The debt clears; the party cross the threshold *unburdened by this one thing*, and Arc A's bargain weighs lighter by exactly that grace.
  - **Refuse / deny.** Turn the Follower away. The forest *sides with the creditor*: the Follower walks in with the party as the Tithe's witness, and the bargain (Arc A) is conducted with the wronged party standing at the fey's elbow, naming the debt aloud.
  - **Be forgiven (if owned, beat 4).** If the party owned the wrong, the Follower may offer *forgiveness* instead of collection — the rarest outcome, available only to a party that didn't flinch from the name. It clears the debt *and* gives the party a witness on their side at the threshold.
- **DOWNSTREAM.** The Follower's resolution is the personal face of Arc A's tally — the single most emotionally weighted input to the finale, and the proof of the campaign's thesis made flesh.

## Read-aloud moments

> The ground here is soft enough to take a print, and so you count them, because something has been
> nagging at you for a day now. Your boots. Your companions'. And then — set carefully inside the
> last of your own, a beat behind, smaller, learning the stride — *one more.* You turn. The road
> behind you is empty, and the wind moves the grass, and the one extra print is already filling
> slowly with water that wasn't there a moment ago.

> The fire is banked when you come back to it — not scattered, not robbed, *banked*, the coals
> raked over and the good wood set aside for morning, exactly the way your father used to do it, the
> way you have never once done it yourself. Beside it, where you will not be able to miss it, someone
> has laid a single flower. It is fresh. It is the kind that grew outside a house you remember. There
> is no such flower within a hundred miles of here, and there has not been, for a long time.

> On the last ridge before the trees, the figure that has walked behind you all this long country
> finally stops withdrawing. It comes down into the firelight, and sits, and warms its hands like
> anyone would, and looks at you with a face you know — a face you had hoped, a little, never to see
> again. It does not reach for a weapon. It does not raise its voice. It only says your name, the way
> someone says it who has been saving it up for a hundred miles, and then it tells you, gently, what
> it came all this way to be paid.

## Interconnections

- **Arc A (the Tithe — the spine).** Arc F *is* the in-code "One Who Follows" thread made into the
  campaign's verdict — the **personal face of Arc A's Tithe.** The "one extra" the Tithe-Warden
  counts (Arc A, beat 3) *is* the Follower; the boot-print plus one is the Tithe's interest given a
  body. Whatever the party owe across the whole journey, the Follower is the part of it that has a
  name and walks behind them to the threshold to collect.
- **Arc B (the Quiet Hand).** The Follower's *kindnesses-but-wrong* deliberately echo the
  Glovewright's smoothing of the road — the same gestures (a cache, a mercy, a thing handled for
  you) turned intimate and uninvited. If the Glovewright's **runner child** is the wronged party
  (paid at last "in the only coin left"), Arc F resolves into Arc B directly.
- **Arc C (the Gilded Company).** **Variant 2** seats the Follower as a spurned **Cadoc Vane** — Arc
  C's "make an ally, a debt, or a corpse of Cadoc" cashed out as a stalker who came to watch the
  party choose again. **Ilse** can walk with the Follower (grieving) or against him (to stop him).
- **Arc D (the Shared Dream).** Arc D is the **most common source** of the Follower — a villager
  let die, a Pedlar-"cured" sleeper, or a claimed **Maready** (Variants 1 and 3). The gifts (clean
  water, a banked fire, a flower from grey-grass land) are Arc D's imagery turned personal.
- **Arc E (the Ledger and the Red Star).** Refusing the Collector's terms (Arc E, beat 5) attaches
  the **shadow-debt that becomes the Follower** — "the interest the ledger charges on a broken
  word," given a face. The **Collector** may *deliver* the Follower to the threshold "as the
  contract requires," and can be the NPC who *names* the Follower at the mirror moment (beat 4).

## Payoffs & consequences

- **Gave gladly across the journey → the Follower is a friend.** Wrenna returned with her girl;
  Maready cured; Cadoc redeemed. They cross into the firelight to *stand with* the party at the
  threshold — a witness on the party's side as Arc A's bargain opens. The campaign's thesis
  (*give gladly*) is rewarded with company at the one door no one wants to face alone.
- **Took and grudged → the Follower is the person they wronged.** They come to be **paid in front
  of the fey**, and the forest sides with the creditor. Arc A's bargain is conducted with the
  wronged standing at the fey's elbow. The doubled price of the Tithe (Arc A's hoarder ending) is
  exacted *through* the Follower — the thing the party value most is taken to settle the Follower's
  claim.
- **Paid the debt at the fire.** The specific wrong is cleared; the party cross unburdened by that
  one thing, and Arc A weighs lighter by exactly that grace — sometimes the margin between walking
  out "neither aged a day" and hanging at the forest's edge as a swaying token.
- **Refused / denied.** The Follower walks in as the Tithe's witness; every ungiven kindness across
  the campaign is remembered against the party; the epilogue's "forest's edge hung with tokens,
  swaying in no wind" (Arc A) includes a token that is *theirs*, placed by the one who followed.
- **Was forgiven.** Rarest. A party that owned its worst failure is *forgiven* by the one it
  wronged — the debt clears and the witness stands on the party's side. The forest, which keeps
  books, notes that a debt was *released* rather than collected, and that is the one entry it does
  not know how to charge interest on.

# The braid converges (GM one-page)

All three arcs are the same debt, met three ways, arriving at the same door:

| | **Arc D — Shared Dream** | **Arc E — Ledger & Red Star** | **Arc F — One Who Follows** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The debt appears as** | a fever on the water | a star in the sky / a clerk on the road | a print in the ground, plus one |
| **The shared object** | the seed (a forest-tendril in the watershed) | the **bark ledger** | the "one extra" the ledger counts |
| **Mortal face** | Sister Maready, the Glovewright | the Collector, the Barefoot Prophet | whoever the party wronged |
| **Fey truth (Arc A)** | the Tithe leaking downstream | the Tithe written in the sky | the Tithe's interest, given a body |
| **The lever** | close the source upstream | argue the ledger's terms | pay the debt at the fire |
| **Feeds the finale by** | how far the forest reaches | who marches with/against the party | who stands at the fire — friend or creditor |

**Run them concurrently.** Seed D on the first river, E under the first dusk sky, F in the first
soft ground — and let the party's handling of each *write the others*: a village let die of the
Dream (D) becomes the Follower (F); a refused ledger-term (E) attaches the shadow that becomes the
Follower (F); the Collector (E) and the Tithe-Warden (A) share the ledger that counts the Follower
(F). They all set down their burden at the **threshold** — the pale ring of mushrooms, the arch of
living fallen branch — where Arc A opens the forest and asks the one question the whole country has
been afraid to say out loud: *what did you give, and what did you grudge?*

*Give gladly.*

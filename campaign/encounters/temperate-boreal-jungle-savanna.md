# PRIMUS Encounter Set — Temperate · Boreal · Jungle · Savanna

*Writers'-room flesh on the PRIMUS-BIBLE Section 3 skeleton. Combat foes are drawn from the
real `BIOME_BANDS` roster in `cavril-encounter-stage.js` (t1 = party tier 1–4, t2 = 5–10,
t3 = 11–16, t4 = 17–20). Arc/NPC ties name the bible's Section 1 arcs (A–F) and Section 2 cast.*

**DC convention:** where a check scales, it's written `DC [12/15/18/21 by tier]` so the GM fills
the band the party is in. `[bracketed]` text is a GM-localize blank.

**Canon order of precedence:** in-code `JOURNEY_THREADS` > this set > improvisation. *Give gladly.*

# TEMPERATE

> *Bluebells over hoof-torn earth; worked land with no one in sight.* The starter biome — where the
> Fading first whispers (the shadowless butterfly, the hummed tune) and the country is still
> talkative enough to drop every hook it owns. Foes: Wolf/Boar/Bandit up through Treant and Adult
> Green Dragon. **Targets: Combat 8 · Social 5 · Discovery 5 · Hazard 3 · Puzzle 3 (24).**

## Combat (8)

### 1. The Hoof-Torn Hedgerow — *combat · tier 1–4*
**Read-aloud:** The hedge has been broken through from the field side, not the road — a gap chewed
wide, the bluebells beneath it flattened in a single panicked rush. Wool snagged on the thorns is
still warm. Somewhere past the next rise, a sheep is screaming the way sheep do when it has already
decided it will die.
**Situation:** A wolf pack (or a lone boar gone rogue) has been working this farm's flock for a
week; the farmer is out of arrows and nearly out of nerve. The party can intercept the predators at
the kill or follow the blood-trail to the den.
**Foes/DCs:** `pack` — Wolf ×4–6, or `packLeader` Boar ×2 + Black Bear ×1. Survival to track the
den DC [12]. A Handle Animal / Persuasion check calms the surviving flock-dog into helping (DC [13]).
**Develops into:** The farmer's thanks come with the week's gossip — *"a fine company came through,
gold-trimmed, paid the bounty-board man to read them first."* (Arc C — **Sir Cadoc Vane** is one
day ahead.) If the party spare a wounded wolf, it turns up again, limping, in the boreal Wolf-Winter.

### 2. The Pre-Claimed Bounty — *combat · tier 1–4*
**Read-aloud:** The bandits have a campfire, a stolen cart, and an argument going about which of
them gets the gilded brooch they pried off "the knight's man" — until they notice you, and the
argument becomes very quickly a single shared opinion about your purse.
**Situation:** A bandit crew the Gilded Company *roughed up and left* rather than finish. They're
sore, broke, and carrying a Company brooch they can't sell. Take them, and the brooch is a thread
straight to Cadoc.
**Foes/DCs:** `skirmish` — Bandit ×3–4 + Spy ×1 (lurker). At tier 5–10, Thug/Berserker ×4 +
Bandit Captain. Intimidation makes two of them bolt (DC [13/15]).
**Develops into:** The brooch and a coerced confession reveal Cadoc "leaves the small fish for the
locals to net — keeps the towns grateful." (Arc C.) The cart's manifest lists medicine bound for
a river-village with white-crossed doors. (Arc D — **the Shared Dream**, running one leg ahead.)

### 3. Druid's Cordon — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The treeline does not want you. Brambles you'd swear were ankle-high a moment ago
now reach your thigh, and the birds have gone two-toned and wrong. A woman in bark-grey stands where
no woman stood, and she is not asking you to leave so much as informing you that you already have.
**Situation:** A grove-druid (and her beasts) defends a wood the party must cross — or she's testing
whether they *take* or *ask*. Violence wins the fight and loses the wood's goodwill for three biomes;
parley (see Social #3 if you'd rather) flips this entirely.
**Foes/DCs:** `mixed` — Druid ×1 (lurker) + Worg ×3 + Giant Boar ×1; she wild-shapes and summons.
At tier 11–16, Druid + Treant ×1. Nature/Persuasion to recognize the rite and back off: DC [15/18].
**Develops into:** Beaten, she warns through bloodied teeth: *"You walk the collecting-road. The
butterflies told the trees. Give gladly when it asks, or it takes the wood instead of you."* (Arc A
— **the Tithe**, named early by something that would know.)

### 4. The Owlbear in the Orchard — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The orchard is silent in the particular way of a place where the small animals have
all left. Half-eaten apples litter the rows, each with one enormous bite. Then the apple-store
door — barred from outside, scored to splinters from within — shudders.
**Situation:** A starving owlbear has denned in a farm's apple-store, trapping the family's youngest
inside with it (alive, terrified, wedged in the rafters). A straight fight risks the child; luring
the beast out first is the kind play.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Owlbear ×1. At tier 11–16, swap to Troll ×1 (regenerating; needs fire/acid).
Stealth or Animal Handling to draw it clear of the child DC [14]. Athletics to wrench the door DC [15].
**Develops into:** The grateful family has nothing to give but a name — *"the Glovewright's runner
came by, left coin for a mended fence and never a face."* (Arc B — **the Quiet Hand** is already
smoothing this road.) The child, saved, draws no shadow at noon for a week after. (Arc A — the Fading.)

### 5. Toll on the King's Road — *combat · tier 5–10 · road feature*
**Read-aloud:** The milestone ahead has been freshly painted, and what it says is not a distance.
It says PAY. Three road-hard veterans lean on spears across the way, unbothered, almost bored —
they have done this a great many times and expect to do it a great many more.
**Situation:** Toll-collectors working *for* (or claiming to work for) the King of the Road. They'll
take coin, goods, or "a story never told before" — and they genuinely prefer the story. Fight them
and you've insulted the Road itself.
**Foes/DCs:** `skirmish` (road band) — Veteran ×2 + Scout ×2, or Bandit Captain ×1 + Thug ×3.
Deception to invent a true-feeling untold story DC [15]; Insight reads whether they're the King's
men or grifters DC [14].
**Develops into:** If the party simply *can't* pay and don't fight, a runner-child in a too-big
glove steps from nowhere, drops a purse, says *"a friend goes ahead of you,"* and is gone. (Arc B.)
Cheat the toll and every milestone for a day reads the same distance. (Feature — **the King of the Road**.)

### 6. The Hill Giant's Tithe-Barn — *combat · tier 11–16*
**Read-aloud:** The barn is the size of a manor and it is being emptied by a creature the size of the
barn. The giant works methodically, almost mournfully, stacking grain-sacks into a sledge — and it
keeps glancing east, toward the deep green smudge on the horizon, as if it too has somewhere it's
been told to be.
**Situation:** A hill giant is stripping a village's winter stores — but it isn't hoarding, it's
*carrying tribute somewhere*. The village will pay for its death; the smarter prize is learning
where the food is going.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo`/`packLeader` — Hill Giant ×1, or Hill Giant + Ogre ×2. At tier 17–20,
Stone Giant ×1 + Treant ×1. Insight to notice it's gathering, not gorging, DC [16].
**Develops into:** Track the sledge and it leads toward an arch of living fallen branch — the giant
is paying its own tithe to the forest. (Arc A — *"the Hunt's wounded beast is going home to pay; so
is everything else."*) **The Tithe-Warden** has been counting the giant too.

### 7. Green Dragon at the Wood's Heart — *combat · tier 17–20*
**Read-aloud:** The old wood goes quiet all at once, the way a room does when the wrong person
enters. The leaf-litter is laced with the sweet-rot smell of green dragon, and the trees lean
imperceptibly inward, listening. A voice like wet bark and old gold says it has been *expecting*
company — and it says it in the cadence of the fey, not the greed of a wyrm.
**Situation:** An adult green dragon has made itself the forest's gatekeeper-by-proxy, "guarding the
road in" — half its own ambition, half a bargain it struck with something deeper. It will trade
passage for a secret, a debt, or a death; or it will simply eat the conversation.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Adult Green Dragon ×1 (add Treant ×2 as the wood "helping"). Persuasion /
Deception in fey-courtesy register DC [18]; Insight to spot its bargain has *its own* creditor DC [19].
**Develops into:** Slain or bargained-with, it lets slip the road's true terminus: *"You're on the
ledger. I only collect the toll; I don't set the price. Mind you give gladly — I didn't, once."*
(Arc A; ties the dragon's own greed to the Tithe — the forest collects from wyrms too.)

### 8. The Stained-Glass Swarm — *combat · tier 1–4 (escalates) · Fading set-piece*
**Read-aloud:** First it is beautiful: a drift of butterflies the colour of cathedral windows,
pouring across the meadow toward you. Then you notice none of them cast a shadow on the bright grass,
and the drift is not drifting — it is *converging*, and where the leading edge touches a flower the
colour runs out of it like water from a wrung cloth.
**Situation:** The Fading made briefly aggressive — a swarm of shadowless butterflies that *leaches*.
Mechanically a swarm hazard-fight; thematically the campaign's opening note. Standing in it too long
drains colour, then warmth, then memory.
**Foes/DCs:** Treat as Swarm of Insects ×2–3 with a rider: each round in the swarm, CON save DC [12]
or lose [1] colour-tier (cosmetic → then a level of exhaustion at 0). A hummed tune (any Performance,
DC [10]) makes them *follow* instead of feed — they want the song.
**Develops into:** That night, everyone who stood in the swarm dreams the same dream of the same far
green place. (Arc A + Arc D braided — the party are marked as carriers; a plague-village two legs on
will describe this exact dream.) **Wrenna**, if near, hums the tune in her sleep, word for word.

## Social (5)

### 9. The Drover's Long Gossip — *social · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The drover lifts a hand before you've said a word — half greeting, half "don't spook
the herd" — and falls into step beside you like the road's been waiting all morning to introduce
someone. By the third anecdote you realize he's told you four things worth knowing and asked you
nothing at all.
**Situation:** A walking newspaper. He trades rumour for company and a share of the fire. Every fact
he gives is *true but slanted* by who told him. The party's chance to load up on hooks cheaply.
**Foes/DCs:** None. Insight (DC [12]) sorts which rumours are first-hand. A drink loosens a fifth
(Persuasion DC [10]).
**Develops into:** Pick any two live arcs and seed them here: *the red star "means a hard winter, my
gran says" (Arc E); a healer "out of everything, working herself to bone, two villages over" (Arc D
— **Sister Maready**); a fine company "buying every bounty before honest folk can read it" (Arc C);
a pale clerk "asking after names, not coin" (Arc E — **the Collector**).*

### 10. The Bounty-Board, Already Read — *social/trade · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The board outside the [taphouse] is papered three deep, and a boy with an ink-stained
thumb is peeling the freshest notice down even as you approach. "Sorry," he says, not sorry. "Read
and claimed. Company pays for first look." Behind him, a gold-trimmed scout watches you read the
leftovers.
**Situation:** The Gilded Company has bought first-read rights on the local bounties. The party can
out-bid, out-charm, or out-fast them — or take the scraps the Company judged beneath them (which are
often the *interesting* ones).
**Foes/DCs:** Persuasion/Intimidation on the board-boy to leak the good notice DC [13]; a gold piece
does it without a roll. Insight on the Company scout reads contempt covering *worry* (DC [15]).
**Develops into:** The scout, if engaged, lets a crack show: *"Captain's been odd since the savanna
talk. Says there's a beast worth a name out there."* (Arc C — Cadoc and the Great Hunt's wrong-beast.)
The leftover bounty is a child gone missing "into the green," never found. (Arc A — early Wrenna echo.)

### 11. The Druid, Asked Not Fought — *social · tier 5–10 · alt to Combat #3*
**Read-aloud:** You lower your hands first, and so does the wood. The brambles ease. The bark-grey
woman tilts her head the way a heron does, deciding whether you're worth the strike — and then she
simply waits, because you came to her wood, and the next word is yours to spend well or badly.
**Situation:** The grove-druid as a parley instead of a brawl. She gates a shortcut through the wood.
She'll grant passage to those who *ask and give* — a name, a true secret, a day's labour mending what
travellers broke. She is, quietly, an agent of the same law the forest runs on.
**Foes/DCs:** Persuasion in the giving register DC [15]; Nature to offer the *right* tribute (a buried
seed, a poured libation) DC [14]. Trying to haggle her *down* fails automatically — "grudging payment
costs double."
**Develops into:** Pass her test and she teaches the road's first rule aloud: *"It will ask. Give
before it takes. The shadowless ones are how it counts who's coming."* (Arc A.) She names **Wrenna**
— *"the barefoot mother passed yesterday, would not give me even her name."*

### 12. The Glovewright's Runner — *social · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The child can't be more than ten and wears a single glove three sizes too big,
folded back at the wrist twice. They press a wax-sealed note into your hands, won't take coin, won't
give a name, and are already walking backward into the crowd by the time you read the first line:
*A friend goes ahead of you. Mind the bridge at [the ford]; it is mended now.*
**Situation:** First contact with the Quiet Hand, at one remove. The runner delivers a true,
useful, *unasked-for* kindness — and the glove's mark is something the party will see again on a
corpse, a king, or a clerk.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight on the child reads fear under the errand (DC [13]) — *they don't choose this
work.* Persuasion to coax a name fails; coax a *direction* and they point east, toward the green (DC [14]).
**Develops into:** The mended bridge is exactly where the note said. (Arc B — **the Glovewright**, her
reach felt before her face.) If the party are kind to the runner, the runner is who follows them at
dusk, biomes later, paid at last "in the only coin left." (Arc F seed — let it land if they're cruel instead.)

### 13. The Collector Falls Into Step — *social · any tier · road*
**Read-aloud:** You did not hear him approach and you cannot say how long he's been walking beside
you — a pale man, funeral-black, a ledger bound in pale bark under one arm. He does not look at you.
He is reading the road. "Don't be alarmed," he says pleasantly. "I'm not here for you. Not yet. Only
confirming the route."
**Situation:** The campaign's omen-in-a-coat, introduced gently. He won't fight, won't be hurried,
won't say whose debt — only that there *is* one, "incurred long ago, by blood or by bargain," and
that the party are "on the way to where it's paid." The terms, he hints, can be *argued* by those clever enough.
**Foes/DCs:** He cannot be meaningfully attacked here (treat as plot-armoured; if struck, he simply
"steps to the left" and is gone, leaving frost). Insight reads that he is *counting the party — and
counts one more than there are* (DC [16]). Persuasion gets one true answer; spend it well.
**Develops into:** *"Your ledger and another's are the same book,"* he says, tapping the bark cover —
the same ledger **the Tithe-Warden** carries (Arc E ↔ Arc A reveal, planted early). He mentions
"a woman who pays in years, a man who gave up his name" — and, almost kindly, that **the Glovewright**
holds an overdue note with him. (Arc B ↔ Arc E.)

## Discovery (5)

### 14. The Worked Field With No One In It — *discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The plough stands mid-furrow, the horse-harness laid neatly over it, a water-jug
still upright in the shade. The field is half-turned. The bread in the cottage is two days stale and
the door stands open. Everything says the people stepped out for a moment, three mornings ago, and
simply never stepped back.
**Situation:** An abandoned holding — no blood, no struggle, no bodies. The signature temperate
unease. The truth is GM-seatable: they walked east following a song; they fled the white-cross
fever; they were the Tithe's first quiet collection.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation pieces together a direction-of-leaving (all tracks east, none returning)
DC [13]. Survival ages the abandonment to "three dawns" DC [12]. Religion notes the family shrine's
offering bowl licked utterly clean (DC [14]).
**Develops into:** A child's chalk drawing on the hearth-stone: a forest with an open door, drawn
by someone who'd never seen one. (Arc A — the dream reaching upstream / Arc D.) Whichever truth the
GM picks, a neighbour two hexes on confirms it and adds the next hook.

### 15. The Shadowless Butterfly — *discovery · any tier · Fading*
**Read-aloud:** It lands on the back of your hand, weightless, the exact blue-and-gold of a chapel's
best window — and where the noon sun should lay its small shadow on your skin, there is nothing. The
hand is bright all over. The butterfly opens and closes its wings, patient, as if waiting to be noticed.
**Situation:** The single most important discovery in the starter biome and the campaign's first true
*wrongness*. Harmless to touch; deeply diagnostic to understand. This is the Fading saying *hello*.
**Foes/DCs:** Nature/Arcana to confirm "it casts no shadow, and neither, now, does the flower it
sat on" DC [14]. Following its drift leads unerringly east (Survival DC [10] — it barely tries to hide).
**Develops into:** That night, the dream. (Arc A primary — the party are *marked*; this is Beat 1 of
the spine.) A herbalist or **Sister Maready** later: *"Shadowless things. The fever-folk draw them in
their sleep. It's begun, then."* (Arc D.) The tune from Combat #8 hums faintly wherever the butterfly went.

### 16. The Standing Stones That Lean East — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** Seven old stones in a meadow, lichen-furred and older than any road — and every one
of them tilts the same handful of degrees, like a congregation turned toward a preacher you can't
see. Set a pebble on the flat of the leaning one and it rolls off the same way. East. Toward the green.
**Situation:** A pre-human waymark that has begun, subtly, to *point*. Extends `BIOME_THEMES.site`.
The lean is recent — moss-lines show the stones stood upright within living memory.
**Foes/DCs:** History/Religion to read the original purpose (a border-rite, a "do-not-cross") DC [15].
Investigation spots the fresh soil where each base shifted, *this season* (DC [13]).
**Develops into:** A rubbing of the eroded glyphs matches marks the party will see again on the
forest's threshold arch. (Arc A — the country itself orienting toward the Tithe.) Camp here and the
dusk brings boot-prints among the stones: the party's number, plus one. (Arc F.)

### 17. The Eel-Wife's Wagon — *discovery/trade · any tier · river feature*
**Read-aloud:** The wagon smells of brine and river-mud and is hung all over with smoked eels like
strange brown bunting. The woman driving it has river-grey eyes and a smile with one too many teeth
in it, and she greets you as if she's been expecting precisely your faces, which she has not, surely.
**Situation:** A roadside merchant (per `MerchantEconomy`) who deals in river-goods, charms, and
*rumour priced as merchandise*. She is mortal — probably — but she trades in the same coin the fey
do, and one item on her board is two biomes too strange.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight to spot which of her "lucky charms" is genuinely fey-touched DC [15]. A good
haggle (Persuasion DC [13]) gets her best rumour thrown in free.
**Develops into:** She'll sell, cheap, a "hazel-charm against thirst" — *"a green-eyed lady left a sack
of them, said folk'd need them down the road."* (Arc A — **the Green-Eyed Sister**'s hazelnut, pre-placed.)
She knows the drowned bell offshore "tolls for them as owe." (Arc A — Ferryman's Debt, foreshadowed inland.)

### 18. The Hedge-Shrine and the Clean Bowl — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** Tucked into the crook of two hedgerows: a shrine no taller than a child, a worn
figure too smoothed to name, and a stone offering-bowl. The bowl has been wiped so clean it gleams —
not empty-from-neglect but *licked*, the moss around it eaten back in a perfect ring.
**Situation:** A folk-shrine where the old "leave a little for them" custom is being *enforced* lately.
Locals have started over-tithing it out of a fear they can't name. The ring of eaten moss widens
weekly. A coin or crumb left here matters later; an *insult* left here matters more.
**Foes/DCs:** Religion to know the custom and the right small gift DC [12]. Investigation reads the
ring's weekly growth from the moss-lines (DC [14]). Leaving nothing (or taking the bowl) earns a
lingering ill-luck the GM can cash in.
**Develops into:** Give gladly here and a later fey bargain opens one notch friendlier — **the
Tithe-Warden** "has it on the ledger." (Arc A — the campaign's generosity tally starts somewhere.)
An old woman tending it: *"Feed the little gods now. The big one's coming to collect, and it counts."*

## Hazard (3)

### 19. The Sour Well — *hazard · any tier · Arc D*
**Read-aloud:** The well-rope is up, the bucket's by the trough, and the whole green sward around the
well-mouth is dying in a slow circle — grass gone the colour of weak tea, widest on the downhill side.
The water, when you draw it, smells faintly of pond and far-off flowers, and tastes, just for a
second, of someone else's dream.
**Situation:** The Shared Dream, arrived by water. Drinking risks the fever (and the dream). The
village downstream is days from sickening and doesn't know it. The dying-grass circle is the Spreading
in miniature, an early geographic tell.
**Foes/DCs:** Medicine/Nature to identify the taint as waterborne, not poison, DC [14]. CON save on
drinking DC [13] or take the dream that night + 1 exhaustion. Survival traces the foul *upstream*
(the source is two biomes off) DC [13].
**Develops into:** Cap the well and warn the village → goodwill the Tithe-Warden later counts (Arc D
→ Arc A). Ignore it → return through here biomes later to white-crossed doors and a fresh grave-row.
A scrap of paper in the bucket: *"It is not a sickness. It is a seed."* (Arc D — the Spreading's journal, leaked downstream.)

### 20. The Mended Bridge That Wasn't — *hazard · any tier · road · Arc B*
**Read-aloud:** The ford-bridge looks freshly repaired — new planks, bright nails, a craftsman's
neat work. It is only when you're halfway across, and the new planks flex like green wood that was
never seasoned, that you understand the repair was done *fast*, by someone who needed you across and
gone, not someone who meant it to last.
**Situation:** A bridge the Glovewright's people mended in haste to speed the party along — sound
enough today, treacherous under load or in a day's time. A benefactor's kindness with a hairline
crack in it. Heavy gear, a wagon, or a second crossing tests it.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation spots the unseasoned timber *before* the middle DC [13]. DEX save to keep
footing if a plank gives DC [14]; Athletics to catch a falling companion DC [13]. Crossing slow and
light avoids the roll.
**Develops into:** The repair-mark is the Glovewright's. (Arc B — *every kindness she buys has a
deferred cost.*) If the party later expose or spurn her, *this is one of the kindnesses that
reverses* — the bridge fails behind them on the way back. (Arc B payoff seed.)

### 21. The Bluebell Bog — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The bluebells don't stop at the wood's edge; they march right on across what looks
like more meadow, a sweep of nodding blue too lovely to distrust. Only the smell warns you — under
the flower-scent, the black-water reek of ground that isn't ground. The first wrong step goes in to
the knee and keeps going.
**Situation:** A classic concealed bog dressed in temperate beauty — flowers grown right over sucking
mire. Pack-laden travellers and panicking horses are at most risk. The pretty cover is the trap.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival/Nature to read the false-meadow before stepping in DC [13]. STR (Athletics) to
pull free, rising each failed round, DC [13/15/17…]. Roping up (Survival DC [10]) lets the party
self-rescue. Rushing in after a sinking companion risks two stuck instead of one.
**Develops into:** Sunk in the peat, perfectly preserved: an older traveller in pilgrim-grey, one
foot bare, a child's shoe still corded at the throat. (Arc A / Arc F — *this is what becomes of a
pilgrim who would not give her name*; a grim mirror of **Wrenna**'s possible fate, dated years back.)

## Puzzle (3)

### 22. The Hummed Tune — *puzzle · any tier · Fading*
**Read-aloud:** You all know the tune. None of you can say from where. The shepherd-boy hums it
tending his flock; the miller's wife hums it at her wheel; it's in the creak of the inn-sign and the
wind in the bluebells, and the longer you're in this valley the surer you are that you knew it before
you came, and that it is, very slowly, teaching itself to you.
**Situation:** A skill-challenge of *resisting an earworm* — the Fading's tune is colonizing the
valley, and the party. Humming it back is harmless and even useful (it calms the stained-glass
swarm); but *finishing* it, or learning its words, advances the dream's hold.
**Foes/DCs:** A repeated WIS save (DC [12], rising +1 each day in the valley) to keep the tune from
"completing" in your head. Performance to deliberately use it (calm the swarm, sing a fey to pause)
DC [13]. Insight/Arcana to realize it's *not yours* and was placed DC [15].
**Develops into:** Whoever lets the tune complete dreams the clearest version of the forest-dream and
wakes able to point its exact direction. (Arc A — the tune is the Tithe's homing-call; useful and
dangerous both.) **Wrenna** sings the missing last line if she's near; it is a child's name.

### 23. The Stone That Counts Wrong — *puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** Boundary-stones march along the old field-edge, each carved with its number, plain as
day: I, II, III, IIII, VI… You count the stones. There are the right number of stones. The numbers
on them are not. One count is missing, and the missing one is the same each time you check, and the
last stone in the row reads a numeral that has no business existing.
**Situation:** A counting-puzzle with a fey logic baked in — *one is always uncounted.* The party can
brute-force the discrepancy (there's a real, hidden marker-stone where the missing number "should be")
or recognize the rule (the same off-by-one that haunts the boot-prints and the Tithe-Warden's tally).
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation to find the buried/displaced stone bearing the missing numeral DC [15].
Arcana/Religion to *name* the rule — "something here always counts one more, or one fewer, than is
there" DC [16]. Solving it uncovers a cache beneath the missing stone.
**Develops into:** The cache holds the Glovewright's first proper note *and* a coin marked like the
Collector's ledger-seal — the brokers leave the same gaps. (Arc B + Arc E.) The off-by-one is the
same arithmetic by which the Tithe-Warden counts "one more than you packed." (Arc A — rule, planted.)

### 24. The Drover's Riddle-Toll — *puzzle/social · any tier · road · alt to Combat #5*
**Read-aloud:** The toll-men don't level their spears this time. The eldest just leans on his and
says, with the satisfaction of a man who's stumped a hundred travellers, that the King's price today
isn't coin — it's an answer. "A thing that's owed before it's borrowed," he says. "Name it, and the
road's yours free. Name it wrong, and you pay double, like everyone who grudges."
**Situation:** The King of the Road's toll as a riddle-gate. The "answer" the toll-men expect is a
clever turn of phrase; the answer the *campaign* wants is a theme (the Tithe — a debt that predates
the bargain). Right answer, free passage; wrong answer, the toll doubles and the milestones loop.
**Foes/DCs:** A flat INT/CHA (player wit) check, DC [15], OR let the table actually solve the riddle
(GM: the intended answer is "the Tithe" / "a debt owed before it's taken" / "the road itself"). A clever
*untold* answer (Performance DC [14]) delights them as much as the right one.
**Develops into:** Answer well and the eldest tips his hat: *"You'll do, on the green road. Most who
come through grudge the toll. The ones who pay glad — the King remembers them kindly."* (Feature ↔
Arc A — *give gladly* taught by the Road itself.) Wrong answer seeds the looping-milestones hazard for
the next road-hex.

# BOREAL

> *Green flat light under spruce, sound gone dead; warm den-mouths in frost-rimed deadfall.* The
> Wolf-Winter's pack runs through here, Cadoc's thinned Company hits the wall here, and the road has
> begun to *order itself into rows* as the boundary nears. Sprung traplines and warm-stewed empty
> cabins make every discovery uneasy. Foes lean beast/giant/fey: Worg, Werebear, Frost Giant, Treant.
> **Targets: Combat 7 · Social 4 · Discovery 5 · Hazard 4 · Puzzle 2 (22).**

## Combat (7)

### 1. The Sprung Trapline — *combat · tier 1–4*
**Read-aloud:** Someone's run a trapline through these spruce, and something has run it ahead of you —
every snare tripped, every catch gone, the wire licked clean of blood and fur. The dead-sound snow
swallows your footsteps so completely that the first growl seems to come from inside your own skull.
**Situation:** A worg or wolf pack has learned to follow the trapper's line and rob it, and now treats
*anyone* on the line as a competitor. They've been getting bolder; the trapper hasn't been seen in days.
**Foes/DCs:** `pack` — Wolf ×4–6, or `packLeader` Worg ×3 + Dire Wolf ×1. Survival to read the pack's
robbing-pattern and predict the next snare DC [12]. Sound carries strangely (disadvantage on hearing-based Perception).
**Develops into:** The line ends at a cold cabin (see Discovery #15). Among the robbed snares, one
holds something that wasn't caught by the trapper — a fey-silver arrowhead, still warm. (Arc A — **the
Huntsman**'s beast passed this way, "going home.")

### 2. Worgs at the Wall — *combat · tier 1–4*
**Read-aloud:** The drystone wall runs off into the trees in both directions, ancient and mossed and
far too long to be a field-boundary — and the worgs pacing its *far* side clearly regard it as a
border they are not yet willing to cross, which tells you the wall is keeping something in, or out.
**Situation:** Worgs harry the party along an old boundary-wall they instinctively won't pass — the
Wolf-Winter's edge. Cross the wall yourself and the worgs break off; press on and you've entered the
pack's true country. The wall is the first row in a landscape learning to order itself.
**Foes/DCs:** `skirmish` — Worg ×4, or with a Goblin Boss outrider (lurker). Animal Handling to read
the worgs' reluctance as fear of *something past the wall* DC [13]. Athletics to take the wall fast under harassment DC [12].
**Develops into:** Beyond the wall, prints in the snow: a man's boots, alone, gold-trimmed gear
discarded beside them. (Arc C — **Sir Cadoc Vane**, company already thinning, walked this line solo.)

### 3. The Werebear's Deadfall — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The deadfall den breathes out a warmth you can feel from twenty feet, steam curling
from the frost-rimed mouth of it, and the smell is bear and woodsmoke *both* — which is wrong, because
bears don't bank fires. Something inside shifts its great weight and says, in a voice far too human:
"Turn around. I won't ask twice, and I can't promise what asks third."
**Situation:** A werebear hermit guards this stretch — territorial, cursed, and barely in control near
the full moon. A fight risks triggering the worse thing it's holding back; a careful retreat (or the
parley in Social #12) is the wise read. It is not, at heart, the party's enemy.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Werebear ×1 (lurker; resistant to non-silvered). At tier 11–16, Werebear +
Werewolf ×2 as its "pack." Insight reads the warning as *protective, not predatory* DC [14]. Silvered
weapons or fire bypass its resistance.
**Develops into:** Spared or talked down, it growls a true thing: *"The cold's not natural this year.
Something walks at the head of the winter and it's calling the wolves home. Same road you're on."*
(Arc A — the Wolf-Winter as a tributary of the Tithe; the beasts are being collected too.)

### 4. The Giant Elk Stampede — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** You hear them before you see them — a drumming through the frozen ground, then the
whole white slope above you comes alive with antlers, a herd of giant elk in headlong panic, and
they are not turning aside for you because something behind them is far more frightening than you are.
**Situation:** Not a hunt — a *flight*. A herd of giant elk stampedes through the party's position,
driven by a predator (or a dread) at their backs. Survive the stampede, then face what spooked them.
**Foes/DCs:** Stampede: DEX save DC [14] to dodge clear, or be trampled (Giant Elk ×3–4 as moving
hazard). What follows: `ambush` Winter Wolf ×2 (lurker) or a Werewolf. Animal Handling to turn a single
elk aside and ride the panic out DC [15].
**Develops into:** Among the herd, one bull elk runs with its head *turned eastward* even mid-flight,
the way the leaning stones did. (Arc A — even the beasts are oriented toward the green.) The predator,
killed, has a fey-silver splinter lodged old in its hide. (Arc A — the Huntsman's shot, ricocheted through the food-chain.)

### 5. The Frost Giant's Cull — *combat · tier 11–16*
**Read-aloud:** The giant stands hip-deep in a snowdrift the size of a hill, methodically breaking the
ice off a frozen waterfall with a maul, and laid out behind it in the snow are wolves — dozens of
wolves, neatly, like a tithe of pelts. It turns its rimed beard toward you and says one word in the
giant-tongue that means, near enough: *"Late."*
**Situation:** A frost giant is *culling* the Wolf-Winter's pack — not for sport, but as a grim toll
of its own, paying down something. It may take the party for more of the wolves' allies, or for the
collectors it's been told to expect. Negotiation and battle are both on the table.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo`/`packLeader` — Frost Giant ×1, or + Winter Wolf ×3 (its hounds). At tier 17–20,
Frost Giant ×2 + Treant ×1. Giant or Persuasion to learn *why* it culls DC [16].
**Develops into:** *"The winter owes a count,"* it rumbles. *"I pay in wolves so the village pays in
none. You — you carry a debt that walks behind you."* (Arc A — the Tithe in a giant's dialect; Arc F
— it *sees* the One Who Follows on the ridge behind the party before they do.)

### 6. The Treant at the Ordered Wood — *combat · tier 11–16*
**Read-aloud:** The trees ahead grow in *rows*. Not a planted orchard's rows — wild spruce, ancient
and huge, standing in ranks as straight as a drilled regiment, aisles between them swept clear of
deadfall. At the head of the formation, one tree opens an eye in its bark, then another, and begins,
with a sound like a forest sighing, to step out of line.
**Situation:** The road ordering itself into rows, made literal and made *angry*. A treant marshals
this stretch of wood into rank as the boundary nears, and resents trespass on the formation. It can be
fought, but it would rather the party *fall in* — walk the aisles correctly and be passed through.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Treant ×1 (add Awakened-tree minions as it "wakes the ranks"). Nature to read
the rows as deliberate, not natural, DC [16]. Walking the formation in the *correct order* (a Survival/
Investigation puzzle, DC [15]) lets the party pass unfought — see Puzzle #21.
**Develops into:** The treant, fought or obeyed, intones: *"The wood remembers how to stand in line.
It is being called to order. So are you. The Warden comes to count the ranks."* (Arc A — **the
Tithe-Warden** is near; the landscape is forming up for inspection.)

### 7. The White Dragon Over the Pass — *combat · tier 17–20*
**Read-aloud:** The pass is the only way through the boreal wall, and the pass is a graveyard — frozen
travellers caught mid-stride, a gold-trimmed banner stiff as a board, everything glazed in a hand's-
thickness of clear blue ice. The cold deepens as you climb. Then the white slope above you unfolds
wings, and you understand the ice was not weather.
**Situation:** An adult white dragon has claimed the only viable pass through the boreal boundary,
icing over everyone who tries it — including, recently, members of the Gilded Company. The party must
pass; the dragon is the gate. The frozen banner is a clue and a warning.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Adult White Dragon ×1 (add Winter Wolf ×3 as its pack). Survival to find the
sheltered approach that avoids its breath line-of-sight DC [18]. The frozen Company banner can be
*used* (a Cadoc-signal) to parley if any of the Company live near.
**Develops into:** Among the frozen dead, one is not quite dead — flash-frozen but breathing slow,
smiling, in time. (Arc D — the Shared Dream's winter form; the fever freezes them smiling.) A
gold-trimmed survivor, thawed, gasps: *"Captain went on alone. Said he'd rather the wall took him
than ask for help."* (Arc C — Cadoc at his lowest, just ahead.)

## Social (4)

### 8. The Reindeer-Herder's Fire — *social · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The herder waves you toward his fire before you've asked, ladles out something hot and
grey and good, and lays down a single rule the way other men say grace: "You're welcome to the warmth.
You're welcome till morning. But you do not step past the firelight after dark. Not for any voice. Not
for any reason. It takes one. Always one."
**Situation:** Terrible hospitality, boreal-style. The herder shares everything freely — *except* he
needs the party to honour his rule, because the dark beyond the fire genuinely takes one who steps out.
The night will test it: a voice will call from the trees in a companion's voice.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight reads the rule as deadly-earnest, not superstition, DC [12]. At night, a WIS save
DC [14] to ignore the calling voice (it mimics someone the party lost). Whoever steps out is *gone* unless
rescued before three rounds (Survival to track in the dark, DC [16]).
**Develops into:** *"Up north the fog does the same, only worse — counts you, takes the count it's
owed."* (Arc A / Arc D — **the Reindeer-Herder** names the Tithe in its colder dialect; foreshadows the
tundra Fog-walker.) Honour his rule and he gifts a "fire-charm" the Tithe-Warden later counts as generosity given. (Arc A.)

### 9. Cadoc at the Wall — *social · tier 11–16 · Arc C centrepiece*
**Read-aloud:** You almost don't recognize him. The gold trim's gone dull, half his company isn't with
him, and the smirk you'd have known him by anywhere is simply absent. Sir Cadoc Vane looks at you
across the dead-sound snow for a long moment, and then he says the words you'd have bet your life he'd
die before saying: "There's a thing up ahead that doesn't care how fine your gear is. I'd take help."
**Situation:** The turn of Arc C. Cadoc, thinned and humbled, asks the party for aid against the
boreal wall (the dragon, the Wolf-Winter, the cold itself). How the party answer — mock, refuse, ally,
exploit — sets the Company's banner-count for the finale.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight reads that the ask is genuine and *costs him everything* to make DC [13].
Persuasion to learn what broke him here gets the dragon, the wall, and his pride, in that order DC [14].
**Develops into:** Help him and his sister appears (Social #10). Mock or refuse him and he defects,
biomes later, to whoever opposes the party — and becomes a candidate for the One Who Follows. (Arc C →
Arc F.) He mentions a debt to "a glove-handed woman who's calling it in." (Arc B — **the Glovewright**.)

### 10. Quartermaster Ilse's Bargain — *social · tier 11–16 · Arc C*
**Read-aloud:** She finds you while her brother sleeps. Ilse Vane has none of Cadoc's swagger and all
of the Company's actual brains, and she lays it out flat, the way a quartermaster counts stores: the
Company's maps, the Company's muscle, all of it, for the forest road ahead — on one condition, and the
condition is that you help her get Cadoc out of here alive, "even the parts of him that don't want to be."
**Situation:** The clearer-eyed Vane offers the party an alliance for the threshold — the Company's
maps and war-band — in exchange for help saving Cadoc from his own pride. A grateful Company is huge at
the forest's edge (Arc A's bargain-by-force ending); a spurned Ilse is a vengeful enemy.
**Foes/DCs:** Persuasion/Insight to negotiate terms that survive Cadoc's ego DC [15]. She'll trade
*one* Company secret to sweeten it (Persuasion DC [14]) — the location of the Cooling Forge that "Cadoc
already tried to loot." (Arc C ↔ regional volcanic.)
**Develops into:** Deal struck, the Company's banners are the party's to call at the threshold. (Arc A
— war-band ending unlocked.) Ilse, if the party prove reliable, becomes a recurring ally; if betrayed,
she blames them for whatever befalls her brother. (Arc C / Arc F.)

### 11. The Trapper's Widow — *social · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The cabin's warm and the stew's still on, but the woman who opens the door is dressed
for the cold and carrying everything she owns on her back. "He didn't come back off the line," she says,
before you've spoken. "Three days. I'm not waiting for the fourth. You're going on east, aren't you?
Everyone is, lately. Even the dead, I think. Even the wolves."
**Situation:** The trapper's widow, abandoning her home to join the eastward drift she can't explain.
She'll trade the trapper's gear, his maps, and his fey-silver find for an escort to the next holding —
or simply for someone to confirm she's not mad to go.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight reads grief curdled into the same compulsion that empties this whole biome DC [12].
Persuasion to learn what she found in his effects (Survival #1's arrowhead, and a map marked with rows) DC [13].
**Develops into:** Her husband's last map shows the wood drawn in *rows* he sketched in his final week,
baffled. (Arc A — even a trapper noticed the land ordering itself.) She speaks of "a humming, like a
tune everyone half-knows" carried up from the south. (Arc A / Arc D — the Fading's tune, travelled north.)

## Discovery (5)

### 12. The Warm-Stewed Empty Cabin — *discovery · any tier · boreal signature*
**Read-aloud:** The cabin door's unbarred, the hearth's banked low but live, and there's a pot of stew
still faintly warm on the hook. Two bowls are set. Two stools are pushed back from the table as if their
owners rose mid-meal a quarter-hour ago. The dead-sound snow outside holds no tracks leaving — only,
faintly, a single set arriving, that does not match either bowl.
**Situation:** The boreal signature discovery — a home left *mid-life*, no violence, no exit-tracks. The
arriving-only footprints are the tell. The truth is GM-seatable (they walked east; the cold took them;
the One Who Follows visited), but the unease is the point.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation reads "left mid-meal, unhurried" and finds the mismatched arriving prints
DC [13]. Survival ages the bank-fire to "a quarter-hour, impossibly — it should be cold" DC [14].
Following the single arriving track leads *in circles* back to the door (Survival DC [13]).
**Develops into:** On the table, between the two bowls, a third place has been *set since* — clean
spoon, no plate, laid by whoever arrived. (Arc F — **the One Who Follows** keeps the party's customs,
wrong and intimate.) The stew, eaten, tastes faintly of the southern dream. (Arc D.)

### 13. The Den-Mouth That Breathes Warm — *discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** A breath of summer-warm air sighs out of the frost-rimed deadfall, steady as a
sleeping thing, melting a clean dark hollow in the snow around the den-mouth. No animal smell. No bones.
Just warmth, rising from a dark you can't see the bottom of, and — if you hold very still — the faint
sound of something far below, breathing in time with the air.
**Situation:** A den-mouth venting unnatural warmth from deep underground — too deep, too clean for a
beast. It connects (the GM decides how literally) to the same "something mines below" running under the
country. Descending is a delve-hook; the warmth alone is the discovery.
**Foes/DCs:** Nature to confirm the warmth is *wrong* — geothermal where there's no volcanism, or worse
DC [14]. Survival to feel the "breathing" rhythm in the airflow DC [15]. Descending wakes whatever
breathes (GM-seatable — Troll lair, or a tunnel that shouldn't exist).
**Develops into:** Scratched at the den-mouth, fresh: a row of tally-marks, the party's number, plus
one. (Arc F.) The warm air carries, faintly, the southern tune and the green smell. (Arc A — the forest
"breathing" up through the country's hollows.)

### 14. The Cold Cabin at the Line's End — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The trapline ends here, at a cabin gone properly cold — hearth dead, frost on the inside
of the windows, the door swinging. The trapper's snowshoes are still on their pegs. His coat's still on
its hook. Whatever took him off the line, he didn't dress for it, and he didn't come back to dress at all.
**Situation:** The end of Combat #1's trapline — the trapper's home, abandoned *without his cold-weather
gear*, which in the boreal means he didn't choose to leave. A grimmer cousin of the warm-stewed cabin:
this one's been empty long enough to freeze.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation reconstructs "left in a hurry, undressed, days ago" DC [13]. The trapper's
journal (Investigation to find, DC [12]) details the wood "growing crooked — the trees standing in lines
that weren't there last season." Survival finds his final track leading east, bootless. (Arc A.)
**Develops into:** His journal's last legible line: *"Followed the silver arrow's shine east. The deer
all face that way now. So do I, God help me."* (Arc A — the Huntsman's arrow and the eastward pull,
witnessed by a doomed local.) His fey-silver find is here if not already taken from the trapline.

### 15. The Rows in the Snow — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** From the ridge you can see it whole, and you wish you couldn't: the forest below is
growing in rows. Long aisles of ancient spruce run dead-straight to the horizon, swept clear between,
ranked like a thing assembling itself for inspection — and from up here you can see the rows all *bend*,
faintly, toward a single point in the deep east where the green goes dark.
**Situation:** The campaign's "road ordering itself into rows" rendered as landscape — visible only from
height, unmistakable once seen. Extends `BIOME_THEMES.site`. The convergence-point on the horizon is the
forest threshold. This is the boreal's single most diagnostic discovery.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival/Investigation from the ridge to confirm the rows are real and converging DC [14].
Nature to know "spruce do not grow like this; nothing makes a wild wood form ranks" DC [15]. Tracing the
bend with a map fixes the threshold's bearing.
**Develops into:** Whoever stares too long at the convergence-point hears the tune complete in their
head, and can thereafter point the way unerringly. (Arc A — the Tithe's homing-call, seen instead of
heard.) That night, **the Tithe-Warden** falls into step on the road below. (Arc A — Beat 3 trigger.)

### 16. The Banner in the Ice — *discovery · any tier · Arc C*
**Read-aloud:** Frozen upright into the snow, stiff as a signpost, a war-banner — gold thread on green,
a sigil you'll come to know: the Gilded Company. The pole's been driven deep and deliberate, a marker,
not a casualty. Lashed to it, ice-glazed, a sealed dispatch-case and a single dropped gauntlet, gold-trimmed.
**Situation:** A marker the Gilded Company left — a warning to their own, or a boundary they didn't dare
cross. The dispatch-case is intelligence; the gauntlet is Cadoc's, dropped (or shed). The party can read
the Company's plans and their fraying nerve.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation to open the frozen case without ruining the papers DC [13]. The dispatch
details the Company's intent to loot something ahead and their dwindling numbers. History/Insight reads
the planted banner as *"do not follow" — left by people who'd lost too many.* DC [14].
**Develops into:** The dispatch names the Cooling Forge as the Company's true target and shows their roster
halved. (Arc C ↔ regional volcanic — Cadoc's looting plan, in writing.) A note in Ilse's hand, unsent:
*"Brother, the glove-woman's note came due. We can't pay her and the forest both."* (Arc B + Arc C.)

## Hazard (4)

### 17. The Whiteout — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The light goes first — the flat green-grey under the spruce simply *thickens* until the
trees are suggestions and your own outstretched hand is a guess. Then the dead-sound snow eats every
noise but your blood in your ears, and you realize you can no longer tell which way is back, or whether
the shape ten feet off is a companion, a stump, or something that has been waiting for exactly this.
**Situation:** A boreal whiteout — navigation and cohesion hazard. The party risk separating, circling,
and freezing. Worse: the dead-sound silence means a predator (or the follower) can close unheard. Roping
up and holding still are the survival plays.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival to hold a true bearing through it DC [15] (fail = lost, circling east). CON save
vs. exposure each hour DC [13]. Roping the party together (Survival DC [10]) prevents separation.
Perception to hear a threat closing is at disadvantage (dead-sound snow).
**Develops into:** When it clears, there are tracks among the party's own that no one made — boots,
their number, plus one, walking inside the whiteout with them the whole time. (Arc F.) If a companion
was lost and recovered, they're smiling, and humming. (Arc D.)

### 18. The Ice That Sounds Hollow — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The frozen tarn is the obvious crossing — flat, white, a straight line to the far
treeline that saves an hour's floundering through drifts. Three steps out, the ice beneath your boot
makes a sound. Not a crack. A *knock*, from below, like a knuckle on the underside of the world, and
then — politely, patiently — it knocks again, somewhere ahead of you.
**Situation:** A frozen lake-crossing that's thinner than it looks and has *something under it* — a
trapped beast, drowned dead, or worse — that knocks. The hazard is the ice (it can give); the dread is
what knocks. Crossing fast, light, and spread out is correct; investigating the knock is a choice.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival to read safe ice-thickness and route DC [14]. DEX save if ice gives DC [15];
cold-water immersion = CON save DC [15] + exhaustion. The knocking source (GM-seatable — Winter Wolf
denned beneath, or a drowned thing) reacts if the ice breaks.
**Develops into:** Cut through, what knocks is — a hand, blue and unhurried, pressed to the underside
of the ice, knocking the rhythm of the southern tune. (Arc D / Arc A — a frozen dreamer under the lake,
keeping time.) Pulled out, they breathe, and smile, and will not wake.

### 19. The Cold That Calls the Wolves Home — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The temperature drops between one breath and the next, hard enough to crack the sap in
the trees like distant gunfire, and from every direction at once the wolves begin to *sing* — not
hunting-howls but something mournful and obedient, all of them, near and far, turning the same way and
moving east. The cold is herding them. And you are standing in the path.
**Situation:** A supernatural cold-front — the Wolf-Winter "calling the pack home" to the forest — that
sweeps the party up in the migration. The hazard is the killing cold *and* being caught in a moving river
of wolves who aren't hunting but won't go around you. Shelter, or move *with* them.
**Foes/DCs:** CON save vs. the snap-cold DC [15] (fail = exhaustion). Survival to find shelter before it
peaks DC [14]. Animal Handling to move *with* the migrating wolves unmolested (they're not hunting) DC [15]
— fighting them is possible but pointless and endless.
**Develops into:** The wolves stream past and around, all facing east, paying their own summons. (Arc A
— the beasts are being collected, the Wolf-Winter is a tributary of the Tithe.) One old wolf breaks the
column to look back at the party — and at the ridge behind them, where a figure stands, raising one hand. (Arc F.)

### 20. The Deadfall Maze — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The storm-felled spruce lie crosshatched for half a mile, a giant's game of jackstraws
twenty feet deep, frost-slick and groaning, every trunk a balance-beam over a tangle of broken branches
below. There's no going around before dark. The warm den-mouths breathing up through the gaps suggest
you are not the only thing using this maze for cover.
**Situation:** A vast frozen deadfall — a terrain hazard that's slow, treacherous, and ambush-friendly.
Crossing risks falls onto the spike-tangle below; the warm den-gaps mean predators den here. Speed
trades against safety. A classic boreal slog with teeth.
**Foes/DCs:** Athletics/Acrobatics to cross the slick trunks DC [14] (fail = fall, [2d6] onto broken
branches). Survival to find the soundest route DC [13]. The den-gaps may vent an `ambush` (Worg ×2 or a
Giant Spider, lurker) onto the party mid-traverse.
**Develops into:** Wedged deep in the jackstraws, gold-trimmed and frozen: a Company scout who fell and
wasn't recovered. (Arc C — the cost of Cadoc's race, left in the deadfall.) His grip still holds a map
sketched with the converging rows. (Arc A.)

## Puzzle (2)

### 21. Walking the Ranks — *puzzle · tier 11–16 · alt to Combat #6*
**Read-aloud:** The treant has not attacked. It waits at the head of its ranked wood like a sergeant at
inspection, and the meaning is plain even before it speaks: there is a *correct* way through the
formation, an order to the aisles, and you are expected to find it and fall in. Step wrong, and the
ranks will close. Step right, and the wood will let its expected guests pass.
**Situation:** The "road ordering itself" as a literal traversal-puzzle. The ranked spruce form a maze
with a fey logic — the correct path follows a *counting rule* (the same off-by-one as the Tithe: always
take the row one fewer than instinct says; or pace each aisle in the order the trees "woke"). Solve it,
pass unfought; fail, and the treant (Combat #6) animates the ranks.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation to spot the wakening-order of the trees (eye-bark opens in sequence) DC [15].
Nature/Arcana to deduce the counting rule DC [16]. Each correct turn earns a sigh from the wood; each
wrong turn, a branch-creak of warning (three strikes = combat).
**Develops into:** Walk the ranks correctly and the treant intones: *"You count well. The Warden will be
pleased — or troubled. Most cannot fall in line." * (Arc A — passing the rite primes a friendlier
Tithe-Warden meeting; the off-by-one rule is the *same* one that counts "one more than you packed.")

### 22. The Trapper's Cipher-Notches — *puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The trapline isn't just wire and snares — every tenth tree wears a cluster of fresh
axe-notches, and they aren't blazes marking the path. They're a *tally*, a working-out, the same marks
repeated and crossed and re-counted, growing frantic toward the line's end, as if a man were trying to
make a sum come out right and the sum kept refusing.
**Situation:** The dead trapper's notch-cipher — a counting-puzzle left in bark. He was tallying his
catch and the tally never matched: *something always took one more than the snares could account for.*
Decoding it reveals the boreal's quiet horror (the land's off-by-one) and points to his fate and his cache.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation to read the notch-system as a tally, not blazes, DC [13]. Solving the
arithmetic — *the catch is always short by exactly one, every count* — is an INT check or table puzzle
DC [15]. The frantic final cluster marks the tree where his cache (and last note) is hidden.
**Develops into:** The cache holds the fey-silver arrowhead (if not yet found), the converging-rows map,
and a final notch that simply reads *+1*. (Arc A — the trapper independently discovered the Tithe's
arithmetic; Arc F — *something* was always taking the extra, and now walks behind the party.)

# JUNGLE

> *Everything breathes; the canopy drips with no rain.* The Green That Remembers feeds a god here, the
> road's ordering becomes overgrown stepped temples, and **the Tithe-Warden** walks the boundary close.
> Highest discovery + hazard mix in the bible — stepped temples, sinkhole pools, things that are almost
> words. Foes: dinosaurs and serpents — Velociraptor, Yuan-ti, Giant Ape, Tyrannosaurus.
> **Targets: Combat 8 · Social 4 · Discovery 6 · Hazard 5 · Puzzle 3 (26).**

## Combat (8)

### 1. The Raptor Pack in the Undergrowth — *combat · tier 1–4*
**Read-aloud:** The jungle's constant breathing-sound changes — not louder, but *organized*, a series of
clicks passed left to right and back through the green wall around you, the way a sentence is passed
between speakers. You have just enough time to understand you are being talked about before the ferns
on three sides explode.
**Situation:** A velociraptor pack hunting with eerie coordination — the jungle's signature ambush.
They've been pacing the party for an hour, communicating. They cut off retreat and test for the weak.
Pure predator, but the *talking* clicks hint at the biome's "almost-words" theme.
**Foes/DCs:** `pack` — Velociraptor ×4–6, or `ambush` Velociraptor ×3 + Giant Poisonous Snake ×1
(lurker). Survival to hear the coordinating clicks and predict the flanks DC [13]. Perception in the
dense green is at disadvantage beyond 20 ft.
**Develops into:** One raptor wears a band of woven vine and bone around its neck — *collared*, by
something with hands. (Jungle regional — the Yuan-ti, or the Green That Remembers, herds the beasts.)
The clicks, recorded by a clever ear, resolve almost into a word. (Arc A — the jungle "almost speaking.")

### 2. The Collared Serpent-Folk — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** They step out of the green without disturbing a leaf — three of them, scaled and
sinuous, and the fourth that follows is wrong above the shoulders in a way your eye keeps sliding off.
The foremost inclines its head with a courtier's grace and hisses something in a tongue that is
*almost* common, and the only word you catch, clear as a struck bell, is: "Expected."
**Situation:** Yuan-ti enforcing the boundary of a temple-precinct, in the fey-courtesy register the
whole forest road speaks. They'd rather the party turn back or *tithe* than fight — but they will
defend the precinct. Combat or the Social #9 parley both live here.
**Foes/DCs:** `mixed` — Yuan-ti Pureblood ×2 + Constrictor Snake ×2, with a Yuan-ti Malison (lurker).
At tier 11–16, Yuan-ti Malison ×3 + Giant Constrictor. Persuasion in the formal register DC [15];
Insight to catch that "expected" is a *fey* word, not a threat, DC [14].
**Develops into:** Their precinct guards a stepped temple where the Green is being *fed*. (Jungle
regional — the Green That Remembers.) Their leader says the party are "on the route the Warden
confirmed." (Arc A — **the Tithe-Warden** is expected here; the Yuan-ti serve the same ledger.)

### 3. The Giant Ape's Grief — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The roar comes from above and the canopy *rains* — torn branches, whole boughs, a
hammer-blow of foliage — and then the ape itself drops into the clearing, vast and silver-backed and
beating its chest over something small and broken cradled in one enormous hand. It has been wronged. It
has decided you are who wronged it. It is not interested in your version.
**Situation:** A giant ape enraged by a *prior* harm (poachers, a raptor pack, the collaring) and now
blaming the party. A fight is winnable but tragic; calming or redirecting it (it grieves a dead infant)
is the better read and earns an unlikely ally.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Giant Ape ×1. Animal Handling to redirect its grief toward the *real* culprits
DC [16]. Showing it the collar from Combat #1 (Persuasion-as-gesture, DC [14]) turns its rage outward.
Insight reads grief, not predation, DC [13].
**Develops into:** Calmed and shown the true enemy, the ape *leads* the party to the poachers' camp — or
the Yuan-ti precinct — and fights at their side. (Jungle regional ally.) Around the dead infant's neck:
a tuft of fey-silver, the same as the Huntsman's arrow. (Arc A — the Hunt's beast passed through, bleeding luminous ichor the locals harvest.)

### 4. The Allosaurus on the Game-Trail — *combat · tier 1–4 (deadly) / 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The game-trail is the only fast way through, and the game-trail has a king. You smell it
before the trees thin — carrion and hot reptile — and then it's simply *there*, filling the trail,
close enough that you can see the old scars and the fresh blood and the unbothered, accountant's
patience with which it decides whether you are worth the chase.
**Situation:** An apex predator owning a chokepoint the party need. Not coordinated like the raptors —
just enormous, hungry, and territorial. The party can fight, lure it off with bait, or find the longer
way through the canopy. A test of whether they spend resources or cleverness.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Allosaurus ×1 (Giant Boar ×1 as scavenger-companion). At higher tier, swap to
Giant Crocodile in a stream-crossing. Survival to find/bait it off the trail DC [14]. Stealth to slip
past while it feeds DC [15].
**Develops into:** Its territory is dotted with kill-caches, and one cache holds a half-eaten traveller
in pilgrim-grey, a child's shoe still on its cord. (Arc A / Arc F — another pilgrim who didn't make it,
mirroring **Wrenna**'s possible fate, jungle-edition.)

### 5. Tyrannosaurus at the Temple Steps — *combat · tier 11–16*
**Read-aloud:** The stepped temple rises out of the green ahead, ancient and root-cracked and *humming*
faintly — and curled at its base, basking on the sun-warmed lowest tier like a cat on a hearth, is the
single largest living thing any of you has seen. It opens one eye the size of a shield. The hum from the
temple deepens, as if the stone itself has noticed you too.
**Situation:** A tyrannosaurus has made the temple's base its lair — but the temple *wants* the party
in (the hum responds to them), and the dinosaur is the obstacle between them and the Green's heart. Fight
the apex, or find the route the temple's puzzle (Puzzle #21) opens around it.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Tyrannosaurus Rex ×1 (add Velociraptor ×3 as scavengers). Survival to spot the
temple's side-route around the basking beast DC [16]. The temple-hum can be *used* — a Performance of the
southern tune (DC [15]) makes the T-Rex uneasy and sluggish (the tune unsettles even it).
**Develops into:** The temple is where the Green That Remembers is fed — and the hum is the southern
Fading-tune, arrived ahead of the party. (Arc A — the jungle is on the homing-call too.) **The
Tithe-Warden** has carved a fresh tally into the temple's lowest step. (Arc A — Beat 3.)

### 6. The Couatl's Test — *combat · tier 11–16*
**Read-aloud:** It uncoils out of a shaft of green light like the light itself learning to fly —
feathered, serpentine, impossibly beautiful — and it does not attack. It *circles*, slow and grave,
and where its shadow passes over you it feels like being read. When it finally speaks, into all your
minds at once, it asks not who you are but what you are *carrying*, and it already knows the answer.
**Situation:** A couatl guardian that fights only if the party *fail its test* — it's weighing what they
carry (the dream, the debts, the fey-silver) and whether they mean to give or take at the forest. A
"combat" that's really a moral gate; violence is the failure-state, not the goal.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Couatl ×1 (lurker; it'd rather not fight). Persuasion/Insight to pass its
weighing — *demonstrate intent to give gladly* DC [16]. Failing the test (or attacking) triggers combat;
it shields and rebukes rather than kills.
**Develops into:** Pass, and it blesses the party with a true warning: *"You carry the forest's debt and
one carries more than the rest. Give it gladly, or the Warden takes it doubled."* (Arc A — a benevolent
confirmation of the Tithe; it can name **the One Who Follows** if pressed.) Fail, and it marks them, and
the next fey meeting is colder.

### 7. The Green Dragon of the Deep Canopy — *combat · tier 17–20*
**Read-aloud:** The jungle's breathing stops. In the sudden, total silence you hear water dripping from
ten thousand leaves and, beneath it, a vast slow inhalation that is not the jungle's — it is something
*in* the jungle, breathing for it, and the canopy-light goes sick-green as a shape the size of the
clearing settles its coils and regards you with the bored, ancient amusement of a thing that has eaten pilgrims before.
**Situation:** An adult green dragon that has half-merged its identity with the Green That Remembers —
it speaks for the jungle-god as much as for itself, and it gatekeeps the deepest route toward the forest.
Bargain in fey-courtesy, fight, or feed it a secret; it collects either way.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Adult Green Dragon ×1 (add Yuan-ti Malison ×2 as cultist-servitors). Persuasion/
Deception in fey-register DC [18]; Insight to tell where the dragon ends and the Green begins DC [19].
**Develops into:** *"The forest and I keep the same ledger now,"* it muses. *"I ate a Warden once. It
counted me anyway. You'll learn — everything pays."* (Arc A — the Green as a tributary deity of the
Tithe; even the dragon is on the ledger.)

### 8. The Stampede of the Spooked Herd — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The ground takes up the jungle's breathing and turns it into a drumbeat — closer,
heavier — and then the green wall bursts and a herd of panicked herbivores pours through, triceratops
and lesser beasts shoulder to shoulder, eyes white, and behind their dust something is driving them that
has made the entire jungle decide, all at once, to run the same way: *east.*
**Situation:** A jungle stampede driven by a dread (or a predator, or the Green's summons) — the party
caught in a flood of fleeing dinosaurs all oriented eastward. Survive the trample, then reckon with what
drives them. Echoes the boreal elk-flight; the eastward orientation is the through-line.
**Foes/DCs:** Stampede: DEX save DC [15] to dodge (Triceratops ×2 + lesser beasts as moving hazard).
The driver behind: `ambush` Allosaurus ×1 or Yuan-ti raiders. Animal Handling to ride the flow out safely DC [15].
**Develops into:** Every beast in the flood runs east, toward the green-dark horizon, the same bearing as
the boreal rows and the leaning stones. (Arc A — the whole living country orienting toward the Tithe.)
A collared raptor runs with them, its vine-and-bone band snapped. (Jungle regional — something's grip is slipping.)

## Social (4)

### 9. The Serpent-Court's Courtesy — *social · tier 5–10 · alt to Combat #2*
**Read-aloud:** They receive you the way a fading aristocracy receives unexpected but not unwelcome
guests — with immaculate, dangerous courtesy, a coil of attendants, and a foremost speaker whose
almost-common hiss shapes itself, with visible effort, into words you can follow. "You are *expected*,"
it says again, and gestures you toward the temple steps as if to a dinner you cannot decline.
**Situation:** The Yuan-ti as hosts rather than guards. They serve the Green (and, beyond it, the same
ledger the forest runs on) and will let the party deeper *if* they observe the courtesy — bring a gift,
speak in the giving register, do not refuse the offered cup. Grudge anything and the courtesy curdles.
**Foes/DCs:** Persuasion in formal register DC [15]; Religion/Nature to know the temple-etiquette (what
gift, what cup, what *not* to take) DC [14]. Refusing hospitality or haggling is the failure-state —
"grudging payment costs double."
**Develops into:** Honoured, they grant passage and a truth: *"The Warden came before you and confirmed
your count. We feed the Green so the Green feeds the forest. You carry its debt — pay it as we do, gladly,
or be paid." * (Arc A — Yuan-ti as Tithe-servitors; **Tithe-Warden** named.) They know the southern
fever "is the Green reaching downstream." (Arc D ↔ Arc A.)

### 10. The Parts-Buyer's Wagon — *social/trade · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** Improbably, a wagon — drawn by a placid, blinkered allosaurus, of all things — sits at a
trail-junction, and the wiry merchant atop it is buying, not selling. He runs a practiced eye over your
kit and your kills and names, without preamble, the one thing he wants above all: *"Luminous ichor. Glowing
beast-blood. Antler-tine that hums. Premium coin. You find any, you find me."*
**Situation:** A roadside parts-buyer (per `MerchantEconomy`) whose shopping list is *the Hunt, two
biomes early*. He doesn't know what he's foreshadowing; the party will. He carries one rumour and one
hook, and his standing order is a thread the GM can pull all the way to the forest.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight to clock that his "shopping list" describes a single specific creature DC [14].
Persuasion gets his best rumour free (DC [13]); a sample of luminous ichor gets his *whole* story.
**Develops into:** *"Fella up north shot something that bleeds light. Fey-silver arrow. Folk say it's
walking home and won't lie down."* (Arc A — **the Huntsman** and the beast, named by a merchant who just
wants the parts.) He'll trade a "hazel-charm against thirst" he bought off a green-eyed woman. (Arc A — **Green-Eyed Sister**.)

### 11. The Mad Cartographer — *social · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** He's lashed himself to a tree to keep from walking east in his sleep, and he's covered
every inch of bark within reach in maps — the same map, over and over, each one showing the jungle's
trails *re-routing* themselves toward a single point. "You see it too?" he says, wild-eyed and almost
laughing. "The paths move. Every night they point a little more *that* way. I'm mapping a country that's
making up its mind."
**Situation:** A surveyor driven half-mad by the jungle "ordering itself" — the road-rows theme, gone
labyrinthine in the green. He has genuinely useful (if frantic) intelligence about the shifting routes,
and he's terrified of the eastward pull he feels in his own legs. Help him, exploit him, or free him.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight to separate true observation from raving DC [13] (most of it is true). Persuasion
to calm him enough to share his best map DC [12]. Medicine to recognize the early-stage dream-compulsion
in him DC [14].
**Develops into:** His maps, collated, reveal the trails converging on the temple of the Green — and,
beyond it, the forest threshold. (Arc A — the route, mapped by a doomed man.) If left lashed to his tree,
he's gone by morning, the rope gnawed through from his side, footprints heading east. (Arc D / Arc F.)

### 12. The Glovewright's Failing Ward — *social · any tier · Arc B + Arc D thread-touch*
**Read-aloud:** Strung between two strangler-figs at the edge of a clearing where the green has gone
*wrong* — leaves blackening inward, the breathing-sound turned to a wet rattle — hangs a line of charms:
salt-knots, iron, woven hair, a single child-sized glove pinned at the center, all of it old and weathering
and visibly *losing*. A woman crouches mending it with shaking hands, far older than her quick movements,
and she does not turn around. "Don't cross the line," she says. "I've held it eleven years. I'd rather not
start again because of you."
**Situation:** First in-person brush with the Quiet Hand, deep in the jungle — the Glovewright herself,
holding a failing ward against a pocket of the Spreading (the Green's rot, reaching). She's spent a
fortune and a lifetime on lines like this; she's tired, and she's been *expecting* the party, because she
intends to ask them something at the forest. For now: help mend the ward, or don't.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight reads her exhaustion as *years* of paying for something, not a day's work, DC [14].
Religion/Arcana to help re-knot the ward correctly DC [15] (success steadies the line a season). Persuasion
to learn what she'll ask gets only: *"Not yet. Soon. You'll want to say yes." *
**Develops into:** The pinned glove is the Quiet Hand's mark — the one the party have seen, or will see, on
a corpse, a clerk, a king. (Arc B — **the Glovewright**, face-to-face; her wards gate the Spreading.) She
knows the southern fever "by its first name" and that "the Green is one of its mouths." (Arc D ↔ Arc A.) Help
her gladly and she remembers it when she calls in her favour at the threshold. (Arc B payoff seed.)

## Discovery (6)

### 13. The Canopy That Drips Without Rain — *discovery · any tier · jungle signature*
**Read-aloud:** It is not raining. The sky, what you can see of it, is hard and bright. And yet the
canopy drips — a steady, patient *tock… tock… tock* of warm water off ten thousand leaves, onto your
shoulders, into the ferns, a whole forest weeping clear and constant under a cloudless sky. When you cup
a hand to catch it, it tastes faintly of green growing things and, underneath, of dream.
**Situation:** The jungle's signature discovery and unease — the forest breathes and *transpires* on a
scale that shouldn't be possible, dripping its own moisture. The drip carries a trace of the Shared
Dream (the Green reaching downstream). Beautiful, wrong, diagnostic.
**Foes/DCs:** Nature to confirm the transpiration is "alive past reason — the jungle is breathing water"
DC [14]. Medicine to detect the dream-trace in the drip DC [15]. Drinking it: CON save DC [12] or take a
faint version of the dream that night.
**Develops into:** The drip pools and runs *east*, downhill, toward a sinkhole — and follows the same
watercourse logic that carries the fever to far villages. (Arc D ↔ Arc A — the jungle is the dream's
green wellspring; "the Spreading" seen as living canopy.)

### 14. The Stepped Temple, Half-Swallowed — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The green parts and there it is: a ziggurat of black root-cracked stone, its tiers
furred with moss and strangler-fig, swallowed to the shoulders in jungle — and *humming*, a low sound
you feel in your back teeth, rising and falling like slow breath. Carvings march up its face: a procession
of figures, each carrying something, climbing toward a door at the summit that is carved as an open forest.
**Situation:** The jungle's central site — a temple to the Green That Remembers, where the procession-
carvings tell the whole Tithe in stone (figures *giving* climb to the open-forest door; figures who *clutch*
their burdens are shown falling). Extends `BIOME_THEMES.site`. The hum is the Fading-tune. A puzzle (Puzzle #21) gates the summit.
**Foes/DCs:** History/Religion to read the procession-frieze as a *bargain* (give to ascend, hoard to
fall) DC [15]. Investigation finds the fresh tally-marks the Tithe-Warden left on the lowest step DC [13].
The hum can be matched (Performance DC [14]) to "answer" the temple.
**Develops into:** The frieze *is* Arc A, carved centuries early: *give gladly, climb; grudge, and fall.*
(Arc A — the Tithe's thesis in stone.) The summit door, opened (Puzzle #21), shows the forest — and one
figure already standing in it, with the party's number plus one. (Arc F.)

### 15. The Sinkhole Pool — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The jungle floor simply ends — a near-perfect circular shaft dropping into the dark, its
walls hung with roots like a green throat, and far below, the gleam of still black water. Things grow
down there that don't grow up here: pale, eyeless, patient. And the constant breathing-sound of the
jungle, you realize, is *loudest* here, welling up out of the hole like the forest's own slow exhalation.
**Situation:** A cenote-style sinkhole — a vertical discovery/delve-hook into the jungle's underdark
plumbing, where the Green's roots drink and the dream-water pools. Descending is dangerous; the rim alone
yields lore. The breathing is loudest here because this is a *lung*.
**Foes/DCs:** Athletics/climber's-kit to descend the root-walls DC [15]. Nature to read the pale growth
as fed by "something below, not the sun" DC [14]. Perception to spot offerings cast into the pool —
recent, deliberate (DC [13]).
**Develops into:** The offerings in the pool are *travellers' goods* — packs, boots, a child's shoe —
cast in by pilgrims paying the Green ahead of the forest. (Arc A — the Tithe collected early, by the
willing.) The water tastes of the southern dream, concentrated. (Arc D — the wellspring.)

### 16. The Things That Are Almost Words — *discovery · any tier · jungle signature*
**Read-aloud:** You've been hearing it for an hour and telling yourself it's birds. It is not birds. The
jungle's breathing has shape tonight — rising and falling in cadences that land just short of language,
a sentence forever about to resolve, spoken by the canopy and the insects and the dripping leaves all at
once, and the longer you listen the more certain you become that the forest is *trying to say your names.*
**Situation:** The jungle's eeriest discovery — the ambient sound organizing into near-speech. The Green
That Remembers is learning to talk, and what it's assembling toward is recognition of the party
specifically. Recording, transcribing, or answering it are all options, all slightly dangerous.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight/Perception to confirm "this is structured — it's almost words" DC [14]. Arcana to
recognize the cadence as the *same* as the southern tune, given a vocabulary DC [16]. Answering it aloud
(Performance/Persuasion DC [15]) gets a clearer "reply" — and the Green's attention.
**Develops into:** Transcribed and parsed, the almost-words are a single repeated phrase: *give, or be
given.* (Arc A — the Tithe, spoken by the forest itself, two biomes early.) That night, **the
Tithe-Warden** falls into step, and finishes the sentence the jungle started. (Arc A — Beat 3.)

### 17. The Poachers' Abandoned Camp — *discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** Someone got here first and left in a hurry. The camp's intact — hammocks, a smoke-rack
hung with curing meat, crates stencilled with a gold-trimmed sigil — but the cook-fire's dead, a rifle
lies dropped in the mud, and the curing-rack's prize specimen has been *taken back*, the lashings torn,
a smear of luminous, faintly glowing blood trailing off into the green.
**Situation:** A hunting-camp (poachers, or the Gilded Company's outriders) that bit off something that
bit back. The glowing-blood trail is the Hunt's beast. The crates' sigil ties it to Cadoc. A discovery
that braids the jungle's regional Hunt with Arc C.
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation reconstructs "they caught something luminous; it (or its kin) came back for
it" DC [14]. The gold-trimmed crates mark the camp as Company-supplied (History/Insight DC [13]). Survival
follows the glowing-blood trail (toward Combat #3's grieving ape, or the temple) DC [13].
**Develops into:** A Company logbook: *"Captain wants the glowing one alive. Says the parts-buyer pays a
fortune. Says it's the same beast the northern fool shot."* (Arc C ↔ Arc A — Cadoc chasing the Hunt's
beast for profit; the **parts-buyer**'s standing order, sourced.) The luminous blood is fey-silver-laced. (Arc A.)

### 18. The Idol That Watches Back — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** Set in a niche of living root, furred with moss everywhere except its face: a small jade
idol, a coiled-serpent-and-leaf thing, its surface worn smooth by centuries of touching hands. Its eyes
are two chips of something that catches the green light — and follows you. Not a trick of the canopy.
You move; the eyes move. You hold still; after a moment, so do they, as if it, too, were being patient.
**Situation:** A shrine-idol of the Green, genuinely (and unsettlingly) *aware*. Leaving an offering
matters later (it's on the same generosity-ledger); taking the idol, or insulting it, earns the Green's
slow displeasure. The watching is the discovery; the choice is the hook.
**Foes/DCs:** Religion to know the offering-custom and the idol's deity DC [13]. Investigation to confirm
the eye-tracking is real, not pareidolia, DC [14]. Taking the idol triggers a lingering ill-fortune (and
the Yuan-ti's enmity); offering to it banks goodwill.
**Develops into:** Give gladly here and the Green "remembers" it — the Tithe-Warden's ledger gains a
mark, and the Yuan-ti receive the party as honoured. (Arc A.) The idol's jade is veined with the same
luminous quality as the Hunt's beast-blood. (Arc A — everything in the green is connected to the Green.)

## Hazard (5)

### 19. The Breathing Mire — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The ground here doesn't just give underfoot — it *breathes*, rising and falling in a slow
swell beneath the leaf-litter, exhaling warm marsh-gas through fissures that open and close like pores.
What looks like solid moss is a skin over something deep and soft, and the jungle's constant respiration
is, here, the sound of the mire itself, inhaling around your sinking boots.
**Situation:** A jungle bog that "breathes" — a sinking hazard dressed in the biome's living-ground
horror. The breathing fissures vent toxic gas; the moss-skin conceals deep mire. Reading the rhythm
(it's firmest mid-exhale) is the survival trick.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival to read the breathing-rhythm and step on the firm beats DC [14]. STR (Athletics)
to pull free, rising each round, DC [13/15/17…]. CON save vs. vented marsh-gas DC [13] (fail = poisoned).
Roping up enables self-rescue (Survival DC [10]).
**Develops into:** The mire's breath, sampled, carries the dream-trace — this is the jungle's lung
exhaling the Shared Dream. (Arc D ↔ Arc A.) Something half-sunk and preserved in the peat clutches a
fey-silver splinter. (Arc A.)

### 20. The Strangler-Fig Snare — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The vines are everywhere and you stopped noticing them an hour ago, which is precisely
the problem. One has looped your ankle. Another, you now see, has drawn taut across the trail at throat
height. They are not moving *fast* — nothing here does — but they are moving *with intent*, tightening
by patient degrees, and the jungle's breathing has taken on the satisfied rhythm of a thing closing its hand.
**Situation:** Predatory plant-life — strangler figs and grasping vines that ensnare slowly and
deliberately (the Green "taking"). A slow-burn hazard: the longer the party linger or struggle, the more
the green commits to holding them. Cutting free is easy *early*, deadly *late*.
**Foes/DCs:** Perception to notice the vines tightening *before* they're taut DC [13]. Athletics or a
blade to cut free, DC rising with delay DC [13→16]. The whole snare can animate as an Awakened-tree-style
hazard if the party fight it instead of slipping it.
**Develops into:** Tangled in the figs, older than the rest: a skeleton in pilgrim-grey, fully
overgrown, the green having *kept* it upright for years like a held breath. (Arc A / Arc F — the Green
collects pilgrims and does not let them fall; a grim preview of the forest's keeping.)

### 21. The Sweet Rot — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The smell reaches you before the clearing does — overripe fruit, cloying and heavy and
not entirely unpleasant, until it is. The ground is carpeted in fallen jungle-fruit gone to glorious
rainbow rot, clouds of iridescent flies rising and settling in patterns that almost look deliberate, and
the air is so thick with fermentation that your head goes light and the green starts, gently, to spin.
**Situation:** A fermentation-hazard — a fruit-fall whose rotting fumes intoxicate and hallucinate, the
flies moving in the biome's "watching patterns." Crossing risks confusion and visions (which bleed into
the Shared Dream). The drunk-jungle-air is the trap; the visions are the hook.
**Foes/DCs:** CON save vs. the fumes each round in the clearing DC [13] (fail = poisoned + a brief
dream-vision). Survival to find the upwind edge and skirt it DC [12]. Nature to identify which rot is
merely drunkening vs. genuinely toxic DC [14].
**Develops into:** The fume-visions are *the same dream* — the far green place, the open door. (Arc D ↔
Arc A — even the jungle's rot exhales the Tithe's dream.) The flies' watching-patterns, mapped, point
east, like everything else. (Arc A.)

### 22. The Quicksand Cenote-Rim — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The trail skirts the lip of a sinkhole and the lip is a lie — what looks like firm red
clay is a slurry of waterlogged silt fed by the pool below, and it has no edge so much as a gradient from
"ground" to "throat." The first to test it goes in to the waist before the clay even decides to hold,
and below, the black water waits with the patience of something that has swallowed travellers before.
**Situation:** A quicksand rim around a cenote (Discovery #15) — a sinking hazard with a long drop below
if the silt gives entirely. Combines the jungle's two signature dangers (living ground + the hungry pool).
Speed and rope save lives; panic and weight doom them.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival to read the false-clay gradient before stepping in DC [14]. STR (Athletics) to
pull free, DC [13/15/17…], with a failed save risking a plunge into the cenote (Athletics to catch a
root DC [14]). Roping the lead climber prevents the worst (Survival DC [10]).
**Develops into:** What surfaces when the silt is disturbed: the cenote's offerings, bobbing up — packs,
boots, a child's shoe — the Green giving *back*, briefly, what pilgrims gave it. (Arc A / Arc F.)

### 23. The Fever-Canopy — *hazard · any tier · Arc D*
**Read-aloud:** The drip from the canopy is heavier here, and warmer, and where it falls on bare skin it
leaves a faint flush that spreads. By the time you've crossed the grove, the slowest of you is sweating,
bright-eyed, swaying, murmuring about a green place with an open door — and the canopy goes on dripping,
patient and warm, feeding the fever down out of the leaves like the jungle is trying to share a dream.
**Situation:** The Shared Dream as a *climate* — a stretch of canopy whose transpiration carries the
fever directly, infecting by contact. The jungle is where the dream is greenest and most contagious.
Crossing risks the sickness; the murmured visions are the diagnostic.
**Foes/DCs:** CON save on entering the grove DC [14] (fail = the fever + the dream; advantage if covered/
hooded). Medicine to recognize "this is the southern sickness, but the *source* is overhead, not in the
water" DC [15]. Survival to route around the worst of the drip DC [13].
**Develops into:** A victim's fever-murmur names the exact dream a temperate plague-village described two
legs back, word for word. (Arc D — same dream, traced to its green wellspring.) **Sister Maready**, if she
reaches here, realizes the cure lies *deeper in*, toward the forest. (Arc D ↔ Arc A — toward the source is toward the danger.)

## Puzzle (3)

### 24. The Procession Door — *puzzle · tier 11–16 · gates Discovery #14 / Combat #5*
**Read-aloud:** The temple's summit door is carved as an open forest, and it does not open. Below it, the
procession-frieze wraps the final tier: figures climbing, each carrying a burden, and a row of worn stone
*pressure-plates* set into the steps before each figure. The meaning assembles slowly: you are meant to
ascend as the procession does — and the procession, you see, is sorted by what each figure *gives up.*
**Situation:** The temple-summit puzzle. The party must climb the final tier in the *correct order*,
treading the plates that correspond to *giving*, not *taking* (the carved figures who clutch their
burdens mark false plates that trigger the temple's defences). It's the Tithe rendered as a tread-puzzle:
the path rewards generosity and punishes hoarding, literally.
**Foes/DCs:** History/Religion to read the frieze's "give to ascend" logic DC [15]. Investigation to map
which plates are the *giving* figures vs. the *clutching* ones DC [16]. A wrong plate triggers a defence
(dart-trap, or wakes the T-Rex of Combat #5). Solving it opens the summit door.
**Develops into:** The open door shows the forest, and a single figure already standing in it — the
party's number, plus one — patient, hand half-raised. (Arc F — **the One Who Follows**, glimpsed through
the Tithe's own door.) Beyond, the Green's heart, and the Warden's fresh tally. (Arc A.)

### 25. The Shifting Trails — *puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** You've passed this strangler-fig before. You're certain of it — and yet the trail you
took clearly led elsewhere. By the third repetition you understand: the jungle's paths are *re-routing*,
slowly, while you walk them, the way the cartographer raved, all of them curling toward the same eastern
point no matter which you choose. The forest is not letting you get lost. It is *guiding* you, and you
cannot tell if that is mercy.
**Situation:** A navigation-puzzle where the jungle actively reshapes its trails (the road-rows theme,
overgrown and labyrinthine). The party can fight the pull (hard — every path bends east) or *use* it
(every trail leads, eventually, toward the temple/threshold). The puzzle is recognizing the rule and
deciding whether to obey it.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival to notice the trails are moving, not the party DC [14]. Investigation/Nature to
deduce the rule — *all paths bend east; resisting costs time and exhaustion* DC [15]. The mad
cartographer's maps (Social #11), if collated, solve it outright.
**Develops into:** Stop fighting the pull and the trail delivers the party to the temple of the Green —
faster than any map could. (Arc A — the country *wants* them at the threshold; the forest is "expecting"
them.) Resist it and they emerge exhausted, having walked all day to arrive where the jungle meant them to. (Arc A.)

### 26. The Almost-Words, Answered — *puzzle · any tier · uses Discovery #16*
**Read-aloud:** The jungle is trying to say something, and you have decided to answer. Transcribed by
firelight, the near-language resolves into a structure — call and response, a phrase that wants
completing, a riddle the forest itself is posing in a vocabulary of breath and drip and insect-drone. Get
the response right and the green *relaxes*, a held breath let go. Get it wrong, and the breathing
sharpens, and something in the dark leans closer.
**Situation:** A linguistic/lore puzzle: parse the jungle's near-speech (Discovery #16) into a call-and-
response and supply the correct answer. The intended answer is thematic — the Tithe's password, *"gladly"*
/ *"I give"* — the same word the whole campaign rewards. Right answer eases the jungle and opens a way;
wrong answer summons its attention (and possibly Combat #6's couatl-as-judge).
**Foes/DCs:** Arcana/Insight to parse the call-and-response structure DC [16]. The "answer" is a table
puzzle (GM: the forest asks, in effect, *give or be given?* — the safe reply is to offer, gladly). A right
answer can be reached by wit *or* by a clever Performance (DC [15]) that simply *gives* the forest a song.
**Develops into:** Answer *gladly* and the jungle parts a clear road to the temple and grants the party
the Green's provisional favour. (Arc A — the password of the whole campaign, learned in the green.) Answer
*take* (or refuse to answer) and the couatl (Combat #6) descends to weigh them in person. (Arc A.)

- A ziggurat of black root-cracked stone rises out of the green, humming in your back teeth, its carved procession climbing toward a door shaped like an open forest. *(site)*
- The jungle floor simply ends in a circular shaft hung with roots like a green throat, and the forest's breathing wells up loudest out of the dark water far below. *(site)*

# SAVANNA

> *Grass to the horizon moving in a wind you can't feel; predators fat and unbothered.* The Great Hunt
> gathers many peoples under truce here, the **Bone-Singer** sells courage by the trophy, and **Sir
> Cadoc Vane** chases the wrong-beast for the name it carries — so combat AND social both peak. Foes:
> Lion, Gnoll Pack Lord, Elephant, Triceratops, Tyrannosaurus.
> **Targets: Combat 8 · Social 5 · Discovery 4 · Hazard 3 · Puzzle 2 (22).**

## Combat (8)

### 1. The Unbothered Pride — *combat · tier 1–4*
**Read-aloud:** They don't run and they don't tense. The lions simply lift their heads from the kill,
golden and enormous and *unimpressed*, and watch you the way fat landlords watch a late tenant — with a
patience that is somehow worse than aggression. This is their grass. You are a question they haven't yet
decided is worth standing up for.
**Situation:** A pride of lions, fat and territorial, holding a kill the party may need to pass (or want
to claim). They're not desperate — which makes them *bold*, not skittish. Backing off cleanly is an
option; so is a hard fight with apex cats who don't bluff.
**Foes/DCs:** `pack` — Lion ×3–4, or `packLeader` Lion ×3 + Giant Hyena ×1. Animal Handling to read the
pride's mood and withdraw without triggering a charge DC [13]. Survival to skirt their kill-ground entirely DC [12].
**Develops into:** The lions' kill is wearing a saddle — a courier's horse, run down on the open grass,
its dispatch-bag still lashed on. (Arc C — Company correspondence, or a Hunt-summons, lost on the
savanna.) Vultures wheel toward a larger gathering on the horizon: the Great Hunt assembling. (Regional savanna.)

### 2. The Gnoll War-Band — *combat · tier 1–4 / 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** You smell them on the windless wind — carrion and wet fur — before the grass gives them
up: a loping pack of hyena-headed raiders, and at their center a brute half again the others' size that
carries a fetish-pole strung with skulls too varied to be only animal. It throws back its head and the
whole band *laughs*, high and grating, the sound of something that has already decided how this ends.
**Situation:** A gnoll war-band raiding the Hunt's edges, breaking the truce that's supposed to hold
during the gathering. They're a threat to the party and to the Hunt itself — bringing their heads to the
Hunt-camp buys standing; letting them raid sours the truce.
**Foes/DCs:** `packLeader` — Gnoll ×4 + Gnoll Pack Lord ×1, or `pack` Gnoll ×5 + Giant Hyena ×2. At
higher tier, add a Gnoll Fang of Yeenoghu (lurker). Intimidation breaks the lesser gnolls if the Pack
Lord falls DC [14].
**Develops into:** The Pack Lord's fetish-pole holds a gold-trimmed skull — a Company scout the gnolls
took. (Arc C — the gnolls have been bloodying Cadoc's people too; a shared enemy, a possible alliance
lever.) Their raiding is drawing the Hunt's wrong-beast toward the camp. (Regional savanna.)

### 3. The Truce-Breakers — *combat · tier 5–10 · social-combat*
**Read-aloud:** It starts as a shoving-match between two hunting-parties over a kill at the gathering's
edge — words, then spears — and you are close enough that when the first blood is drawn, every head in
the camp turns, because blood spilled *under the Hunt's truce* is the one thing all these peoples agreed
would never happen. The grass goes very quiet. Everyone is now deciding whom to blame.
**Situation:** A truce-violation at the Great Hunt — rival hunting-parties (gnolls, human bands, a Company
patrol) come to blows, and the party are positioned to either *quell* it (heroic, builds standing) or be
swept into it. The Bone-Singer watches to see who keeps their head. Cadoc may be a provocateur.
**Foes/DCs:** Combat if it ignites: a scrum of Scout ×3 + Veteran ×2 + Gnoll ×3 (two sides). Persuasion/
Intimidation to *stop* it before it spreads DC [15] (a CHA "combat" against the brawl). Insight spots who
struck first — and that it was *staged* DC [14].
**Develops into:** The brawl was provoked — a gold-trimmed hand wanted the truce broken so the Hunt would
scatter and leave the wrong-beast to *one* claimant. (Arc C — Cadoc, or Ilse over his head, manipulating
the Hunt.) Quell it, and the Bone-Singer marks the party as truce-keepers. (Regional savanna.)

### 4. The Elephant Matriarch's Charge — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The herd was a grey island in the grass and you were giving it a wide, respectful berth —
until the matriarch wheeled, ears flared like sails, and came. The ground is a drum. There is a calf
behind her, and a fresh spear-wound weeping down her flank, and in her small furious eye is the
absolute, unnegotiable conviction that you are the hunters who did this, come back to finish it.
**Situation:** A wounded elephant matriarch charging the party in defence of her calf — mistaking them
for the poachers who speared her. A tragic fight; the kinder play is to break off, prove you're not the
threat, and turn her grief toward the real hunters (often Cadoc's).
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Elephant ×1 (Elephant ×2 if the herd joins). Athletics/Acrobatics to evade the
charge DC [15]. Animal Handling to break her fixation and show you're not the poacher DC [16]. The fresh
spear in her flank is gold-trimmed (Investigation DC [10]).
**Develops into:** The spear is Company-marked — Cadoc's people wounded her and lost the kill. (Arc C —
the Company takes and leaves wreckage.) Spare her, and the herd later *helps* the party against the
wrong-beast or the gnolls. (Regional savanna — generosity to the grass repaid.)

### 5. The Wrong-Beast — *combat · tier 11–16 · Arc C + regional savanna centrepiece*
**Read-aloud:** The Hunt has gathered for a great beast, and a great beast has come — but it is *wrong*.
Too large, too still, and it watches the gathered hunters with an intelligence no animal should own, its
hide marked with a pattern that is almost a sigil, its eye fixed on the horizon-east as if the Hunt is
beneath its notice and only the green distance matters. The Bone-Singer has gone silent. And across the
grass, gold trim catches the sun: Cadoc, already nocking, hungry for the name this kill would carry.
**Situation:** The Great Hunt's quarry is no ordinary apex — it's a Tithe-touched beast (a triceratops or
tyrannosaurus marked by the forest, "going home"), and killing it is both the trophy of a lifetime and a
*mistake*. The party race Cadoc for the kill — or stop the kill, recognizing what it is. This is Arc C's
savanna fulcrum.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Triceratops ×1 or Tyrannosaurus Rex ×1, with the unnatural-intelligence rider
(it's *aware*, fights tactically, ignores easy prey for the threat). At tier 17–20, add the Hunt itself
as chaos. Insight/Nature to recognize the beast is *marked*, not natural, DC [16]; Religion to know
"killing this pays a debt to something, or *incurs* one" DC [17].
**Develops into:** Let Cadoc make the kill and he takes the name, the trophy, and the cost — the beast
was the forest's, and what he's looted from the Tithe will chase him to the volcanic Forge. (Arc C —
*"whatever he chased, chased back"*; Arc A — the Hunt's beast was "going home to pay.") Take it yourself,
and *you* carry the cost to the threshold. The **Bone-Singer** weighs whoever claims it. The beast's
blood is luminous, fey-silver-laced — the **parts-buyer**'s prize. (Arc A.)

### 6. The Rhinoceros Ambush at the Waterhole — *combat · tier 5–10*
**Read-aloud:** The waterhole is the only water for miles and everything knows it, which is why the
churned mud around it is a palimpsest of every track on the savanna — and why the rhinoceros dozing in
the reeds does not appreciate the competition. It comes out of the cover with no warning and no top
speed, just a single, committed, locomotive certainty aimed at the nearest of you.
**Situation:** A territorial rhino (or a pair) controlling a vital waterhole the thirsty party need.
Brute, fast, and disinclined to share. The hazard of thirst (see Hazard #17) makes the water worth
fighting for; the rhino makes the fight expensive.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Rhinoceros ×1, or `skirmish` Rhinoceros ×2. Athletics to sidestep the charge DC [14].
Survival to water at the hole's far edge without provoking it DC [13]. Animal Handling to drive it off
short of a fight DC [15].
**Develops into:** Half-sunk in the waterhole's mud, a Hunt-offering: a beast-skull crowned and weighted
down deliberately, the Bone-Singer's mark on its brow. (Regional savanna — the Hunt tithes the waterhole
too.) Tracks show a gold-trimmed party watered here a day ago, heading toward the gathering. (Arc C.)

### 7. The Gnoll Fang's Hunt — *combat · tier 11–16*
**Read-aloud:** This one is not laughing. Where the war-band's brutes are loud, the thing that pads out
of the grass now is silent and certain and *blessed* — a gnoll grown into something worse, hyena-grin
gone solemn, a demon's favour hanging off it like a stink — and it has come not to raid but to *consecrate*,
to turn the whole Great Hunt into a slaughter-offering to the thing it serves.
**Situation:** A Gnoll Fang of Yeenoghu attempting to corrupt the Great Hunt into a massacre — the
regional savanna threat at its sharpest. It's hunting the Hunt itself. The party must stop it before the
truce becomes a bloodbath; the Bone-Singer and even Cadoc may be temporary allies against it.
**Foes/DCs:** `packLeader` — Gnoll Fang of Yeenoghu ×1 + Gnoll Pack Lord ×1 + Gnoll ×4. At tier 17–20,
add Lamia (lurker) as its ally. Religion to know its rite and how to disrupt it DC [16]. Killing the Fang
breaks the lesser gnolls' nerve.
**Develops into:** The Fang's rite, examined, was meant to "feed the grass to the god so the god grows
fat for the green" — even Yeenoghu's gnolls are oriented toward the forest. (Arc A — every hunger feeds
the Tithe.) Stopping it earns the Hunt's gratitude and the Bone-Singer's true respect. (Regional savanna.)

### 8. The Brass Dragon's Riddle-Hunt — *combat · tier 17–20*
**Read-aloud:** It has been circling so high you took it for a vulture, and now it lands in a downdraft
that flattens the grass for fifty feet and folds its great brass wings and *grins*, because brass dragons
love nothing on this earth so much as a captive audience. "Splendid," it rumbles, delighted. "Travellers.
And in such a *hurry.* Sit. Talk. I so rarely get to finish a conversation before the talking stops."
**Situation:** An adult brass dragon that hunts *conversation*, not flesh — it'll badger the party for
talk, riddles, and gossip, and turns violent only if *bored* or refused. The savanna's apex as a social-
combat: entertain it and pass, snub it and fight a wyrm. It knows the Hunt, the Company, and the road
ahead.
**Foes/DCs:** `solo` — Adult Brass Dragon ×1 (Lion ×3 as its "court"). Persuasion/Performance to keep it
entertained DC [18]; Insight reads exactly how close to bored it is DC [17]. Refusing to talk, or trying
to slip away, provokes its (regretful) breath weapon.
**Develops into:** Charmed, it gossips freely: *"The gold knight's chasing a beast that'll eat his legend.
A pale clerk's been through, confirming routes. And the grass — have you noticed? — all leans east now,
like it's listening for something."* (Arc C + Arc E + Arc A, delivered by a dragon who just wants company.)

## Social (5)

### 9. The Great Hunt's Welcome — *social · any tier · regional savanna hub*
**Read-aloud:** The gathering is enormous — a hundred fires, a dozen peoples who'd kill each other anywhere
else, all moving under one fragile law: *no blood but the beast's.* A herald meets you at the camp's edge,
spear reversed in the sign of truce, and lays it out plain: you are welcome to the Hunt, the fires, and
the trade, so long as you spill no blood here but the quarry's. Break the truce, and every hand turns on you.
**Situation:** The savanna's social hub — the Great Hunt under truce, where many peoples gather and the
party can trade, gather rumours, recruit, and pick up the regional and Arc-C threads. The single rule
(no blood but the beast's) is the pressure that makes everything here tense. This frames Combat #3, #5, #7.
**Foes/DCs:** Persuasion to win standing among the gathered bands DC [13]; Insight to read the truce's
fault-lines (who's about to break it) DC [14]. Carousing/Performance at the fires loosens every rumour
on the savanna.
**Develops into:** The Hunt is gathered for the wrong-beast (Combat #5); Cadoc is here, "buying courage
off the Bone-Singer" (Social #11); a pale clerk passed through "confirming the route" (Arc E — the
Collector). Every arc with a savanna leg surfaces a hook here. (Arcs C, E, A.)

### 10. The Bone-Singer's Bargain — *social · any tier · regional savanna · Arc C*
**Read-aloud:** The Bone-Singer's tent is hung with the trophies of great beasts — skulls and horns and
tusks, each tagged with a name and a price — and the singer sits among them stringing a new fetish,
humming a low note that makes the bones answer. "You killed something," they say, without looking up.
"Bring me its courage and I'll sell it back to whoever's run short. Or — " and now they do look up, weighing
you " — I'll tell you what your courage is worth, if you've the stomach to hear it."
**Situation:** The savanna's signature broker — the Bone-Singer buys trophies of great beasts and sells
their "courage" (boons, blessings, reputation) back to the fearful. They weigh ambition with a cold ear,
and they're *appraising* Cadoc's hunger as much as anyone's. A source of boons, lore, and Arc-C insight.
**Foes/DCs:** Persuasion to strike a good trade for a trophy DC [13]; Insight to catch what the Singer
really thinks of Cadoc DC [14]. Religion/Nature to know which trophy carries which "courage" DC [13].
**Develops into:** *"The gold knight wants the courage of a beast he hasn't killed yet. He'll pay any
price. That kind of want gets a man eaten." * (Arc C — the Bone-Singer reads Cadoc's doom.) The Singer
warns that the wrong-beast's "courage" is *not for sale* — "you don't sell what the forest lent." (Arc A.)

### 11. Cadoc Buys Courage — *social · tier 11–16 · Arc C*
**Read-aloud:** You find Cadoc Vane in the Bone-Singer's tent, and for once the smirk is working overtime
to cover something underneath it. He's haggling — hard — for the courage of the wrong-beast, the kill he
hasn't made, and when he sees you his bravado snaps back into place like a visor. "Come to watch me make
history?" he says. "Stay. Learn how it's done. Leave some glory for the rest, mind — there's plenty."
**Situation:** The savanna beat of Arc C — Cadoc at his most hubristic, buying the courage to kill the
wrong-beast, dismissing every warning. The party can goad him toward the kill (and his doom), warn him
off (he won't listen *now* — but it banks credit for the boreal turn), or race him for the beast.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight reads the fear beneath the swagger — *he knows the beast is wrong and wants it
anyway* DC [14]. Persuasion to plant a doubt that surfaces later DC [15] (he'll scoff now, remember it at
the boreal wall). Intimidation/Deception to make him hesitate at the kill DC [16].
**Develops into:** Whatever the party do, Cadoc takes the warning *with* him — scoffed-at now, it's why,
at the boreal wall, he finally asks for help. (Arc C — the seed of his turn.) If goaded into the kill, his
doom is on the party's hands, and Ilse remembers. (Arc C ↔ Arc F.) He mentions, sourly, "the glove-woman's
note." (Arc B.)

### 12. The Truce-Keeper's Quiet Word — *social · any tier · regional savanna*
**Read-aloud:** An old hunter draws you aside from the fires, far enough that the laughter dims, and
speaks low. "You've a steady look. So I'll tell you what the young ones won't hear: this Hunt's wrong. The
beast we've gathered for isn't a beast. The grass leans east. And a pale man came through counting us like
livestock. Old truces don't break from outside, friend. They break because something *wants* them to."
**Situation:** The savanna's wise-elder beat — a seasoned Hunt-keeper who's noticed everything the young
hunters dismiss and slips the party the real picture. A clean delivery vector for the regional savanna
truth and the cross-arc through-lines (the eastward lean, the Collector, the staged truce-break).
**Foes/DCs:** Insight confirms the elder is sober and serious, not fear-mongering, DC [12]. Persuasion to
draw out what they'd *do* about it gets a plan (and a favour owed) DC [13].
**Develops into:** The elder names all three threads converging on the Hunt: the wrong-beast (Arc A/
regional), the pale counting-clerk (Arc E — **the Collector**), and "a gold-trimmed hand" stirring the
truce (Arc C — Cadoc/Ilse). They become a recurring savanna ally if heeded. (Regional savanna.)

### 13. The Memory-Veiled Stranger — *social · any tier · Arc E thread-touch*
**Read-aloud:** They sit alone at a fire no one else will share, veiled against a sun that isn't shining,
and they greet you by a name you haven't given — your *grandmother's* name, or a dead friend's, spoken
gently, as a kindness. "I trade in what you won't miss," they murmur, and produce a skin of water so sweet
the smell alone makes your throat ache. "A sip for a small forgetting. Out here, water is worth more than memory. Isn't it?"
**Situation:** A savanna touch of Arc E — a memory-broker (the Thirst-King's emissary reaching west, or a
freelance imitator) trading impossibly sweet water for forgotten memories "you won't miss." On the dry
savanna, the offer is *tempting* — which is the trap. It's the desert's economy, leaking into the grass.
**Foes/DCs:** Insight reads that each "small forgetting" is *not* small DC [15]. Wisdom save to decline
the ache-sweet water when thirsty DC [14]. Persuasion to learn *who* they answer to gets "the Thirst-King,
and the one who keeps the same ledger." (Arc E.)
**Develops into:** *"A pale clerk and I keep the same accounts,"* they note, sipping. *"Memory, years,
names. The fey and the ledger trade the same coin." * (Arc E — the Collector and the Thirst-King's
market revealed as one; planted on the savanna.) Each forgetting taken is a debt the Tithe-Warden later counts. (Arc A.)

## Discovery (4)

### 14. The Bones the Wind Arranges — *discovery · any tier · savanna signature*
**Read-aloud:** A great beast died here, long ago, and the savanna's wind — the wind you cannot feel —
has spent the years *arranging* it. The bones lie in a pattern too deliberate for scavengers and scatter:
ribs fanned like a sundial, the skull set at a focus, smaller bones laid in arcs around it, a near-circle
the wind has tended like a grave it visits. And every long bone points, you notice, the same way. East.
**Situation:** The savanna's signature discovery — a beast's skeleton the windless wind has composed into
an almost-deliberate figure, oriented east like everything else in the country. Eerie, beautiful, and a
quiet tell of the Tithe's reach. Extends `BIOME_THEMES.site`.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival/Nature to confirm "no scavenger arranges bones like this; nothing natural does"
DC [14]. Investigation reads the orientation — every bone aligned east — DC [13]. Religion senses it's
"a marker, or a grave that's been *re-made*" DC [14].
**Develops into:** At the skull's focus, pressed into the dust by no hand: a fresh tally-mark, the party's
number plus one. (Arc F.) The bones point precisely at the green-dark eastern horizon. (Arc A — the
savanna's dead, oriented toward the Tithe; the wind itself in service to the forest.)

### 15. The Trophy-Field — *discovery/site · any tier · regional savanna*
**Read-aloud:** The Hunt has hunted here for generations, and the proof is a field of standing trophies —
great horned skulls mounted on poles, tusks driven upright into the earth, each weathered monument
crowned and marked and *named*, a forest of the savanna's mightiest dead. The Bone-Singer's marks are
everywhere. And among the ancient grey relics, you find one that is new, and gold-trimmed, and does not
belong.
**Situation:** A sacred trophy-ground of the Great Hunt — a site that tells the savanna's whole culture
at a glance, and where a *new, wrong* trophy (a Company kill, or a Tithe-touched beast's skull) marks a
recent transgression. Respecting it matters; looting it earns the Hunt's lasting enmity.
**Foes/DCs:** History/Religion to read the trophy-field's customs and the Bone-Singer's marks DC [14].
Investigation spots the new gold-trimmed trophy as out of place — *and improperly taken* DC [13]. Taking
any trophy is a truce-grade offence.
**Develops into:** The new gold-trimmed trophy is a beast Cadoc killed *out of season and unsanctioned* —
the Hunt is quietly furious. (Arc C — Cadoc's transgression, evidence in hand; a lever to turn the Hunt
against him.) One ancient skull's "courage," the Bone-Singer says, the party "may yet need at the green." (Arc A.)

### 16. The Watering-Place Truce-Stone — *discovery/site · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The waterhole has a stone, old as the Hunt and worn by generations of hands, and the
worn-smooth carving on it is plain even now: a beast, and a circle of figures around it, spears reversed.
The same law the living Hunt keeps, cut in rock by hunters centuries dead. Offerings ring its base —
beads, a tusk, a poured measure of the precious water — left by everyone who drinks here, because out here,
to take water and leave nothing is to spit on the dead.
**Situation:** A truce-monument at a vital waterhole — the savanna's "leave a little" custom rendered in
stone, on the resource that matters most. Leaving an offering banks goodwill (and the Tithe-tally);
drinking without giving earns ill-luck and the Hunt's contempt. A quiet sibling to the temperate hedge-shrine.
**Foes/DCs:** Religion to know the offering-custom and the right small gift DC [12]. History reads the
stone as "older than this Hunt — the truce is *inherited*, not invented" DC [14]. Drinking without
offering earns a lingering misfortune.
**Develops into:** Give gladly here — water shared, an offering left — and the savanna remembers it: the
Bone-Singer, the truce-keeper, and the Tithe-Warden's ledger all gain a mark. (Arc A — generosity on the
grass, counted.) The stone's reversed-spear law is the *same* gesture the Hunt's herald uses — continuity in rock. (Regional savanna.)

### 17. The Grass That Leans East — *discovery/site · any tier · savanna signature*
**Read-aloud:** The wind you can't feel moves the grass in long slow combers, and for a while it looks
like any sea of grass anywhere — until you stand still long enough to be sure, and you are sure: the grass
is not just *moving* east, it is *leaning* east, every blade canted a few degrees the same way, holding the
lean even when the phantom wind dies, a whole horizon of grass bowing toward the green-dark distance like a
congregation that has heard its name called.
**Situation:** The savanna's quiet-horror discovery — the eastward pull made into the landscape itself, the
grass permanently canted toward the forest the way the boreal wood grew in rows and the temperate stones
leaned. It's the campaign's spine (Arc A) rendered in the biome's signature image: grass moving in a wind
no one can feel. Beautiful, vast, and unmistakable once seen.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival/Nature to confirm "the grass holds its lean with no wind to hold it — nothing grows
like this" DC [14]. Investigation to fix the lean's bearing on the green-dark horizon DC [13]. Standing in
it too long, a WIS save DC [13] or feel your own feet wanting to start walking east.
**Develops into:** The grass leans precisely toward the same point the boreal rows bent to and the
temperate stones tilted at — one bearing, three biomes, one forest. (Arc A — the whole country orienting
toward the Tithe.) That night, **the Tithe-Warden** falls into step on the grass-road and remarks, mildly,
that the party "lean east now too." (Arc A — Beat 3 / 4.)

## Hazard (3)

### 18. The Thirst of the Open Grass — *hazard · any tier · savanna signature*
**Read-aloud:** The grass goes to the horizon in every direction, moving in a wind you cannot feel, and
there is no shade in any of it. The sun is a weight. The waterskins lighten by the hour. The waterhole the
guide promised is dry, or moved, or was never there — and the grass, rippling endlessly under its phantom
wind, begins to look less like a sea and more like a thing that is patiently waiting for you to lie down in it.
**Situation:** The savanna's defining hazard — exposure and thirst on the open grass, where water is rare
and contested (see Combat #6, Discovery #16). A slow-burn environmental threat that shapes every other
encounter: the party's need for water is what makes the rhino's hole, the truce-stone, and the memory-
broker's offer all *bite.*
**Foes/DCs:** CON save vs. heat/thirst each half-day without sufficient water DC [13] (escalating
exhaustion). Survival to find water — a hidden seep, a beast-trail to a hole — DC [14]. Nature to read
which grass-greenness signals subsurface water DC [13].
**Develops into:** Desperate thirst makes the memory-broker's ache-sweet water (Social #13) nearly
irresistible — the savanna's hazard *sets up* Arc E's temptation. (Arc E.) A mirage-city shimmers on the
horizon and is gone — the desert's "heat-cities that fall," reaching east into the grass. (Cross-biome — desert leaking in.)

### 19. The Grass Fire — *hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud:** The smell of smoke on the windless air is the first wrong thing, because smoke needs wind
and there is none — and then you crest a rise and the whole western grass is *alight*, a low orange line
crawling toward you faster than a person can comfortably run, throwing up a wall of smoke and fleeing
animals, every beast on the savanna suddenly running the same direction: away from the fire, and east.
**Situation:** A grass-fire sweeping the savanna — a fast, wide environmental hazard that turns the open
plain into a death-race and stampedes every animal (and the Hunt) ahead of it. The party must outrun it,
find a firebreak, or burn-back. The fleeing animals all run east — the fire driving the country's
eastward pull faster.
**Foes/DCs:** Survival to find the firebreak / read the fire's true speed and line DC [15]. CON save vs.
smoke DC [14]. Athletics for the run if caught in the open DC [15]. Burning-back a controlled break
(Survival DC [16]) saves the party and anyone with them.
**Develops into:** The fire wasn't natural — it was *set*, a firebreak-line laid to drive the Hunt and the
wrong-beast toward a single waiting claimant. (Arc C — Cadoc, or someone, herding the savanna with fire.)
Every animal it drives runs east, toward the green. (Arc A.)

### 20. The Drover's-Dust Sickness — *hazard · any tier · Arc D thread-touch*
**Read-aloud:** The herd-trail you've been following kicks up a fine pale dust that hangs in the windless
air long after the cattle have passed, and it gets in your throat, your eyes, your sleep. By the second
night, the one who breathed the most of it is feverish and bright-eyed, murmuring — not coughing, *murmuring* —
about a green place with an open door, and the dust still hangs over the trail behind you, patient and pale,
waiting for the next travellers.
**Situation:** The Shared Dream, savanna-vector — carried in the dust of a sick herd driven up from
infected water downstream. The fever rides the dust, not just the water. A reminder that Arc D follows the
party across every biome, finding new ways to travel.
**Foes/DCs:** Medicine to recognize "this is the southern sickness, carried in the dust off a sick herd"
DC [15]. CON save vs. heavy dust-exposure DC [13] (fail = the fever + the dream). Survival to route off
the contaminated herd-trail DC [12].
**Develops into:** The herd's drover, found ahead, is already deep in the dream and driving his sick
cattle *east* by instinct, toward the green. (Arc D ↔ Arc A — the fever and the eastward pull, fused in
one doomed man.) **Sister Maready**, if near, is following this very herd-trail, losing. (Arc D.)

## Puzzle (2)

### 21. The Bones That Number the Dead — *puzzle · any tier · uses Discovery #14*
**Read-aloud:** The wind-arranged skeleton is not just a figure — it is a *count.* The smaller bones
laid in arcs around the skull are grouped, deliberately, in clusters, and the clusters tally something:
a record, kept by the wind, of every beast that died on this ground, or every traveller, or every season
the Hunt has hunted — and when you total the clusters, the sum is always one more than the bones can
account for. One death in the count has no bone. As if something here dies that leaves no body.
**Situation:** A counting-puzzle in the savanna's signature bones — the wind's tally always runs one over
(the Tithe's off-by-one, savanna-dialect). Solving it reveals what the extra, bodiless death *is* — and
ties the savanna into the campaign's running arithmetic of "one more than there should be."
**Foes/DCs:** Investigation to read the bone-clusters as a deliberate tally DC [14]. The arithmetic — *the
count exceeds the bones by exactly one, every time* — is an INT check or table puzzle DC [15]. Solving it
uncovers, at the missing-bone's place, a buried offering (and a tally-mark).
**Develops into:** The bodiless death in the count is the same off-by-one as the boreal trapper's notches
and the temperate counting-stones — *something always takes one extra.* (Arc A — the Tithe's signature
arithmetic, third sighting.) The buried offering is fey-silver. (Arc A — the Hunt's beast, again.)

### 22. The Reversed Spears — *puzzle/social · any tier · regional savanna*
**Read-aloud:** The truce is breaking in front of you — two bands an inch from blood — and the herald
thrusts a bundle of spears into your arms with a desperate look, because there is a *rite* to calling the
truce back, an old sequence of reversed spears and spoken names and shared gestures that every band half-
remembers and none can complete alone, and the only people not yet committed to the fight are you. Get it
right, and the grass stands down. Get it wrong, and you've insulted them all.
**Situation:** A social-ritual puzzle: re-invoke the Hunt's truce by performing the reversed-spear rite
correctly, drawing the right gesture/word from each band before blood spills. It's a de-escalation
challenge with a procedural solution — the party must read each band's custom and assemble the shared rite
under pressure. (Resolves Combat #3 without a brawl.)
**Foes/DCs:** Insight/History to recall each band's piece of the rite DC [14]. Persuasion to get each
band to *perform* their part DC [15]. Religion to know the closing gesture (spears reversed, a name to the
grass) DC [13]. Three pieces assembled in order restores the truce; a fumble ignites Combat #3.
**Develops into:** Restore the truce and the whole Hunt owes the party — the Bone-Singer, the truce-keeper,
the gathered bands, all marked grateful. (Regional savanna — standing earned the *generous* way.) The
herald murmurs that the rite is "the same law the forest keeps — give the grass a name, or it takes one." (Arc A.)

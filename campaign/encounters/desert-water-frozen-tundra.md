# PRIMUS Encounter Set — DESERT · WATER · FROZEN · TUNDRA

*Writers'-room encounter templates for four biomes, built against PRIMUS-BIBLE Section 3 target
counts and the live `BIOME_BANDS` rosters in `cavril-encounter-stage.js`. Tier bands map to APL:
**T1** = levels 1–4, **T2** = 5–10, **T3** = 11–16, **T4** = 17–20. Combat foes are drawn from the
real per-biome/per-tier roster (pool / lurker / apex). DCs and proper nouns are GM-fill in [brackets].*

Canon precedence: in-code `JOURNEY_THREADS` text > this set > improvisation. The fey always take.
Give gladly.

# DESERT

> *Heat-shimmer cities that fall before you reach them; bones the wind arranges into near-circles.
> Every draught of water has a price, and the price is something you'll never miss until it's gone.*

**Target counts:** Combat 6 · Social 5 · Discovery 5 · Hazard 6 · Puzzle 4 — **26 total.**
**Arc anchors:** the **Thirst-King's Road** (memory-for-water), the **Thirst-King's veiled Emissary**
(Arc E), the **Collector** trading in the same currency (Arc E), the **Glovewright** buying the party's
water without consent (Arc B).

## Combat (6)

### 1. The Jackals at the Dry Tank — *Combat · T1*
**Read-aloud.** The map-marked cistern is a cracked stone throat half-buried in dune, and it is dry —
but the *jackals* know better. They have dug a seep at its lowest corner, and they have decided the
seep is theirs. A dozen pairs of eyes catch the low sun as the pack fans out between you and the only
water for a day in any direction.
**The situation.** This isn't a hunt; it's a standoff over water. The jackals will break and flee the
moment the seep is no longer contested — but the seep is *also* the only water the party has found.
Killing the pack lets you drink; driving them off (Animal Handling or a tossed kill) banks goodwill
with a desert that remembers mercy.
**Foes/DCs.** 4–6 **Jackal** + 1 **Giant Hyena** (apex) leading. Animal Handling **DC [12/15/18 by
tier]** to drive off rather than fight; Survival **DC [10/13]** to find a second seep and avoid the
whole thing.
**Develops into.** The seep is *salted with a sweetness that shouldn't be there* — the first taste of
the **Thirst-King's** water seeping up from below. A pull-tab to the **Emissary** (Arc E).

### 2. The Gnoll Scent-Line — *Combat · T2*
**Read-aloud.** You smell them before the dunes give them up: wet fur and old blood on a wind that
carries nothing else. A gnoll war-band has cut your trail and run it down with the patience of things
that have never once gone hungry by choice. Their pack-lord lopes at the rear, unhurried, letting the
pack tire you first.
**The situation.** The **Gnoll Pack Lord** is herding the party toward a killing ground — a slot
between two dunes where the sand is loose and footing fails. Spotting the trap (Survival/Perception)
lets the party choose the ground instead.
**Foes/DCs.** 4 **Gnoll** + 1 **Gnoll Pack Lord** (apex); a **Cult Fanatic** of Yeenoghu travelling
with them at higher tables. Perception **DC [13/16]** to read the herding; Survival **DC [12/15]** to
break to firmer footing.
**Develops into.** The fanatic carries a tally-scrap of *names sold for water* — a thread to the
**Thirst-King's Emissary** and the **Collector's** shared market (Arc E).

### 3. The Scorpion in the Shade — *Combat · T1–T2 (ambush)*
**Read-aloud.** The only shade for miles is a tilted slab of sandstone, and you are not the first to
want it. As the lead boots cross the cool line, the sand *erupts* — a giant scorpion, pale as old
parchment, that has been waiting in the one place anything alive would go.
**The situation.** Pure ambush, sprung by the biome's cruelty: the shade is bait. A successful read of
the disturbed sand turns the surprise round around.
**Foes/DCs.** 1–2 **Giant Scorpion** (lurker). Perception/Survival **DC [14/16]** to spot the buried
ambusher before it strikes; Nature **DC [12]** to know shade-slabs are scorpion lairs.
**Develops into.** Under the slab, a **half-buried mile-marker** in a script no one in the party
reads — the **Thirst-King's Road** runs through here, and it is older than the kingdom that paved it.

### 4. The Lamia at the False Oasis — *Combat/Social · T2–T3*
**Read-aloud.** The palms are real. The water is real. The beautiful, gracious host who welcomes you
into the shade with cool cups and cooler words is real too — and her lower half, when she finally
rises, is not the shape of anything that should pour you wine. The **lamia** has run this oasis as a
larder for longer than the dunes have had this shape.
**The situation.** She would much rather charm than fight — every cup deepens her hold. The encounter
can resolve as parley (refuse the cups, argue the terms) or detonate into combat once she's named.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Lamia** (lurker/apex) + 2–3 **Cultist** thralls. Wisdom save **DC [14/16]** to
refuse the cup's compulsion; Insight **DC [13/16]** to clock the charm before drinking.
**Develops into.** The lamia *brokers* for the Thirst-King — she's a retail outlet for the same
memory-for-water trade. Sparing and bargaining with her opens a back-channel to the **Emissary**
(Arc E) the Collector would rather the party never find.

### 5. The Mummy in the Tilted Tomb — *Combat · T3*
**Read-aloud.** The storm peels back a dune and lays bare a door that was meant to stay buried: a tomb
tilted on its side, its seals broken outward. Something inside has been *waiting to be asked the wrong
question*, and the wind through the cracks sounds, almost, like it's counting.
**The situation.** The **Mummy** (or **Mummy Lord** at T4) guards a name, not a hoard — speak the name
carved above the door and it stands down; loot blindly and it rises. The tomb is a riddle-door (see
Puzzle 1) with a corpse behind it.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Mummy** + 2 **Skeleton** wardens (T3); **Mummy Lord** + lurker support (T4).
Religion/History **DC [15/18]** to read the name-rite; Dispel/Remove Curse for the mummy's rot at
**DC [16]**.
**Develops into.** The grave-name matches a name on the **Collector's bark-bound ledger** — "a man
who gave up his name." The dead here paid the Thirst-King's price and were filed. (Arc E.)

### 6. The Salamander on the Glass Flat — *Combat · T3–T4*
**Read-aloud.** The desert here has been struck to glass — a black, mirror-smooth pan where lightning
or something hotter fused the sand. Heat-haze coils above it in shapes that are almost coils, and one
of those coils turns out to have eyes. The **salamander** uncurls from the heat itself, trailing
smoke that screams faintly as it moves.
**The situation.** The glass flat radiates lethal heat (tie to Hazard 5) — the salamander is at home
and the party are cooking. Fighting on the glass costs fire damage each round; drawing it onto sand
levels the field.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Salamander** (T3) → **Efreeti** or **Marid** (apex, T4). Constitution save
**DC [13]** vs. ambient heat each round on the glass; Survival **DC [15]** to bait it onto cooler sand.
**Develops into.** The glass is a **forge-scar** — the desert's edge brushes the Cooling Forge's
country. A merchant two biomes back paid for "glass-that-sings"; this is its source. (Cross-biome
foreshadow → volcanic.)

## Social / parley (5)

### 7. The Veiled Emissary at the Well — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A figure sits in the only shade by a well that should be empty, veiled head to foot in
funeral linen the colour of old bone, a brass dipper resting across its knees. It does not look up as
you approach. "You are thirsty," it says, in a voice like wind over a tomb-mouth. "I have water sweeter
than any you will taste again. The price is small. You will not even miss it."
**The situation.** This is the **Thirst-King's Emissary** (Arc E). Each draught costs *a memory you
won't notice gone* — a name, a face, a year. The Emissary keeps a tally. The whole encounter is the
bargain: drink and lose, refuse and thirst, or *argue the terms* (the one fey-adjacent market mortal
wit can dent).
**Foes/DCs.** No combat unless provoked (then **Mummy**-statted). Persuasion/Deception **DC [16/18]**
to barter the price down; Insight **DC [14]** to sense *what* a draught would take before you drink.
**Develops into.** The Emissary and the **Collector** deal in the same coin — memory, years, names.
Meeting both is the campaign's quiet reveal that *the fey and the ledger are one market* (Arc E ↔ Arc A).

### 8. The Glovewright's Paid Water — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A boy no older than ten waits at the caravanserai gate wearing a glove a size too big,
and presses a sealed skin of water into your hands before you can speak. "It's paid for," he says.
"A friend went ahead of you." The wax seal bears a glove pressed into it — and you have seen that mark
before, on a corpse, or a king, or a clerk.
**The situation.** The **Glovewright** (Arc B) has *bought the party Thirst-King water on their behalf*
— a debt of memory incurred in their name, without consent. The water is genuinely sweet, genuinely
free, and genuinely mortgages something. Accept gratefully, refuse warily, or interrogate the boy.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [15]** off the boy to sense the gift is *also* a hook; Investigation
**DC [13]** on the seal to match the glove-mark to a prior sighting.
**Develops into.** "I have spent much on you. Soon I will ask one thing, and you will want to say yes."
The smoothed road is the Quiet Hand's leash (Arc B), and the glove is the bible's recurring object.

### 9. The Caravan That Won't Stop — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A merchant train crests the dune at the wrong hour — noon, when no sane caravan moves —
and does not slow for your hail. The lead drover, sun-blistered and wild-eyed, calls down without
reining in: "Don't make us stop! Whatever's behind us, it walks when we walk. Buy quick or wave us on,
but for the love of water *keep us moving*."
**The situation.** A roadside merchant (per `MerchantEconomy.TYPES`) fleeing something — possibly the
**One Who Follows** (Arc F), possibly heat-madness, possibly a real predator. Trade happens at a walk,
or not at all. The drover carries one rumour and one hook.
**Foes/DCs.** None (unless the thing behind them is real — GM's call). Persuasion **DC [13]** to make
them pause; Insight **DC [14]** to judge whether the fear is founded.
**Develops into.** The drover's rumour: *"A pale clerk asked after you by name, two wells back."* The
**Collector** is on the road ahead (Arc E). What follows the caravan may be Arc F, early.

### 10. The Pilgrim Who Won't Drink — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A lone woman walks the wrong way down the caravan road — toward the deep desert, not
out of it — with a child's shoe knotted on a cord at her throat and a waterskin so light it whistles
empty in the wind. She does not ask your name. She does not ask for water. "I've made this crossing
before," she says, when you offer. "I know what the sweet water costs. I'd rather thirst and remember."
**The situation.** **Wrenna** (the Pilgrim, Arcs A/D/F), crossing the desert on her endless road to the
forest. She refuses the Thirst-King's water *on principle* — she will not trade memory, because memory
is all she has of the daughter. A conversation, a warning, a glimpse of the spine arc.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [12]** to see her grief is *older and stranger* than ordinary loss;
History/Arcana **DC [16]** to recognise she's done this crossing more than one mortal lifetime allows.
**Develops into.** "Give gladly, or pay double." Wrenna is the barometer of the **Tithe** (Arc A); her
refusal of the desert's bargain foreshadows the forest's.

### 11. The Two Wells, Two Claimants — *Social · T1–T2*
**Read-aloud.** Two families stand on either side of a low stone well, and between them the air is all
edges. One holds a deed inked in a dead king's hand; the other has drawn from this well for nine
generations and never heard of a deed. A child on each side clutches an empty jug. The well, you note,
is half-full — enough for one household through the dry season. Not two.
**The situation.** A water-rights feud with no villain — just two true claims and not enough water.
The party can adjudicate (Persuasion/Insight), find a *second* source (Survival), or expose that the
well's been *quietly tapped from below* by a third party (echo of the Weir-War's hidden profiteer).
**Foes/DCs.** None (de-escalation challenge). Persuasion **DC [15]** to broker a share; Survival
**DC [16]** to dowse a second seep; Investigation **DC [14]** to find the siphon.
**Develops into.** If tapped from below: the siphon-line runs toward a **buried, sweet-watered place** —
the Thirst-King's reach, or the Glovewright's old infrastructure (Arc B). Banked goodwill the
**Tithe-Warden** later counts (Arc A/D).

## Discovery / site (5)

### 12. The City That Falls — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** On the horizon, a city: white towers, green gardens, the glint of a thousand windows
catching the sun. You walk toward it for an hour and it does not grow closer. You walk for two, and it
*falls* — towers folding into haze, gardens sublimating into shimmer — and where it stood there is only
a low ring of foundation-stones and a single standing arch, half-swallowed by sand.
**The situation.** A heat-mirage of a city that *really stood here once* — the desert remembers it
hard enough to project it. The standing arch is real, and walking through it is a threshold-event
(faint fey logic; the desert's edge brushes the forest's). Investigation rewards the foundation-ring.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Arcana **DC [15]** to recognise the mirage as memory, not heat; Investigation
**DC [13]** at the arch for the city's *name*, carved where the keystone should be.
**Develops into.** The arch is twinned with the **Courteous Guide's** threshold-trunk in the forest
("they arrive at the same place"). The city fell paying a debt — a Tithe rehearsal (Arc A).

### 13. The Bone Circle — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The wind has arranged a field of sun-bleached bones into a near-circle — too regular to
be chance, too rough to be made by hands. At the centre, a single skull faces outward, jaw wired shut
with desert wire, and the wind moving through the bones makes a sound that is *almost* a word, repeated.
**The situation.** A discovery-site that extends `BIOME_THEMES[desert].site`: the bones of a caravan
that paid the Thirst-King's price and forgot the way home. The "word" the wind says is the name they
sold. Salvage among the bones; respect or disturb the centre-skull.
**Foes/DCs.** None (unless disturbed → 1–2 **Skeleton**). Perception **DC [12]** to make out the word;
Religion **DC [14]** to lay the circle to rest (banks goodwill); Investigation **DC [13]** for grave-goods.
**Develops into.** The wired-shut jaw is the Emissary's signature — *they keep the names they take.*
A direct artefact-thread to Arc E's memory-market.

### 14. The Buried Door with the Riddle-Lintel — *Discovery/Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Half-excavated by the last storm, a stone door stands in the lee of a dune, utterly
intact, its lintel carved with a single line of figures: a sun, a cup, a closed eye, a sun again. There
is no handle, no keyhole, no hinge you can see. The sand at its base is undisturbed. Whatever is inside
has been inside a very long time.
**The situation.** A sealed **Thirst-King vault** — the door opens to those who *understand the price*,
not to those who force it. (Forcing it triggers Hazard 3, the collapsing dune.) See Puzzle 1 for the
riddle mechanism; this is its physical site, full of grave-goods and one *unforgotten* thing.
**Foes/DCs.** None at the door. Investigation **DC [15]** to read the lintel; History/Arcana
**DC [16]** to place the iconography as Thirst-King script.
**Develops into.** Inside: a sleeper who *kept* their name when everyone else sold theirs — a potential
ally, a witness against the Emissary, or the Collector's one unpaid account (Arc E).

### 15. The Glassed Battlefield — *Discovery · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** A shallow valley floored entirely in black glass, smooth as a frozen lake, and frozen
*into* the glass: shapes. Weapons. A wheel. The suggestion of a hand. Something fused this army where
it stood, in an instant, and the desert has been polishing the result ever since.
**The situation.** Discovery-site tied to Combat 6 / Hazard 5: a battle ended by a single
catastrophic heat-event (the **Cooling Forge's** reach, or a dragon long dead). Walking the glass is a
hazard; reading it is a discovery. Things are *preserved* in it, including one still-intact weapon.
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution save **DC [13]** vs. radiant heat per round on the glass (Hazard 5). History
**DC [16]** to read the battle; Investigation **DC [15]** to extract the intact blade without cracking it.
**Develops into.** The intact weapon hums faintly — **forge-work**, and a buyer two biomes on pays in
gold for "anything the smith-god touched" (Arc C / volcanic foreshadow).

### 16. The Drowned Garden — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** In a slot canyon where flash-flood water once pooled and never fully left, you find the
impossible: a garden. Date palms, a fig, a tangle of flowering vine, all fed by a spring that should
not exist in this country — and at its centre, a stone basin of water so clear and so sweet-smelling
that your whole body leans toward it before your mind can object.
**The situation.** A hidden oasis that is *too good* — the spring is the Thirst-King's sweet water at
its source, and the garden is a snare grown around it. The **Green-Eyed Sister** may appear here to
press a hazelnut on the party "for a thirst you can't yet name," warning them off the basin (Arc A).
**Foes/DCs.** Wisdom save **DC [15]** to resist drinking from the basin on sight; Nature **DC [14]** to
recognise the garden as *cultivated*, not wild — someone tends this snare.
**Develops into.** The **Two Sisters** thread: the Green-Eyed Sister's hazelnut vs. the River-Grey
Sister's water, the fey quarrel over the party's debt, here in its desert key (Arc A).

## Hazard / environmental (6)

### 17. Thirst — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The waterskins are lighter than they were this morning, and the next marked well is a
day off, and the sun has not yet reached its worst. Your tongue is thick. The horizon does that thing
where it tilts. Somewhere ahead, you're fairly sure, is water — and you would believe almost anyone who
told you which way.
**The situation.** The desert's great hazard, made mechanical: a dehydration clock. This is the
pressure that makes every social water-offer (Emissary, Glovewright, lamia) bite. Manage it with
Survival, ration discipline, or *accept a stranger's water at a price.*
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution saves **DC [escalating: 10 → 13 → 16]** at intervals without water, with
exhaustion on failure; Survival **DC [14]** to find an unmarked seep and reset the clock.
**Develops into.** Thirst is the lever the **Thirst-King's Road** pulls (Arc E). A party run dry is a
party that *will* drink the sweet water — and the Emissary is always, conveniently, near.

### 18. The Sandstorm — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The horizon to the west goes the colour of a bruise, and then it *stands up* — a wall
of sand a thousand feet high, walking toward you faster than anything that size should move. You have
minutes. Maybe. The wind ahead of it already tastes of grit and old graves.
**The situation.** A `haboob` that erases visibility, navigation, and tracks. Shelter (a slab, a
canyon, a buried door) or ride it out roped together. The storm *reveals* as much as it buries — tombs,
markers, the City-That-Falls's foundation-ring all surface in its wake.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** to navigate or find shelter; Constitution save **DC [13]** vs.
abrasion/choking if caught in the open; Strength **DC [14]** to hold the rope-line together.
**Develops into.** In the storm's lee, *boot-prints that aren't yours* — the party's number, plus one,
already filling with fresh sand (Arc F, the One Who Follows).

### 19. The Collapsing Dune — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The slope ahead is the easy way — a long, smooth face of fine sand sloping down toward
the route. The first boot onto it sends a hiss running ahead, and then the whole face *moves*, a slow
avalanche of sand that wants to take your legs, then your hips, then your air, down toward whatever the
dune has been keeping buried.
**The situation.** A slip-face / sand-sink hazard, often guarding the buried door (Discovery 14): force
the door and the dune comes down. Strength/Acrobatics to escape; the sink *uncovers* something on its
way down.
**Foes/DCs.** Acrobatics **DC [13]** to ride the slide out; Strength (Athletics) **DC [15]** to climb
clear; failure → restrained/buried, **DC [16]** to dig free or be dug free.
**Develops into.** The sink exposes the **riddle-door's** threshold (Discovery 14 / Puzzle 1) — or a
fresh skeleton clutching a Thirst-King dipper (Arc E).

### 20. The Glass Flat's Heat — *Hazard · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** The black glass pan ahead is the fastest way across — flat, smooth, no dunes to climb.
It is also radiating the stored heat of a furnace, and the air above it bends into shapes that scream
faintly if you listen. Your boot-soles soften. Metal buckles get too hot to touch. Crossing this is a
race against your own cooking.
**The situation.** The forge-scarred glass (Discovery 15 / Combat 6) as standalone hazard: ambient
radiant heat per round, gear damage, mirage-disorientation. Cross fast, cross roped, or go around and
lose a day (feeding Hazard 17, Thirst).
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution save **DC [13]** per round on the glass (exhaustion on fail); Survival
**DC [12]** to pace the crossing in the cool dawn hours instead.
**Develops into.** Heat-haze resolves, once, into a **figure standing on the far edge with one hand
raised** — gone when you reach it (Arc F). Or the screaming steam shapes *form a word* (volcanic echo).

### 21. The Scarab Swarm — *Hazard · T1–T2*
**Read-aloud.** The ground ahead is *moving* — a living carpet of carrion beetles flowing across the
sand toward something dead, or toward you, the difference academic. They part around obstacles like
water and reform on the far side, and the dry rattle of a million carapaces fills the air like rain on
a tin roof.
**The situation.** A `swarm-of-insects` hazard treated as terrain: the swarm itself is the obstacle.
Wait it out, route around, or risk crossing (damage + the swarm strips anything organic it touches).
Fire and water both deter it.
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution save **DC [12]** per round if crossing; Survival **DC [13]** to predict its
path and route around; a torch/flask buys safe passage.
**Develops into.** What the swarm was *flowing toward*: a fresh corpse on the road, dressed for travel,
a **glove a size too big** still on one hand (Arc B — a Glovewright runner who didn't make it).

### 22. The Night Cold — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The same sun that tried to kill you all day abandons you at dusk, and the desert turns
on a knife-edge from furnace to ice. Without fuel — and there is no fuel here — the cold reaches in
through the bedrolls and finds the bone. By the third hour you are colder than you have ever been in a
place this hot.
**The situation.** The desert's overlooked hazard: brutal nighttime temperature swing. Fuel is the
problem (nothing burns). Forces hard choices — burn gear, huddle, or push on by moonlight (and into
the predators of Combat 1–3). Ties the desert to the cold biomes thematically.
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution save **DC [12]** without adequate shelter/fire; Survival **DC [13]** to
build a sand-shelter that holds warmth; **DC [15]** to find buried scrub-fuel.
**Develops into.** In the firelight someone *banks the coals while the watch's back is turned* — a small
kindness, intimate and wrong (Arc F). Or the cold draws the **Emissary** to a nearby fire, offering warmth
and water both.

## Puzzle / skill-challenge (4)

### 23. The Riddle-Lintel — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The sealed door's lintel reads, in carved figures: *sun, cup, closed eye, sun.* Below,
smaller, a line you can almost translate: "*Drink, and the door opens. Remember, and it stays shut.*"
There is no lock to pick and no hinge to lever — only the riddle, and the certainty that forcing it
brings the dune down (Hazard 3).
**The situation.** A skill-challenge gate, not a lock. The door opens to whoever *demonstrates they
understand the Thirst-King's price* — by pouring out water as offering (sun→cup), by speaking a memory
aloud and choosing to *give* it (closed eye), or by solving the iconographic sentence. Brute force =
Hazard 19.
**Foes/DCs.** Arcana/History **DC [16]** to translate the rite; Religion **DC [14]** to perform the
water-offering correctly; a *willing* sacrifice of a memory (roleplay) opens it without any roll.
**Develops into.** The door honours **generosity over cleverness** — the party who *gives* water to open
it (rather than tricking the lock) bank the lesson the **Tithe** will test at the forest (Arc A). The
sleeper within kept their name (Discovery 14).

### 24. Navigating by the Falling City — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Every landmark in this country is a liar. The mirage-city rises and falls at different
points on the horizon depending on the hour; the dunes walk; the stars are right but the ground beneath
them is wrong. Your map shows a route. The desert shows you four, and three of them end in bones.
**The situation.** A navigation skill-challenge built on the desert's *distance-lies* signature.
Triangulate using fixed points (the true standing arch, the bone-circle, the stars at night) against
the liars (mirages, walking dunes). Failure burns water (Hazard 17) and loops the party past the same
landmark twice.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [14/16]** per leg; Arcana **DC [15]** to tell true landmarks from
remembered ones; Investigation **DC [13]** to log fixed bearings. 3 successes before 2 failures.
**Develops into.** Done right, the party arrives at a **place not on any map** — the Falling City's true
foundation, or the Thirst-King's road-head. The desert "arrives at the same place" as the forest's
threshold logic (Arc A, the Courteous Guide).

### 25. Bargaining Down the Emissary — *Puzzle/Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The Emissary tilts its veiled head as you make your counter-offer, and for the first
time it seems *interested.* "You wish to pay in something other than memory," it says, the brass dipper
turning slowly in its wrapped hands. "Few think to ask. Fewer ask correctly. Tell me, then — what is a
fair price for a thing you will never miss?"
**The situation.** The one fey-adjacent market mortal *wit* can dent (Arc E). A debate-as-puzzle: the
party must find an alternative currency the Emissary will accept (a *true* name freely given vs. taken;
a future favour; a memory the party *wants* gone) — argued, not rolled to death. Clever terms shave the
Tithe later.
**Foes/DCs.** Persuasion/Deception **DC [16/18]** as a *fallback* if the argument is weak; a genuinely
clever counter-offer (GM adjudicates) succeeds with advantage or automatically. Insight **DC [15]** to
read which terms the Emissary actually values.
**Develops into.** Terms struck here are *recorded in the bark ledger* — the **Collector** honours them
(Arc E), and the **Tithe-Warden** counts the precedent (Arc A). Mortal wit, banked against a fey debt.

### 26. The Sun-Dial Door — *Puzzle · T1–T2*
**Read-aloud.** A circular plaza, drifted half-full of sand, with a tall central gnomon and twelve
worn niches around its rim — and a sealed bronze hatch at the gnomon's foot. As the sun crawls, the
gnomon's shadow sweeps the niches one by one, and you realise: the shadow is a key, and the lock is
*the time of day.*
**The situation.** A timing/observation puzzle. The hatch opens only when the gnomon's shadow falls on
the correct niche — but which niche is encoded by clues in the plaza (a carved sun-position, a
star-map, the orientation of the city that fell). Wait for the wrong hour and nothing; the right hour,
once, and it opens. Thirst (Hazard 17) makes *waiting* costly.
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [14]** to read which niche is keyed; Survival/Nature **DC [13]** to
predict when the shadow reaches it; History **DC [15]** to shortcut via the city's known orientation.
**Develops into.** Below: a cool, sealed cistern of *ordinary* water — a genuine mercy in this country,
and a cache the **Glovewright** has clearly used before (a too-big glove hung on a peg, Arc B).

# WATER

> *Salt laid over the river's green smell; a bell offshore with no boat to swing it. The current always
> tugs a hand's-width the wrong way — toward the bell, toward the source, toward the forest.*

**Target counts:** Combat 7 · Social 5 · Discovery 5 · Hazard 5 · Puzzle 3 — **25 total.**
**Arc anchors:** the **Ferryman's Debt** & the drowned bell counting the party (Arc A), the **Two
Sisters** (River-Grey offers water, Green-Eyed offers the hazelnut — Arc A), the **Shared Dream** moving
downstream on the current (Arc D), the **Weir-Keeper** and the hidden profiteer (Feature/Weir-War),
**Threnmouth** the town that drowned (regional water).

## Combat (7)

### 1. The Reef-Shark Pack — *Combat · T1*
**Read-aloud.** The water under the boat goes from green to grey to *full of shapes* in the space of a
breath. Reef sharks, a dozen of them, drawn by something — blood you didn't spill, or the boat's own
heartbeat — and circling tighter with every pass. The first one bumps the hull hard enough to spill a
rower off the bench.
**The situation.** A feeding-frenzy hazard-fight: the sharks aren't hunting the party so much as
responding to *something in the water already bleeding* (tie to Hazard/Discovery — a wreck, a body, the
drowned bell's resonance). Address the cause and the sharks scatter.
**Foes/DCs.** 4–6 **Reef Shark** + 1 **Hunter Shark** (lurker). Survival/Nature **DC [13]** to find what
they're feeding on; Athletics **DC [12]** to haul a spilled crewman back aboard before a pass.
**Develops into.** What's bleeding in the water is *ringing* — the **drowned bell** of the Ferryman's
Debt, tolling below, and the current tugs the boat a hand's-width toward it (Arc A).

### 2. The Sahuagin Raid — *Combat · T1–T2*
**Read-aloud.** They come over the gunwale all at once, dripping and silent, webbed hands finding
purchase where no handhold should be — sahuagin, the sea-devils, who have been pacing the boat just
below the surface since dusk and waiting for the watch to thin. Their eyes are flat black and they do
not blink at the spray.
**The situation.** A boarding action. The sahuagin want cargo, captives, or *territory* (the party
crossed a reef they hold). A `priestess` or **Sahuagin Baron** (apex, T2+) directs from the water.
Fighting on a pitching deck adds an Acrobatics tax.
**Foes/DCs.** 4 **Sahuagin** (T1) → + **Sahuagin Baron** (apex, T2/T3). Acrobatics **DC [12]** to keep
footing on the wet deck each round; Perception **DC [14]** to spot the boarding before it lands and
deny surprise.
**Develops into.** A captured sahuagin hisses of a *drowned bell-tower the deep-folk shun* — Threnmouth,
the town that drowned (regional water), and the bell that counts (Arc A).

### 3. The Hunter Shark in the Shallows — *Combat · T1–T2 (ambush)*
**Read-aloud.** The ford looked safe — knee-deep, sand-bottomed, the far bank an easy wade. You are
halfway across when the wake appears, a grey fin cutting upstream against the current toward the
splashing, and you understand that the shallows were never safe; they were *bait.*
**The situation.** A lurker ambush at a crossing: a hunter shark (or **Giant Octopus**) lairs at a
ford and takes waders. Spotting the wake early lets the party cross fast or bridge the gap.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Hunter Shark** or 1 **Giant Octopus** (lurker). Perception **DC [13]** to read the
disturbed water before entering; Athletics **DC [14]** to cross before it closes.
**Develops into.** Snagged on the lurker's lair: a waterlogged **ferry-pole**, and the current here runs
*upstream*, toward the source — the river's debt pulling everything the wrong way (Arc A).

### 4. The Sea Hag's Bargain-Gone-Sour — *Combat/Social · T2–T3*
**Read-aloud.** She rises from the kelp like something the tide forgot to take back — a sea hag, green
and dripping and *smiling*, with a voice like a mother's lullaby running backward. "You've come for the
drowned thing," she says, delighted. "Everyone does. Let me tell you my price first."
**The situation.** Starts as parley (she guards or sells access to a sunken site — the bell, a wreck,
Threnmouth's reliquary) and sours into combat if the party refuse her terms or *grudge* the payment
(her law: grudging payment costs double). She can also be a *source* — hags know the river's secrets.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Sea Hag** (lurker) + 2–3 **Merrow** or **Sahuagin** at higher tiers. Insight
**DC [14]** to spot the trick in her terms; Wisdom save **DC [14]** vs. her Death Glare if it comes to blows.
**Develops into.** Her price is *a memory of dry land* — the same currency the **Thirst-King's Emissary**
and the **Collector** trade in (Arc E), proving the market spans biomes. She knows the **drowned bell's**
true name (Arc A).

### 5. The Water Elemental at the Weir — *Combat · T2–T3*
**Read-aloud.** The river piles up against the old weir and *will not pass it cleanly* — the water humps,
coils, stands taller than water should, and then it has arms, and a face that is only the shape the
current takes when it's angry. The weir's sluice-gate hangs broken, and the river has had enough of being
told where to go.
**The situation.** An environmental boss tied to the Weir-War (Feature arc). The elemental rose when the
weir was sabotaged; calming the river means *fixing the weir* (Puzzle 2) as much as fighting. The
**Weir-Keeper** may beg the party to do the former, not the latter.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Water Elemental** (lurker/apex) + 2 **Merfolk** caught in the churn. Strength
**DC [15]** to brace against the current's pull; Athletics **DC [14]** to reach the broken sluice mid-fight.
**Develops into.** The weir was *sabotaged on purpose* — the **Weir-Keeper** knows a third party has grown
fat on the feud for years (Feature/Weir-War). The hidden profiteer is the hook that drops.

### 6. The Giant Shark — *Combat · T2–T3*
**Read-aloud.** The shadow under the boat is the size of the boat. It does not circle; it does not
hurry. It rises once, breaking the surface with an eye the size of a dinner-plate and a back ridged like
a keel, takes the measure of you, and sounds again into the green dark to decide whether you are worth
the trouble.
**The situation.** A solo apex predator — a real "do we fight or flee this" beat. The **Giant Shark**
can be outrun (a chase-skill-challenge), appeased (chum the water and slip past), or fought (a deadly
deck-and-water battle). At T4 it's a **Kraken's** lesser cousin announcing the deep.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Giant Shark** (apex) → **Kraken** at T4. Survival **DC [13]** to chum and divert it;
a rowing/sailing chase at escalating **DC [13/15/17]** to outrun.
**Develops into.** The shark flees something *deeper* — the water goes wrong, the **bell** tolls, and
whatever the deep-folk shun is stirring (Arc A / Threnmouth, regional water).

### 7. The Kraken's Herald — *Combat · T4*
**Read-aloud.** The sea draws *back* — a long, wrong, sucking withdrawal that leaves the boat tilting on
bared mud and stranded fish flopping in the sudden quiet. Then, on the horizon, the water stands up.
Among the lesser shapes that come ahead of the swell is one vast tentacle, pale and scarred, testing the
air like a finger reading a page.
**The situation.** The campaign's deep-water apex (Arc A / regional water). The **Kraken** itself need
not be beaten — surviving its *herald*, reading its rules (it is *collecting*, like the forest), and
paying or evading its toll is the encounter. Folk-horror logic: the deep has terms.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Kraken** (apex) + 2–3 **Giant Shark** / **Sahuagin Baron** outriders. Constitution
saves vs. the swell **DC [16]**; a *bargain* with the deep (roleplay) in place of a fight it can't win.
**Develops into.** The Kraken counts the boat — "*you carry more than you packed*" in a colder dialect —
the **Tithe** read from the sea floor (Arc A). The drowned bell is its tongue.

## Social / parley (5)

### 8. The Ferryman Who Waves Off Coin — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A flat-bottomed boat is already crossing toward you before you reach the bank, poled by
a lean figure who does not wait to be hailed. He takes you aboard without a word, poles you across the
deep, easy water, and when you reach for payment he waves it off with a hand like driftwood. "The river
gave you the easy water," he says, not unkindly. "It will want the favour returned. Upstream. At the source."
**The situation.** The **Ferryman** (Arc A, the Ferryman's Debt). His crossing is a *gift accepted* —
and the fey's first law is that a gift accepted is a claim. He cannot be paid in coin; the debt is owed
*to the river*, upstream, toward the forest. A conversation that puts the party on the ledger.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [14]** to feel the *weight* under his courtesy; Arcana/History
**DC [16]** to recognise that he, or his twin, has poled this crossing far longer than a man could.
**Develops into.** "The river remembers the bell, and the bell remembers a debt." The party are now on
the ledger (Arc A). His twin may pole the *return* crossing — and ask for the favour back (Arc A beat 2).

### 9. The Two Sisters at the Landing — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Two women wait at the river-landing, alike as reflections and utterly unlike. One,
green-eyed and dry, presses something small and hard into your palm — a hazelnut — and murmurs, "For a
thirst you can't yet name." The other, river-grey and dripping, offers a dipper of water so sweet the
smell of it buckles your knees, and says only, "Drink. Carry us home in your dreams."
**The situation.** The **Two Sisters** (Arc A), the central fey choice in water-country. The hazelnut
(gift, generosity, safe) vs. the sweet water (claim, the Shared Dream, the forest in your sleep).
Refuse the River-Grey Sister and "*her cold thumb leaves a mark that aches toward the forest.*" This is
a bargain offered, not a fight.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [15]** to sense which sister's gift *takes*; Wisdom save **DC [15]** to
refuse the dipper once it's under your nose.
**Develops into.** Drink the water and the party become carriers of the **Shared Dream** (Arc D) and the
**Tithe** (Arc A). Keep the hazelnut for "a thirst you can't yet name" — the desert, two biomes on
(Arc A cross-biome callback). The Sisters' quarrel *is* two fey collectors fighting over the party's debt.

### 10. The Weir-Keeper's Confession — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The keeper of the old weir is a grey, tired man who has watched two river-towns hate
each other across his sluice-gates for thirty years. He waits until the others have gone before he
speaks, low and fast. "They'll tell you it's about water rights. It isn't. Someone's been bleeding both
towns dry and selling the feud back to them. I can prove it. I just can't say it and live here."
**The situation.** The **Weir-Keeper** (Feature/Weir-War) slips the party the truth both towns refuse
to hear: a third party (a merchant cartel, a fey broker, the **Glovewright?**) has profited from the
war for years. A social investigation: gather proof, choose whom to expose, decide if peace serves the party.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Persuasion **DC [13]** to win his full confidence; Investigation **DC [15]** to
corroborate the hidden profiteer; Insight **DC [12]** to judge the keeper isn't himself the profiteer.
**Develops into.** Exposing the profiteer ends the Weir-War — and if the profiteer is the **Glovewright**,
it cracks Arc B open early. The water elemental (Combat 5) stands down when the weir is mended honestly.

### 11. The Smuggler with the Wrong Cargo — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A low, fast boat ties up alongside in the dark, no lantern lit, and a smuggler with a
quick smile and quicker eyes offers to sell you passage, contraband, or silence — your pick. But the
crates in her hold *drip*, and *hum*, and one of them is packed in river-ice around something that glows
a sick luminous green. "Don't ask about that one," she says. "Parts-buyer up north pays triple. No questions."
**The situation.** A roadside-merchant beat on the water (per `MerchantEconomy.TYPES`): the smuggler
carries one rumour and one hook. The glowing cargo is **luminous ichor** — the Hunt's wounded beast,
harvested two biomes early (cross-biome foreshadow, Arc A / the Hunt).
**Foes/DCs.** None (unless the party press too hard → she fights or flees). Insight **DC [13]** to read
what she's hiding; Investigation **DC [15]** on the humming crate to identify the ichor.
**Develops into.** The "*parts-buyer up north*" is the Hunt's market (Arc A) — the fey-wounded beast is
"going home" to pay its tithe, and its parts are being sold along the way. The Huntsman is downstream of this.

### 12. The Drowned-Town Mourners — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** On a spit of land where a river meets the sea, a handful of people in grey stand at the
water's edge at low tide, looking out at nothing — except that when the tide is fully out, the *nothing*
resolves into the broken tops of chimneys, a leaning steeple, the suggestion of a street, all furred
green and barnacle-white. "That's Threnmouth," one says, without turning. "That's home. It drowned in a
night, and the bell still rings."
**The situation.** Survivors of **Threnmouth, the town that drowned** (regional water). They mourn, they
warn, and they *need* something recovered from the drowned town (a relic, a body, the truth of why it
drowned). A social hook into the deepest water-discovery.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Persuasion **DC [12]** to earn the mourners' charts of the drowned streets; Insight
**DC [14]** to learn they suspect the drowning was *owed*, not natural.
**Develops into.** Threnmouth drowned paying a debt to the deep — a **Tithe** rehearsal in water (Arc A).
The bell that still rings is the one that counts the party (Arc A). Diving the town = Discovery 16.

## Discovery / site (5)

### 13. The Bell Offshore — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Somewhere out past the surf, a bell is ringing — slow, deep, and patient — and there is
no boat out there to swing it, no buoy, no tower breaking the grey water. The locals don't look up when
it tolls. When you ask, an old woman says only: "It rings for the ones it's counted. Best hope it hasn't
counted you," and goes back to mending her net.
**The situation.** The **drowned bell** of the Ferryman's Debt (Arc A) as a discovery-site: it tolls from
the sea floor (Threnmouth's steeple, or older). Finding it, hearing your own number in its toll, and
deciding whether to *answer* it is the core spine-arc beat in water-country. Extends `BIOME_THEMES[water].site`.
**Foes/DCs.** None at the surface. Perception **DC [14]** to count the *pattern* of the tolls (it counts
the party + one); Arcana **DC [16]** to recognise it as a fey reckoning-device, not a navigation bell.
**Develops into.** The bell counts the party — *and counts one extra* (the **One Who Follows**, Arc F,
in its water key). It is the Tithe's tongue (Arc A); the Ferryman named it; the Kraken speaks through it.

### 14. The Wreck on the Reef — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A ship lies broken across the reef like a gutted fish, its ribs picked clean by the sea,
its hold open to the green light filtering down. Fish school through the captain's cabin. Something in
the hold catches the light — gold, or glass, or bone — and the only thing between you and it is the
water, the depth, and whatever made its home in the dark below the keel.
**The situation.** A classic dive-site discovery: salvage in the hold, a hazard in the depth (Hazard 17,
the depths) and a possible lurker (Combat 3) sheltering in the wreck. The ship's *name* and *cargo*
matter — it may be a Threnmouth boat, a Gilded Company loss, or a Glovewright shipment.
**Foes/DCs.** Athletics (swim) **DC [13]** to reach the hold against the current; Constitution **DC [13]**
to hold breath at depth (Hazard 17); Investigation **DC [14]** for the manifest naming the cargo.
**Develops into.** The manifest reads "*for the Gilded Company*" or bears the **Glovewright's glove-seal**
(Arc B/C) — a rival's loss or a benefactor's secret shipment, here on the reef.

### 15. The Tidal Caves — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A sea-cave mouth yawns in the cliff, half-drowned, and the receding tide invites you in
— but the high-water line on the walls is *over your head*, and the wet sand of the floor is dimpled with
the trickle of water already beginning to return. Deeper in, where the light fails, something pale is
wedged in a crevice, just above where the tide will reach.
**The situation.** A discovery on a *clock* (the tide, Hazard 19): the cave holds something worth taking
— a cached relic, a marooned survivor, a fey-left gift — but the tide returns and drowns the cave on a
hard timer. Greed vs. the water.
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [13]** to spot the cached thing; Survival **DC [14]** to read the tide
and know exactly how long you have; Athletics **DC [15]** to swim out if you cut it too fine.
**Develops into.** The pale thing wedged above the tide-line is a **child's shoe**, or a hazelnut shell,
or a glove — a token left by an arc-NPC (Wrenna / the Sisters / the Glovewright) to be found (Arc A/B/F).

### 16. Diving Drowned Threnmouth — *Discovery · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** At low tide and in good light you go down into the town that drowned. The streets are
still streets, furred green; doors hang open on dark rooms; a child's hoop rests in a doorway where the
current has held it for years. And ahead, the steeple — and in the steeple, the bell, vast and black and
*silent now that you are close enough to touch it.*
**The situation.** The deep-water set-piece (Arc A / regional water): the party reach the **drowned bell**
itself inside Threnmouth's flooded steeple. Why the town drowned, what the bell is for, and whether to
ring/silence/answer it are all here. Underwater exploration with a held-breath / depth hazard and the
bell as centrepiece.
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution **DC [14]** for sustained depth (Hazard 17); Investigation **DC [15]** to read
the town's last day from the frozen tableau; Arcana **DC [16]** at the bell to understand what answering it does.
**Develops into.** The whole spine arc, underwater: the bell counts, the Tithe is owed, Threnmouth was
the **down payment** (Arc A). Ringing it summons the Ferryman; silencing it angers the deep (Combat 7).

### 17. The River's Wrong Current — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** You've followed this river for a day, and something about it has been quietly wrong the
whole time. Now, at a still pool, you can prove it: a dropped leaf, a thread of spit, a thrown twig — all
of them drift *upstream.* Gently, patiently, against every law you know, the water is flowing toward its
own source. Toward the high country. Toward the forest.
**The situation.** A discovery that *is* the cross-biome connectivity made visible (Bible Thread 1): the
river carries the dream — and itself — *upstream toward the forest*. A quiet, eerie, low-combat beat that
hands the party a compass: follow the wrong current and it ends at the threshold.
**Foes/DCs.** Nature/Arcana **DC [15]** to confirm the reversal isn't tide or eddy but *intent*; Survival
**DC [13]** to follow the wrong current upstream without losing it.
**Develops into.** The wrong current is the **Tithe** drawing everything home (Arc A) and the **Shared
Dream** moving on the water (Arc D). Trace it upstream and it ends at the forest. The fey-silver arrow
(the Hunt) points the same way (Arc A).

## Hazard / environmental (5)

### 18. The Turning Tide — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The mudflat crossing was firm and dry an hour ago. Now the channels between the sand-bars
are filling, fast, from a sea that is suddenly *closer*, and the firm sand has gone to sucking porridge
underfoot. The far shore is exactly as far as it was — but the water between you and it is rising at the
pace of a walking man, and gaining.
**The situation.** The water biome's recurring hazard (Bible: "tide as the recurring hazard"). A race
against the incoming tide across a mudflat / causeway / reef. Misjudge it and you're swimming, in your
armour, in a current. This is the clock under Discovery 15 and 16.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [14]** to read the tide and pace the crossing; Athletics **DC [13]** to wade
the deepening channels; Strength **DC [15]** if caught swimming in armour.
**Develops into.** Stranded on a shrinking sand-bar as the water rises, the party glimpse the **bell's
buoy** that isn't there, or a **figure on the far shore raising one hand** (Arc A / Arc F).

### 19. The Depths — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Below the green, the light fails by degrees: gold, to grey, to a blue that presses on
your ears and your chest and your nerve. Your lungs count down. The pressure squeezes. And the dark below
*moves* in ways that might be current, or might be something that has never needed light, rising slow to
see what fell into its sky.
**The situation.** The diving hazard for every underwater site (Discovery 14, 16): breath-hold timers,
pressure, cold, and the psychological weight of the deep. Pairs with any aquatic lurker (Combat 3, 6).
The deeper you go, the closer the **bell** and the **Tithe.**
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution **DC [13 → 16 by depth]** for breath-hold; Wisdom save **DC [12]** vs. the
deep's dread; failure → exhaustion or a forced ascent (leaving the prize behind).
**Develops into.** At the deepest point, the **bell tolls once, very close**, and the party *feel
themselves counted* (Arc A). Or the deep-thing that watched you returns the favour, biding (Combat 6/7).

### 20. The Rogue Wave / Squall — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The horizon astern has gone the colour of slate, and the wind has teeth now, and the
swells that were rolling have begun to *stack.* The first big one lifts the stern and slews the boat
broadside before the helm can answer. The second is bigger. Somewhere in the spray, a line parts with a
crack like a shot.
**The situation.** A storm-at-sea / capsize hazard: keep the boat afloat, keep the crew aboard, keep the
cargo dry — a multi-skill survival beat on a pitching deck. Loss here (a swamped boat, a man overboard)
seeds later beats (the drowned become Arc F candidates).
**Foes/DCs.** Athletics/sailing **DC [15]** to hold the boat to the wind; Dexterity save **DC [13]** to
keep your footing/grip each big wave; Athletics **DC [14]** to recover a crewman overboard.
**Develops into.** Whatever the storm *takes* — a crewman, a passenger, a cargo — washes up later, changed,
on a downstream shore (Arc F). Or the storm drives the boat onto the reef (Discovery 14).

### 21. Fouled Water / the Dream Downstream — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The river-village you're passing has painted white crosses on its doors and strung a rope
across the landing. A woman in grey, hollow-eyed, calls across the water as you drift past — not a plea,
a *warning*: "Don't drink downstream of us. It came up the road and it's going your way too. And the sick
ones — they all dream the same dream. Of a forest. Of going *toward* it."
**The situation.** The **Shared Dream** (Arc D) as a water-borne hazard: drinking downstream of a fouled
source risks the dream-sickness. Avoid the bad water (Survival), carry medicine in (Sister Maready's
need), or trace the foulness upstream to its source.
**Foes/DCs.** Nature/Survival **DC [14]** to identify and avoid the tainted reach; Medicine **DC [15]** to
treat early symptoms; a Constitution save **DC [13]** if the party drink before they know.
**Develops into.** The dream all the sick share is *of the forest*, and it moves on the current (Arc D /
Bible Thread 1). Trace it up and it ends at a fouled source — a seed someone planted (Arc D). **Sister
Maready** is a day behind, out of everything (Arc D).

### 22. The Whirlpool / the Pull — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The current under the boat has acquired an opinion. It draws to the right, always the
right, and the river ahead is dimpling into a slow rotation centred on nothing you can see — a smooth,
patient, widening throat of water, and the boat is already leaning into the turn whether the rowers will
it or not. The pull, you realise, is *toward the bell.*
**The situation.** The **Ferryman's Debt** as a navigation hazard (Arc A): the current itself tugs the
party "a hand's-width toward" the drowned bell / the source / the forest. Resist it (Strength rowing),
ride it knowingly, or be drawn to the bell whether you like it or not.
**Foes/DCs.** Strength (rowing) **DC [15]** to hold a true course against the pull; Survival **DC [13]** to
chart a line that uses the eddy instead of fighting it.
**Develops into.** Yield to the pull and it delivers the party to the **bell** (Discovery 13/16) — the
river collecting them, gently, the way the forest will (Arc A). The drowned bell remembers a debt.

## Puzzle / skill-challenge (3)

### 23. The Ferryman's Wordless Crossing — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The Ferryman waits at the far bank, pole grounded, and will not bring the boat across
until the rite is observed — though no one ever told you the rite. There are rules to this crossing
written nowhere: how to board, what to carry, what to leave, what *not* to offer. He watches, patient as
stone, while you work out how to be ferried by something that takes only in courtesy.
**The situation.** A folk-horror skill-challenge (Arc A): the crossing has *rules*, and knowing the rule
is half the win. Offer coin and he refuses (the debt isn't coin). Board ungraciously and he won't pole.
Leave a *gift freely given* and he crosses you — but a gift accepted is a claim. Generosity is the only
safe currency, and it is never quite enough.
**Foes/DCs.** Insight **DC [15]** to read the unspoken etiquette; History/Arcana **DC [16]** to know the
old crossing-customs; a *freely given* gift (roleplay, no roll) earns the crossing — at the price of the debt.
**Develops into.** However the party cross, they are now *on the ledger* (Arc A). Cross gladly and the
debt is light; grudge it or cheat it and it doubles, to be paid at the source (Arc A's thesis: give gladly).

### 24. Mending the Weir — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The old weir is a machine of sluice-gates, mill-races, and counterweights, and someone has
jammed it *wrong* — gates that should be open are pinned shut, the mill-race runs backward, and the river
is rising against the whole structure, furious (Combat 5). The Weir-Keeper presses a rusted key into your
hand. "I know how it *should* run. I'm too old to climb it. You're not."
**The situation.** A mechanism puzzle tied to the Weir-War and the water elemental (Combat 5): read the
weir's logic, undo the deliberate sabotage, and set the gates to their true configuration to calm the
river. Doing it *right* (honestly) stands the elemental down; doing it wrong floods a town downstream.
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [15]** to read the weir's intended state; Thieves' Tools/Strength
**DC [14]** to free the jammed gates; a wrong solution floods downstream (a moral cost, not a combat one).
**Develops into.** The sabotage was *deliberate* and *recent* — the hidden profiteer's work (Feature/
Weir-War). Mending the weir honestly exposes them (Arc B if it's the **Glovewright**) and ends the feud.

### 25. Reading the Tides to Reach the Drowned Bell — *Puzzle · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** The drowned steeple is reachable for exactly one window: the lowest tide of the month, at
slack water, in daylight, before the current turns. The mourners' charts disagree with each other; the
moon and the bell's own toll-pattern hint at the true hour; and getting it wrong means the tide returns
while you're inside the steeple, in the dark, with the bell.
**The situation.** A timing/navigation skill-challenge gating Discovery 16: triangulate the one safe dive
window from conflicting clues (tide charts, lunar phase, the bell's toll-count, the mourners' lore).
Succeed and you reach the bell with time to spare; fail and the tide (Hazard 18/19) traps you at depth.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival/Nature **DC [15]** to compute the slack-water window; Investigation **DC [14]** to
reconcile the conflicting charts; History **DC [13]** to weight the mourners' lore correctly. 3 successes
before 2 failures.
**Develops into.** Hit the window and the party stand before the **drowned bell** at slack water (Discovery
16, Arc A) — the spine arc's water-heart, reached by *reading the rules of the place*, folk-horror style.

# FROZEN

> *Cold with a glassy creak underfoot; an aurora that gives your shadow three edges. The dream freezes

## Combat (6)

### 1. The Wolf Pack on the White — *Combat · T1*
**Read-aloud.** You see them before you hear them — wolves are silent on snow — grey shapes flowing
over a drift in a loose crescent, lean past hunger, their breath the only steam in a still white world.
They are not afraid of you. In this country, this season, nothing four-legged is afraid of anything on
two legs anymore.
**The situation.** A pack hunt sharpened by famine: the wolves are *starving* and will press an attack
they'd normally break off. A **Winter Wolf** (lurker) may shepherd them — and winter wolves are
cunning, herding prey toward thin ice (Hazard 18) or a crevasse (Hazard 19).
**Foes/DCs.** 4–6 **Wolf** + 1 **Winter Wolf** (lurker) or 1 **Polar Bear** (apex). Survival **DC [13]**
to read the herding toward a hazard; Animal Handling **DC [15]** to face down rather than fight a
starving pack.
**Develops into.** The wolves flee *uphill, away from something* — the **Wolf-Winter** proper, the great
pack and the wall it runs (Arc C / regional). Even the predators here are running from what's ahead.

### 2. The Winter Wolf Ambush — *Combat · T1–T2 (ambush)*
**Read-aloud.** The drift ahead is the only sheltered line through the pass, which is exactly why the
snow on it is *too smooth* — until it isn't, until a wolf the colour of old ice rises out of the white
where no wolf was, its breath already frosting in a cone that finds the gap in your column. It speaks,
once, in Common, just to watch your face.
**The situation.** A lurker ambush by a winter wolf (intelligent, cruel, cold-breathed). It has chosen
the ground — a chokepoint over thin ice or near a drop. Reading the too-smooth snow denies the
surprise; the wolf will parley *briefly* if it thinks it can lead the party into worse terrain.
**Foes/DCs.** 1–2 **Winter Wolf** (lurker). Perception **DC [14]** to spot the buried ambusher;
Constitution save **DC [13]** vs. the frost-breath cone; Insight **DC [13]** to catch the wolf's lie.
**Develops into.** The wolf serves the **Wolf-Winter's** pack-mind (Arc C / regional) — and knows where
**Cadoc's thinned company** is pinned at the wall (Arc C). It might trade that knowledge for the party
walking into a worse place.

### 3. The Polar Bear at the Seal-Hole — *Combat · T1–T2*
**Read-aloud.** The white bear is the size of a cart and the colour of the country itself, hunched over
a dark hole in the sea-ice where it has been waiting, motionless, for hours. It lifts its head as you
crest the pressure-ridge — black eyes, black nose, a muzzle stained pink — and rises onto its hind legs
to a height that rewrites your sense of how much animal the world can hold.
**The situation.** A solo apex defending a kill or a hunting-hole. The bear isn't malicious — it's
*territorial and enormous.* Back away slowly (Animal Handling) and it may let you pass; crowd it and
it charges. The sea-ice underfoot (Hazard 18) makes this fight lethal in three dimensions.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Polar Bear** (apex, T1) → 2 at T2. Animal Handling **DC [14]** to withdraw without a
charge; Acrobatics **DC [13]** to keep footing on the sea-ice each round; Perception **DC [12]** to spot
weak ice before the bear drives you onto it.
**Develops into.** Frozen *into* the ice near the seal-hole, just under the surface, a **human face** —
eyes open, smiling, breathing slow (Discovery 13 / Arc D). The bear has been guarding more than a meal.

### 4. The Yeti in the Whiteout — *Combat · T2–T3 (ambush)*
**Read-aloud.** The whiteout drops visibility to an arm's length, and that is when you feel watched.
Something moves at the edge of the white — pale, huge, *patient* — and then its eyes find you, and your
muscles lock with a cold that has nothing to do with the wind. The yeti hunts in the blizzard it
belongs to, and it wanted you blind first.
**The situation.** A lurker that *uses the weather* (Hazard 20): the yeti ambushes in a whiteout, its
gaze paralysing. Fighting blind is the challenge. Fire terrifies it (yeti dread fire) — a torch is worth
more than a sword here.
**Foes/DCs.** 1–2 **Yeti** (lurker) → **Abominable Yeti** (lurker/apex, T3). Constitution save
**DC [13]** vs. the **Chilling Gaze** (paralysis); Perception **DC [15]** to track it in the whiteout;
fire imposes disadvantage on it.
**Develops into.** The yeti's lair is hung with **frozen sleepers** it has *collected* but not eaten —
smiling, breathing in time (Arc D). It hoards the dreamers; it doesn't understand why. (Arc D / Bible
Thread 9.)

### 5. The Abominable Yeti at the Wall — *Combat · T3*
**Read-aloud.** The thing that comes down off the ice-wall is a yeti the way an avalanche is a snowball —
white-furred, ice-clawed, taller than two men, and trailing a cold so absolute the moisture freezes out
of the air in a glittering veil around it. It roars, and the sound is the sound the glacier makes when
it calves, and the ground shakes.
**The situation.** A frozen apex (Arc C's country): the Abominable Yeti guards the high pass or the
Wolf-Winter's wall. Its frost-breath and chilling gaze make it a brutal solo. **Cadoc** may already be
fighting it — and losing — when the party arrive (Arc C).
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Abominable Yeti** (apex) + 2 **Winter Wolf** outriders. Constitution save **DC [15]**
vs. the cone of cold and the gaze; Survival **DC [14]** to fight from higher, firmer ground.
**Develops into.** This is the wall where **Sir Cadoc Vane** loses his smirk (Arc C): "There's a thing
up ahead that doesn't care how fine your gear is. I'd take help. I never thought I'd say it."

### 6. The White Dragon's Demesne — *Combat · T3–T4*
**Read-aloud.** The cold here is *organised.* It has a direction, a will, a centre — and the centre is a
shape coiled on a glacier of frozen plunder, white scales rimed with frost, that opens one ice-blue eye
the size of a shield as your shadow crosses its snow. It does not roar. It does not need to. The
temperature simply *drops*, and you understand whose country this is.
**The situation.** The frozen apex predator (Arc C's volcanic-mirror in winter): a **White Dragon** atop
its frozen hoard. Cruel, territorial, low cunning but high malice. It can be fought, evaded across its
glacier (a hazard-laden chase), or *bargained* with (it is greedy, and the deep-cold has its own laws).
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Young White Dragon** (apex, T3) → **Adult White Dragon** (apex, T4) + **Frost Giant**
thrall. Constitution save **DC [15/17]** vs. the cold-breath; the glacier itself is Hazard 19/21.
**Develops into.** The dragon's hoard holds **Gilded Company gear** — Cadoc's losses, or his *band's
bodies* (Arc C). And frozen at the hoard's heart, a **sleeper smiling on a far green country** (Arc D).

## Social / parley (3)

### 7. The Frozen Sentinel — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A figure stands in the pass ahead, perfectly still, facing the way you've come — a
traveller in furs, frost-rimed, one hand raised as if in greeting or warning. You hail them. They don't
answer. They don't move. And as you come close enough to see the smile fixed on the blue lips and the
slow, slow fog of breath still ghosting from the nostrils, you understand they are not dead. They are
*dreaming.*
**The situation.** A frozen-but-living sleeper (Arc D) as a social/horror beat: they're alive, breathing,
*dreaming the Shared Dream*, and if thawed carefully they can be woken — to describe the forest in detail
no one this far north should know. A conversation with a person halfway into the dream.
**Foes/DCs.** Medicine **DC [15]** to thaw without killing them; Insight **DC [13]** to realise they're
alive; if woken, they speak of the forest (Arc D) before the cold or the dream takes them back.
**Develops into.** The woken sleeper finishes sentences **Wrenna** starts (Arc D), describes the same far
green country the plague-sick dream (Arc D / Bible Thread 9). The dream froze them *facing home* — toward
the forest.

### 8. Sir Cadoc Vane, Humbled — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** You find what's left of the Gilded Company camped in the lee of an ice-wall, and it is
*less* than it was — fewer banners, fewer fires, fewer men. Sir Cadoc Vane himself sits apart, polishing
a blade he hasn't used, and when he sees you the old smirk tries to surface and *fails.* "Come to gloat?"
he says. Then, quieter: "There's a thing up ahead that doesn't care how fine your gear is. I'd take help.
I never thought I'd say it."
**The situation.** The **Cadoc humbled** beat (Arc C beat 4): the rival who always got there first, broken
against the cold and the Wolf-Winter's wall. Help him, refuse him, exploit his weakness — the choice bends
the Company's fate and their banners at the forest's edge.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [12]** to gauge his sincerity (it's real); Persuasion **DC [14]** to set
terms for aid; Intimidation to extract concessions (he's desperate enough to give them, and to resent it).
**Develops into.** Help him and the **Company becomes a war-band at the forest's edge** (huge in Arc A's
bargain-by-force ending). **Ilse Vane** waits to make her own offer (Arc C beat 5). Spurn him and he
defects to whoever opposes the party.

### 9. Quartermaster Ilse Vane's Offer — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Cadoc's sister finds you away from the fires, clear-eyed where her brother is wild. "He'll
die for the legend if I let him," she says flatly, "and take the last of us with him. I run the maps, the
supply, the parts of this Company that still work. Get him out of here *alive* — not victorious, alive —
and the Company's strength is yours, at the forest, where you'll need it. Let his pride finish him, and
you'll have made an enemy of the only Vane worth the name."
**The situation.** **Ilse Vane** (Arc C beat 5), the Company's real brain, makes the campaign-shaping
trade: the Company's maps and muscle for the party's help getting Cadoc out alive. A negotiation that
sets the size of the army at the threshold.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [13]** to confirm she means *alive*, not *triumphant*; Persuasion
**DC [14]** to widen the terms; History/War **DC [12]** to grasp what the Company's strength is worth at
the forest.
**Develops into.** Save Cadoc → an allied war-band at the forest (Arc A bargain-by-force). Fail him →
Ilse is a grateful ally *or* a vengeful enemy who blames the party (Arc C / Arc F — Cadoc as the One Who
Follows if spurned).

## Discovery / site (5)

### 10. The Body in the Blue Ice — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The glacier here is clear as bottle-glass, and frozen deep within it, suspended
mid-motion, is a person — arms out, cloak streaming, face turned up — caught in the exact instant of a
fall that happened a hundred years ago, or yesterday; the ice keeps no time. They are perfectly
preserved. They might, if you crack the ice, be perfectly *alive.*
**The situation.** A discovery-site extending `BIOME_THEMES[frozen].site`: a sleeper (Arc D) or an
ancient traveller frozen mid-fall. Cutting them out is a hazard (the ice is a wall, the cold a clock);
what they carry, and whether they wake, is the payoff. Some are dead; some are *dreaming.*
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [13]** to read whether they're dead or dreaming; Tools/Strength
**DC [15]** to extract them intact; Medicine **DC [16]** to revive a dreamer without killing them.
**Develops into.** A dreamer woken here speaks of the forest (Arc D); a corpse carries a **token** of a
prior arc — a glove, a fey-silver arrowhead, a child's shoe (Arc A/B/F). The ice keeps the dead the way
the dream does — *breathing in time* (Bible Thread 9).

### 11. The Smiling Camp — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A camp, intact, in a hollow out of the wind: tents standing, a fire long dead, bedrolls
neatly laid — and in every bedroll a sleeper, frost-furred, smiling, breathing so slowly you have to
watch a long moment to be sure they breathe at all. No struggle. No alarm. They simply lay down one night
and began to dream, and the cold kept them, and the dream never let them up.
**The situation.** The **Shared Dream** as a discovery-site (Arc D / Bible Thread 9): a whole camp frozen
mid-dream, breathing in time. Loot it (grimly), wake one (Social 7), or read the camp's journals for *what
they dreamed* before they lay down. The cold "keeps them breathing in time."
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [13]** for the journals; Medicine **DC [15]** to wake a sleeper; Insight
**DC [12]** to notice they all breathe *in unison*, as if dreaming one dream.
**Develops into.** The journals describe the dream **growing stronger as they travelled north** (Arc D) —
the dream froze them *facing the forest.* The camp is the plague's winter terminus; **Sister Maready**
would weep to find it (Arc D).

### 12. The Aurora-Lit Shrine — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Under an aurora that drapes the sky in slow green fire, a shrine stands on a rise of bare
black rock — old, weathered, its carvings half-eaten by ice, but its central basin still bright with a
rime of frost that catches the aurora and *holds* it, glowing softly from within long after a curtain of
light has passed. Your shadow, in the aurora's triple light, has three edges.
**The situation.** A discovery-site keyed to the frozen biome's signature (triple-edged shadows, aurora):
an old shrine to the cold, or to whatever the herd-folk once placated. The frost-basin is a minor wonder
(it stores light); the carvings hint at the dream's *rules* (Puzzle 23). A place of eerie, quiet awe.
**Foes/DCs.** Religion/History **DC [15]** to read the carvings' meaning; Arcana **DC [14]** to understand
the light-holding frost; Perception **DC [13]** to notice the three-edged shadow phenomenon and what casts true.
**Develops into.** The carvings name the **rule of the cold** — the same "owe a death" logic the herd-folk
keep in the tundra (Arc D / Bible Thread 9), the **Tithe** in a colder dialect (Arc A).

### 13. The Frozen Falls — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A waterfall hangs frozen in mid-plunge, a vast organ of blue-white ice pipes, and behind
its translucent curtain there is a *space* — a cave, a hollow, a dark mouth the falls have curtained off
for who knows how long. Through the ice, distorted, you can make out shapes back there. Some of them might
be stored. Some of them might be waiting.
**The situation.** A discovery behind a frozen waterfall: a hidden cave (Hazard-gated by the ice curtain),
sheltering a cache, a lair (Combat 4, the yeti), or a sleeper-hoard (Arc D). The frozen falls is both
landmark and door. Breaking through is a hazard; what's behind is the reward.
**Foes/DCs.** Strength **DC [14]** to breach the ice curtain (or Survival **DC [13]** to find the natural
gap); Perception **DC [13]** to read the distorted shapes before committing.
**Develops into.** Behind the falls: a yeti's **collection of dreamers** (Combat 4 / Arc D), or a fey
cache left by the **Two Sisters / Glovewright** (Arc A/B), or the **Wolf-Winter's** den-mouth (Arc C).

### 14. The Three-Edged Shadow — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** It's the aurora that does it — three curtains of light at three angles, so that every
upright thing throws three faint shadows, fanned like the spokes of a broken wheel. You've grown used to
it. But here, in the lee of a drift, one of the three shadows belongs to *nothing you can see* — a fourth
edge, where there should be three, cast by something standing just out of the light beside you.
**The situation.** A discovery-as-omen tied hard to the biome signature: the aurora's triple shadow
reveals a *fourth* — the **One Who Follows** (Arc F), or a fey watcher, made briefly visible by the trick
of the light. A quiet, skin-crawling beat. Investigate the extra shadow's source; it won't be there when
you turn.
**Foes/DCs.** Perception **DC [15]** to fix the fourth shadow's origin before it fades; Arcana **DC [16]**
to understand the aurora reveals what walks unseen; Insight **DC [13]** to sense it means greeting, not threat.
**Develops into.** The fourth shadow is the **follower** (Arc F) — the party's number plus one, made
visible by the cold light. It thickens toward the forest (Arc F). Or it's a fey escort, confirming the
party are *expected* (Arc A).

## Hazard / environmental (7)

### 15. The Crevasse Field — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The snow ahead looks unbroken — a smooth white plain, easy walking. But the smoothness is
a lie the wind tells: beneath the crust, the glacier is split with crevasses, blue-black throats fifty
feet deep, bridged over with snow that will hold a bird, or a rabbit, or nothing at all the weight of a
booted foot.
**The situation.** The frozen biome's signature hazard: hidden crevasses under snow-bridges. Probe, rope
up, and cross slow — or trust the smooth snow and fall. This is where the winter wolves and yetis *herd*
you (Combat 1, 2, 4).
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** to read safe lines and probe the bridges; Dexterity save **DC [14]** if
a bridge gives; Athletics **DC [13]** (roped) to self-arrest or haul a fallen companion out.
**Develops into.** Down a crevasse, wedged on a ledge: a **body**, a **cache**, or a **way in** to an
ice-cave (Discovery 13) — and frozen beside it, sometimes, a *smiling sleeper* (Arc D).

### 16. The Whiteout — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The blizzard arrives not as wind but as *erasure*: the horizon goes first, then the middle
distance, then your own outstretched hand, until the world is a roaring grey-white nothing with no up, no
down, no forward, and no sound but the wind screaming the loss of every landmark you were navigating by.
**The situation.** A visibility-and-navigation hazard (Bible: frozen is hazard-dominant). Hold the
rope-line, shelter and wait, or push blind and risk walking in circles — or off a crevasse (Hazard 15), or
into a yeti (Combat 4). The whiteout is the yeti's *ally.*
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [16]** to hold a true bearing or build snow-shelter; Constitution save
**DC [13]** vs. the cold if caught exposed; a failed navigation loops the party past their own tracks.
**Develops into.** Out of the whiteout, briefly, a **figure standing motionless, one hand raised** (Arc F /
the Frozen Sentinel, Social 7) — gone when the snow thickens again. Or the party stumble onto the Smiling
Camp (Discovery 11).

### 17. Killing Cold / Exposure — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** There is no wind, no storm, no drama — only the cold, vast and patient and total, a cold
that is not an event but a *condition*, leaching the warmth from your core hour by hour no matter how you
walk or what you wear. Fingers stop answering. Thinking goes slow and sweet and dangerous. The cold does
not need to hurry. It has already won, if you let it.
**The situation.** The fundamental frozen hazard: ambient lethal cold as an attrition clock (Bible:
"exposure"). Fuel, shelter, and movement discipline against the slow steal of heat. The cold is also what
*keeps the sleepers breathing* (Arc D) — it preserves as it kills.
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution saves **DC [escalating 12 → 15 → 18]** at intervals without adequate warmth,
exhaustion on failure; Survival **DC [14]** to build a proper cold-camp; the "slow sweet thinking" is the
dream's doorway (Arc D).
**Develops into.** Let the cold take you toward sleep and the **dream begins** (Arc D) — the forest, green
and warm and waiting. The frozen sleepers all started here. The cold and the dream are the same mercy
(Bible Thread 9).

### 18. The Thin Ice — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The fastest way across is the frozen lake — a flat white road where there used to be deep
water. But the ice *talks*: it creaks, it groans, it sends long cracking booms racing out from under your
boots like something below testing the lid. In places it's clear enough to see the black water moving,
patient, a hand's-width beneath your weight.
**The situation.** A frozen-water-crossing hazard: ice of uncertain thickness over deep, killing-cold
water. Read it, spread your weight, cross roped — or break through into water that stops a heart in
minutes. This is where the **Polar Bear** (Combat 3) and **Winter Wolf** (Combat 1) drive their prey.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [14]** to read safe ice and pace the crossing; Dexterity save **DC [13]** to
react if it cracks; Constitution save **DC [15]** vs. cold-shock if you go through; **DC [14]** Athletics to
self-rescue.
**Develops into.** Through the ice, in the green-black water, a **face looking up** — eyes open, not
struggling, *smiling* (Arc D). The ice is a window onto the drowned-and-dreaming.

### 19. The Avalanche Slope — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The pass climbs beneath a loaded slope — tons of snow hung at an angle that wants to fall,
held by nothing but cold and luck and the silence you are all suddenly, instinctively keeping. A raised
voice could bring it down. A dragon's roar (Combat 6) certainly would. The white weight waits above you,
patient as everything else in this country.
**The situation.** A triggered-collapse hazard: cross the loaded slope without setting it off (sound,
vibration, a misstep). The dragon (Combat 6) or the abominable yeti (Combat 5) can *weaponise* it. Outrun
it, ride it, or shelter behind a buttress.
**Foes/DCs.** Stealth/Survival **DC [14]** to cross without triggering it; Athletics/Acrobatics **DC [15]**
to outrun or ride the slide if it goes; Strength **DC [13]** to dig free if buried.
**Develops into.** The avalanche *uncovers* as it settles: a frozen camp (Discovery 11), an ice-cave mouth
(Discovery 13), or the **Gilded Company's** lost baggage-train (Arc C).

### 20. Aurora-Madness / the Sky's Wrong Light — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The aurora tonight is too much — not the slow green drapery of other nights but a writhing,
pulsing, *purposeful* light that seems to keep time with something, that throws your trebled shadow into a
dance, that whispers at the very edge of hearing in a language the back of your skull almost knows. Stare
too long and you forget to be cold. You forget to move. You forget.
**The situation.** A psychological/exposure hazard unique to the frozen sky: the aurora lulls, like the
dream (Arc D). Watching it is hypnotic and warm-feeling and *fatal* (it stops you moving while the cold
works). The triple shadow (Discovery 14) and the dream (Arc D) braid here.
**Foes/DCs.** Wisdom save **DC [14]** to look away and keep moving; Constitution save **DC [13]** if the
lull holds you still in the cold; Arcana **DC [15]** to recognise the aurora is *carrying* the dream.
**Develops into.** The aurora's whisper is the **dream's voice** (Arc D), and its light reveals the
**fourth shadow** (Discovery 14 / Arc F). The sky here is a curtain the forest is reaching through (Arc A).

### 21. The Glacier's Calving / Shifting Ice — *Hazard · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** The ice underfoot is not the still, dead thing it pretends to be — it *moves*, grinding
downhill an inch an hour, and where it meets the sea or the cliff-edge it sheds in catastrophes: a crack
like the world splitting, and a tower of ice the size of a keep shearing away into the water or the void
below, throwing up a wave of cold and a wind of pulverised ice.
**The situation.** A large-scale environmental hazard for the dragon's glacier (Combat 6) or the high
passes: the living ice cracks, shifts, and calves without warning, opening new crevasses (Hazard 15) and
shearing the route away. Time the crossing; don't be on the calving edge.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** to read the ice's stress and time the crossing; Dexterity save
**DC [14]** to leap clear of an opening crack; Athletics **DC [15]** to reach stable ice as a section calves.
**Develops into.** The calving exposes the **dragon's hoard** (Combat 6) or a **sealed ice-cave** (Discovery
13), and drops the party toward the **void**, which is one thin step to the left of here (Arc A's threshold,
felt early).

## Puzzle / skill-challenge (3)

### 22. Waking the Sleeper Without Killing Them — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The dreamer in the ice is alive — barely, slowly, smiling — and you mean to wake them. But
thaw them too fast and the cold-shock will stop the heart that's beating once a minute; too slow and the
cold takes them first; and all the while the *dream* has its hooks in them, and a part of them does not
want to come back from the green warm country behind their eyes.
**The situation.** A multi-discipline skill-challenge (Arc D): warm them at the right rate (Medicine),
keep the heart going (Medicine/Survival), and *call them back from the dream* (Persuasion/a personal
anchor) — all at once, against three clocks. Success yields a witness who's *seen the forest.*
**Foes/DCs.** Medicine **DC [15]** to manage the thaw; Survival **DC [13]** to stage warmth correctly;
Persuasion/Insight **DC [14]** to anchor them against the dream's pull. Stage successes; rushing any one fails another.
**Develops into.** A woken sleeper is an **eyewitness to the forest** (Arc D) who finishes Wrenna's
sentences (Arc D / Bible Thread 9) — and may beg to be let go *back* into the dream, the first sign of how
the Tithe seduces (Arc A).

### 23. Reading the Cold's Rule — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The aurora-shrine's carvings (Discovery 12), the herd-folk's warnings, the pattern of who
the cold takes and who it spares — they all circle one rule, written nowhere whole. The sleepers were not
chosen at random. The cold has *terms*, the way the fog does, the way the river does. Work out the rule,
and you can walk this country without lying down to dream.
**The situation.** A folk-horror deduction puzzle (Arc D / Bible Thread 9): assemble the cold's *rule*
from carvings, survivor-lore, and the evidence of the frozen (who, when, facing where). Knowing the rule
is half the win — it lets the party avoid the dream, wake sleepers safely (Puzzle 22), and treat the cold
as a *bargain* rather than a death.
**Foes/DCs.** Religion/History **DC [16]** to read the shrine; Investigation **DC [15]** to find the
pattern in who froze; Insight **DC [13]** to interpret the herd-folk's oblique warnings. Assemble 3 clues
to derive the rule.
**Develops into.** The cold's rule is the **Tithe in a colder dialect** (Arc A / Bible Thread 9) — "owe a
death," the same logic the tundra herd-folk keep. Knowing it lets the party *negotiate* the forest's
bargain later from a position of understanding (Arc A's thesis).

### 24. Crossing the Glacier — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The glacier between you and the pass is a maze written in white: crevasse-fields you can't
see (Hazard 15), thin spots over melt-pools, calving edges (Hazard 21), and snow-bridges that hold or
don't. There is a safe line through it. There is also a dozen lines that end in a fifty-foot fall or a
heart-stopping plunge. Reading the right one is the whole task.
**The situation.** A navigation skill-challenge stitching the frozen hazards together (15, 18, 19, 21):
read the glacier's surface, probe the bridges, time the calving, and thread the one safe route. The cold
(Hazard 17) is the clock; the predators (Combat 1–6) are the ambush if you stall.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** per leg to read safe ice; Investigation **DC [14]** to interpret
surface-cues (sag, colour, sound); Athletics **DC [13]** for the roped crossings. 3 successes before 2 failures.
**Develops into.** Threaded right, the glacier delivers the party to the **wall** (Combat 5 / Arc C, where
Cadoc waits) or to the **void's edge** (Arc A's threshold) — the frozen country tips, at its top, into the
place that is "one thin step to the left."

# TUNDRA

> *Flat white where distance lies and the eye finds no scale; fog that moves the ground beneath it. What
> Walks in the Fog hunts here, and it has rules, and it always takes one. Always one.*

**Target counts:** Combat 5 · Social 5 · Discovery 4 · Hazard 6 · Puzzle 4 — **24 total.**
**Arc anchors:** **What Walks in the Fog** and its counting-rules (regional tundra); the
**Reindeer-Herder** and the herd-folk's terrible hospitality (Arc D / "owe a death"); the **fog-walker**
that can be *honoured* (folk-horror, Bible Section); the **Shared Dream** carried north in the fog (Arc
D / Bible Thread 9); the herd-folk's "owe a death" as the **Tithe** in a colder dialect (Arc A).

## Combat (5)

### 1. The Worg Pack Out of the Fog — *Combat · T1*
**Read-aloud.** The fog ahead thickens, thins, and *delivers* them — worgs, big as ponies and twice as
mean, materialising out of the white at a dead run with no warning bark, no scent on the still air, just
the sudden drum of paws and the yellow intelligence in their eyes. They've hunted in this fog before.
They know it hides them. They know it hides the ground.
**The situation.** A pack hunt that *uses the fog* (Hazard 17): the worgs appear and vanish in the white,
striking from blindsides. They may be driving the party toward a fog-hidden hazard (a bog, a drop) — or
toward the fog-walker's reach. Fighting half-blind is the challenge.
**Foes/DCs.** 4–6 **Worg** + 1 **Winter Wolf** (lurker) or 1 **Saber-Toothed Tiger** (lurker). Perception
**DC [14]** to track them in the fog and deny their blindside strikes; Survival **DC [13]** to spot what
they're herding you toward.
**Develops into.** The worgs break off at an unseen line in the fog and *will not cross it* — the
**fog-walker's** territory (regional tundra). Even the worgs honour the rule. Whatever they fear, the
party are about to enter (Arc D).

### 2. The Saber-Tooth Ambush — *Combat · T1–T2 (ambush)*
**Read-aloud.** The fog plays tricks with sound and shape, so you don't see the cat until it's already
moving — a saber-toothed tiger, low and tawny and silent, that has been pacing your column from a parallel
line in the white, waiting for the fog to thin between it and the smallest of you. Its first leap clears
fifteen feet and you never heard it coil.
**The situation.** A lurker ambush amplified by the fog: the cat uses the white as cover, striking from an
unexpected angle. Reading its parallel track in the soft ground denies surprise; otherwise it gets a
brutal opening pounce.
**Foes/DCs.** 1–2 **Saber-Toothed Tiger** (lurker). Perception **DC [15]** to catch its parallel track in
the fog; Survival **DC [13]** to read the soft ground for its approach; Athletics **DC [13]** to break its grapple-pounce.
**Develops into.** The cat's tracks, when you backtrack them, run beside *another* set — bipedal,
barefoot, the party's number *plus one* (Arc F). Something else is pacing the column too.

### 3. The Mammoth's Charge — *Combat · T2–T3*
**Read-aloud.** The grey hill in the fog ahead is not a hill. It resolves, as you approach, into a wall of
shaggy flesh and curved ivory taller than a house — a mammoth, and behind it the dim bulk of its herd,
and it has decided you are close enough to its calves to warrant a warning. The warning is a charge that
shakes the frozen ground and parts the fog before it.
**The situation.** A megafauna encounter that's *territorial, not predatory*: the mammoth charges to
drive the party off, not to eat them. Back away (Animal Handling) and the herd lets you pass; provoke or
corner it and you fight something that weighs as much as the boat you came on. The fog hides the herd's true size.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Mammoth** (apex) → 2–3 at T3 (a herd). Animal Handling **DC [15]** to withdraw without a
charge; Acrobatics **DC [14]** to evade the charge-line; Survival **DC [13]** to route around the herd in the fog.
**Develops into.** The herd is being **driven** — by worgs (Combat 1), by the fog-walker, or by the
**Reindeer-Herder's** people moving their animals ahead of something (Arc D). What moves the mammoths is the hook.

### 4. The Frost Giant Hunter — *Combat · T2–T3*
**Read-aloud.** A figure stands in the fog the size of a watchtower, and it is *hunting* — a frost giant,
blue-skinned and rime-bearded, dragging a sledge piled with frozen kills and casting about for more. It
hasn't seen you yet. The fog that hides it from you hides you from it. But its dire wolves have your scent,
and they are already circling wide through the white.
**The situation.** An apex hunter with a pack: the frost giant works the tundra with **Winter Wolf** or
**Worg** hounds. It can be avoided (the fog cuts both ways), parleyed (giants have honour and hungers), or
fought (a brutal reach-and-boulder battle in poor visibility). It may *also* be hunting the fog-walker.
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Frost Giant** (apex) + 2–3 **Winter Wolf** / **Worg** hounds. Stealth **DC [14]** to slip
its hounds in the fog; Persuasion **DC [15]** to parley (offer a kill, a tale, tribute); Constitution save
**DC [13]** if a thrown boulder lands.
**Develops into.** The giant's sledge of kills includes things *killed but not eaten, smiling* — it's been
clearing the fog-walker's frozen dreamers (Arc D), or hunting the thing that makes them. Its lore is the hook.

### 5. The White Dragon in the Fog — *Combat · T3–T4*
**Read-aloud.** The cold deepens past reason, and the fog ahead begins to *swirl* — not on the wind, but
around a centre, drawn in toward a long pale shape that the white had hidden completely until you were far
too close. An ice-blue eye opens at head-height. The fog, you understand, was never weather. It was
*breath.*
**The situation.** The tundra's rare apex (shared with frozen): a **White Dragon** whose cold-breath *is*
the fog that's plagued the country. Killing it might lift the fog; it might also be *holding back* something
worse. Cruel, greedy, low cunning — fightable, evadable in its own fog, or bargainable (it hoards).
**Foes/DCs.** 1 **Young White Dragon** (apex, T3) → **Adult White Dragon** (apex, T4) + **Frost Giant**
thrall. Constitution save **DC [15/17]** vs. the breath; the fog gives the dragon constant concealment
(Perception **DC [16]** to pin it).
**Develops into.** If the dragon's breath *is* the fog, the **fog-walker** and the dragon are entangled (Arc
D / regional) — and killing one may free or unleash the other. The dragon's hoard holds **herd-folk
tribute** and a **smiling sleeper** (Arc D).

## Social / parley (5)

### 6. The Reindeer-Herder's Fire — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A fire burns small and bright in the great white nothing, and beside it a weathered herder
in reindeer-hide raises a hand — not in warning, in welcome. "Sit," he says. "Eat. You're safe at my fire."
And then, as you settle, gravely, the one condition: "Whatever you hear out there in the fog — whatever
voice, whatever face — you do not step beyond the firelight. It takes one. Always one. Stay in the light
and it takes nobody tonight."
**The situation.** The **Reindeer-Herder** (Arc D) and the herd-folk's *terrible hospitality*: genuine
warmth and safety, on one absolute rule — don't leave the firelight in the fog. The fog-walker (regional
tundra) will *test* the rule tonight (Hazard/Puzzle). A warm, dread-laced social anchor.
**Foes/DCs.** None at the fire. Insight **DC [12]** to know the herder means the rule *exactly*; History
**DC [14]** to learn the "owe a death" custom behind the hospitality; Persuasion to learn what the fog *is.*
**Develops into.** The herder's "it takes one, always one" is the **Tithe in a colder dialect** (Arc A /
Bible Thread 9). The fog-walker can be *honoured* (folk-horror) — and the herder knows how (Puzzle 22). The
**Shared Dream** rode the fog here (Arc D).

### 7. The Voice in the Fog — *Social/Hazard · any tier (at the herder's fire)*
**Read-aloud.** Past midnight, the fog presses close against the firelight, and out of it comes a voice you
*know* — a companion's voice, or a lost friend's, or your own name in a tone that turns your stomach with
how right it sounds — calling, just past the edge of the light, asking you to come, just a few steps, just
to see. The herder does not look up. "It always knows a voice you'll follow," he murmurs. "Stay seated."
**The situation.** The **fog-walker's** test (regional tundra), run as a social/will beat: it calls in a
voice you trust, from just beyond the firelight, and *every step toward it is the trap.* Resisting is the
encounter. If anyone steps out, it takes them (and the night's "one" is paid — Puzzle/Hazard cascade).
**Foes/DCs.** Wisdom save **DC [15]** to keep your seat against the called voice; Insight **DC [14]** to know
the voice is *bait*, however perfect; the herder's rule is the only sure protection.
**Develops into.** The voice it uses is sometimes one the party **lost earlier on the road** (Arc F) — the
fog-walker and the **One Who Follows** share a hunger. Honour the fog-walker's rule (Puzzle 22) and the
voice goes quiet (Arc D).

### 8. The Herd on the Move — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Out of the fog comes a whole people on the move — a long line of reindeer and laden sledges
and bundled figures, herd-folk driving their animals north at a pace that speaks of fear, not season.
Their leader reins in at your hail, eyes flicking back the way they've come. "We don't stop in open fog,"
she says. "Something's behind us that wasn't, last moon. Travel with us or stand aside, but don't ask us to stop."
**The situation.** A migrating herd-folk band (Arc D), fleeing the fog-walker's worsening reach — or the
Shared Dream, or the Wolf-Winter pressing up from the boreal. A social beat on the move: trade, news,
shelter-in-numbers, and a hook about *what's behind them.* Echoes the desert's fleeing caravan.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Persuasion **DC [13]** to win a place in their column; Insight **DC [14]** to gauge what
they're truly fleeing; History **DC [12]** to learn their customs (and the "owe a death" rule) on the march.
**Develops into.** What the herd flees is the **fog-walker grown bolder** (Arc D) or the **dream taking more
each moon** (Bible Thread 9). Their destination — where the fog *doesn't* reach — is a hook toward a refuge,
or toward the forest itself.

### 9. The Wayfarer in the White — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A lone figure sits cross-legged in the open fog, perfectly calm, a child's shoe on a cord at
her throat, looking north into the white as if she can see something in it you can't. She doesn't startle as
you loom out of the murk. "It counts, you know," she says, not turning. "The fog. The cold. The forest at
the end of all of it. They all count, and they all collect. I'm only further along the road than you."
**The situation.** **Wrenna** (Arcs A/D/F) in the tundra, further north on her endless road than any mortal
should be. She speaks of the fog, the dream, and the Tithe as one thing — *counting and collecting* — and
of the forest at the end. A conversation, a warning, the spine arc in the herder's cold dialect.
**Foes/DCs.** None. Insight **DC [13]** to feel her grief is older than a lifetime; Arcana/History
**DC [16]** to grasp she's walked this road before; she gives the fog-walker's rule freely if asked kindly.
**Develops into.** "They all count, and they all collect." Wrenna ties the **fog-walker**, the **dream**, and
the **Tithe** into one law (Arc A / D / Bible Thread 9). "Give gladly, or pay double." She is the barometer.

### 10. The Fog-Walker Honoured — *Social · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The herder leads you, before dawn, to the edge of the firelight, and there in the fog the
*shape* waits — tall, indistinct, patient, neither advancing nor retreating, a courtesy in its very
stillness. "It is owed," the herder says quietly. "Every camp owes it. You can pay in fear and lose one of
your own to the dark — or you can pay in *gift*, freely given, and it will let the night pass. It always
takes. The only choice is *how*."
**The situation.** The folk-horror heart of the tundra (regional / Bible Section): the **fog-walker** can be
*honoured.* It is owed a death — but a *freely given gift* (food, a tale, a treasured thing, a memory) can
stand in its place, the way the Ferryman's crossing can. Knowing the rule is the whole win. A bargain
offered, not a fight.
**Foes/DCs.** Insight **DC [14]** to read the courtesy in its stillness; a *freely given* gift (roleplay, no
roll) satisfies it; *grudging* the payment costs double (it takes one anyway). Religion/History **DC [15]**
to perform the honouring rite correctly.
**Develops into.** The fog-walker honoured is the **Tithe rehearsed** (Arc A): give gladly and it lets the
night — and the party — pass; grudge it and it takes one, doubled, from someone they care about. The same
law the forest keeps (Arc A's thesis, Bible Thread 9).

## Discovery / site (4)

### 11. The Counting-Stones — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A line of standing stones marches off into the fog, evenly spaced, and each one is scored
with tally-marks — clusters of five, hundreds of them, thousands, cut by countless hands over countless
years. The newest cuts are fresh. At the base of the nearest stone, a small offering: a carved bone, a
twist of dried meat, a child's mitten, left where the fog will find it.
**The situation.** A discovery-site that *is* the fog-walker's ledger (regional tundra / extends
`BIOME_THEMES[tundra].site`): the herd-folk tally the nights paid and the ones taken. Reading the stones
reveals the *rules* (Puzzle 22) and the long, grim arithmetic of "it takes one, always one." The offerings
show how it's honoured (Social 10).
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [13]** to read the tally-system; History **DC [15]** to grasp the count's
meaning and antiquity; Insight **DC [12]** to understand the offerings are *payment*, not memorial.
**Develops into.** The counting-stones are the **fog-walker's ledger** — bark-and-bone kin to the
**Collector's** and the **Tithe-Warden's** (Arc A/E). The same account, kept in the cold. The newest tally
might bear *the party's number* (Arc A/F).

### 12. The Fog-Drowned Camp — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** A herd-folk camp stands abandoned in the white — tents up, fire cold, sledges still laden,
a meal half-eaten and frozen in the bowls. There's no sign of struggle, no blood, no bodies. Only, in the
soft ground around the dead fire, footprints: the whole camp's worth, all leading *outward*, into the fog,
in a slow procession that does not come back.
**The situation.** A discovery-site of a camp the fog-walker *took whole* (Arc D) — or a camp that broke the
rule and walked out, one after another, into the white. Eerie, bloodless, total. Salvage the laden sledges;
read the tracks for *why they all left the light.*
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [14]** to read the outward tracks and the camp's last night; Survival
**DC [13]** to follow the procession's trail (into the fog, toward something); Insight **DC [13]** to sense
they walked *willingly*, called.
**Develops into.** The camp followed a *voice in the fog* (Social 7) out into the white, one by one — the
fog-walker took the whole camp because no one stayed in the light (Arc D). Their tracks lead toward the
**counting-stones** (Discovery 11) or the **dream's source** (Arc D / Bible Thread 9).

### 13. The Smiling Drift — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Half-buried in a fresh drift, the fog still clinging to the hollow, lies a single
traveller — face up, frost-furred, eyes open on the white sky, *smiling*, the slow fog of breath still
rising from parted lips. They went down in the fog, alone, and the cold caught them mid-dream, and the
white has been gently, patiently covering them ever since.
**The situation.** The **Shared Dream's** winter form (Arc D / Bible Thread 9) as a tundra discovery: a lone
dreamer the fog and cold took, smiling at the forest behind their eyes. Wake them (Puzzle/Medicine), read
what they carry, or simply understand — the fog carries the dream north, and lays the dreamers down where
they fall.
**Foes/DCs.** Medicine **DC [15]** to wake them without killing them; Insight **DC [13]** to realise they're
alive and dreaming; Investigation **DC [13]** for what they carry (a token, a journal, a fog-walker offering).
**Develops into.** A woken dreamer speaks of the forest (Arc D), finishes **Wrenna's** sentences (Bible
Thread 9). The fog *is* the dream's carrier here (Arc D) — and the fog-walker may be its hunter, or its
shepherd (regional tundra).

### 14. The Place Where the Ground Moves — *Discovery · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The fog here does the impossible thing it only hints at elsewhere: it *moves the ground.*
A landmark you fixed an hour ago — a black rock, a lone stunted tree — is somehow behind you now, or to the
left, or gone. The flat white reorders itself in the murk, distance lying outright, and the only fixed
thing is a low cairn that you are fairly sure you have now passed three times.
**The situation.** A discovery-as-phenomenon keyed to the tundra signature ("fog that moves the ground
beneath it"): in the fog-walker's deepest reach, space comes loose — a rehearsal of the **void's**
distance-lies (Arc A's threshold, felt early). The cairn is the one true fixed point; reading *why* it holds
still is the discovery.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** to notice the landscape is reordering, not your navigation failing;
Arcana **DC [16]** to recognise the fog bends space the way the void does; Investigation **DC [13]** at the
cairn for why it stays fixed.
**Develops into.** The moving ground is the **fog-walker's domain** (regional tundra) and a foretaste of the
**void/forest threshold** where "distance and direction come loose" (Arc A). The fixed cairn is a herd-folk
anchor against it — and points the only true way through.

## Hazard / environmental (6)

### 15. The Fog That Moves the Ground — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The fog isn't just blinding; it's *dishonest.* You walk a straight line and arrive where you
started. You fix a landmark and it migrates. North stops meaning north. The flat white tundra, featureless
to begin with, becomes under the fog a place where distance and direction quietly come unmoored, and the
only thing you can trust is the rope between you and the next person — and even that, sometimes, seems longer than it was.
**The situation.** The tundra's signature hazard (Bible: "the fog IS the hazard"): navigation fails not by
your error but by the fog's *intent* (regional fog-walker). Hold the rope-line, anchor on the counting-stones
(Discovery 11) or the fixed cairn (Discovery 14), and move slow — or walk in circles into the fog-walker's reach.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [16]** to hold a true bearing against the bending fog; Wisdom save **DC [13]** to
trust the rope over your lying eyes; failure loops the party past their own tracks, deeper in.
**Develops into.** Lost long enough in the fog, the party hear the **voice** (Social 7) calling from the
white, and the **boot-prints** of the One Who Follows appear in their own (Arc F). The fog delivers them to
the fog-walker (Arc D).

### 16. The Called Voice — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Out in the fog, someone is calling you. Not a stranger — *you know this voice.* It uses your
name, or a pet-name only one person ever used, in a tone of such warmth and need that your feet have turned
toward it before your mind has voted. It's close. Just past the white. Just a few steps off the path, and
you'll see them, and everything will be all right.
**The situation.** The **fog-walker's** lure (regional tundra) as a standalone compulsion-hazard (Social 7
formalised): the fog calls in a voice you trust, and *every step toward it strays you fatally.* Resist
(Wisdom), tie yourself to the line, and do not leave the path. The lure is strongest for whoever's lost
someone (Arc F bait).
**Foes/DCs.** Wisdom save **DC [15]** to resist following the voice (disadvantage if it's someone you've
lost); Insight **DC [14]** to recognise the bait; a roped line and a steady companion grant advantage.
**Develops into.** Whoever follows the voice off the path is *taken* (Arc D), paying the night's "one"
(Social 6/10). The voice the fog chooses is sometimes the party's **own dead** (Arc F). Honouring the
fog-walker (Social 10) silences it.

### 17. Whiteout on the Flat — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** On the open tundra a whiteout is total in a way it never is in the mountains — no slope to
orient by, no rock, no tree, nothing but a horizonless grey-white void in every direction, ground and sky
the same blank nothing, so that you cannot tell if you are walking uphill or down or in a slow circle, and
the wind erases your tracks behind you as fast as you make them.
**The situation.** A featureless-terrain whiteout (compounding Hazard 15): on the flat there is *nothing* to
navigate by, and the fog's ground-moving makes it worse. Shelter and wait, or rope-line and crawl on a
compass-bearing you can't verify. The whiteout is when the fog-walker (Hazard 16) and the worgs (Combat 1) hunt.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** to hold a bearing with no landmarks; Constitution save **DC [13]** vs. the
cold if caught exposed; Wisdom save **DC [12]** against the disorientation-panic.
**Develops into.** The whiteout lifts to reveal the party have wandered to the **counting-stones** (Discovery
11), the **fog-drowned camp** (Discovery 12), or *back to the herder's cold fire* — which is empty now, a
single set of tracks leading out (Arc D).

### 18. The Hidden Bog — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** Under the snow and the fog the tundra hides its meltwater — a skin of frozen moss over black,
half-frozen muskeg that looks exactly like solid ground until your leg punches through to the thigh in
water cold enough to stop a heart, and the more you struggle the more the cold mud takes hold and the more
the fog presses down to watch you sink.
**The situation.** A fog-hidden bog/muskeg hazard: thawing ground beneath the white, invisible until you're
in it, lethally cold and grasping. Probe ahead, spread weight, rope up — or be driven into it by the worgs
(Combat 1) or the fog's bad navigation (Hazard 15).
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [14]** to spot and avoid the bog under the snow; Strength (Athletics) **DC [15]**
to haul a sinking companion free; Constitution save **DC [14]** vs. the cold-immersion if you go in deep.
**Develops into.** What the bog has *preserved*, surfacing as you flounder: an ancient body, a herd-folk
offering-sledge, a **fog-walker tribute** sunk generations ago (Discovery 11 / Arc D).

### 19. The Cold and the Thin Air — *Hazard · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** It is not the dramatic cold of the high glaciers — it's worse, in a way, because it's so
*ordinary.* A flat, grey, endless cold under a flat, grey sky, with a damp in the fog that finds its way
through every layer and sits on the chest, and a tiredness that comes too easy, and a warmth that beckons if
you'd only stop, only sit, only close your eyes here in the soft white quiet.
**The situation.** The tundra's attrition hazard: damp, penetrating cold and the dream-adjacent fatigue it
brings (Arc D). The "warmth that beckons if you'd only stop" is the **dream's doorway** (Bible Thread 9) —
the same lull as the frozen biome, here laced with fog. Keep moving, keep warm, keep awake.
**Foes/DCs.** Constitution saves **DC [12 → 15]** at intervals without warmth; Wisdom save **DC [13]** against
the seductive urge to *stop and rest* in the open; Survival **DC [13]** to make a dry, warm cold-camp.
**Develops into.** Yield to the urge to stop and the **dream takes you** (Arc D) — you lie down in the soft
white and become the next **Smiling Drift** (Discovery 13). The fog and the cold carry the dream north
together (Bible Thread 9).

### 20. The Stampede — *Hazard · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** The ground begins to *drum*, far off and then suddenly not, and out of the fog the world
becomes a wall of moving animals — reindeer, or mammoth, or both, a whole herd panicked into a stampede by
something behind them, pouring through the white with no path and no mercy, and you are directly in their
line with seconds to clear it.
**The situation.** A megafauna stampede hazard (tying to Combat 3 and the fleeing herds, Social 8): a herd,
panicked by a predator or the fog-walker, thunders out of the white. Dive clear, find high ground, or turn
the herd (Animal Handling) — and ask *what panicked them.*
**Foes/DCs.** Dexterity save **DC [14]** to dive clear of the line; Acrobatics **DC [13]** to scramble onto a
rock or sledge above it; Animal Handling **DC [16]** to split the stampede around you.
**Develops into.** What panicked the herd comes *behind* them out of the fog — the **fog-walker** (Arc D), a
**Frost Giant** hunter (Combat 4), or the **Wolf-Winter** pressing north (Arc C). The stampede is the warning;
the hunter is the hook.

## Puzzle / skill-challenge (4)

### 21. Honouring the Fog-Walker — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The herder has shown you the shape in the fog, and named the price, and now it waits, patient
and indistinct, for you to choose *how* you pay. There is a rite — a thing freely given, offered in the
right way, with the right words, at the right edge of the light. Get it right and the night passes and no
one is taken. Get it wrong — or give grudgingly — and it takes one anyway, and the grudged gift costs double.
**The situation.** The tundra's central folk-horror puzzle (regional / Bible Section, Arc A's thesis in cold
form): perform the honouring rite correctly — the right *gift* (not the most valuable, the most *freely
given*), offered in the right manner, at the firelight's edge. Knowing the rule (from the herder, the
counting-stones, Wrenna) is the whole win. Cleverness without *generosity* fails.
**Foes/DCs.** Religion/History **DC [15]** to perform the rite correctly; Insight **DC [14]** to judge what
the fog-walker truly values (freely-given over precious); a *grudged* or *clever-but-stingy* offering fails —
the giving must be glad. A genuinely generous gift (roleplay) succeeds.
**Develops into.** The fog-walker honoured *gladly* lets the night and the party pass (Arc D) — and the
party have **rehearsed the Tithe** (Arc A): give gladly, the forest lets you walk out; grudge it, it takes
one doubled. The herd-folk's law and the forest's are *one law* (Bible Thread 9).

### 22. Navigating the Lying Fog — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** The fog moves the ground (Hazard 15), and your map is a liar's confession, and north is a
rumour — but there is a way through. The counting-stones march in a true line (Discovery 11). The fixed
cairn does not move (Discovery 14). The herd-folk have songs that hold a bearing the fog can't bend. Stitch
the true things together and you can cross what cannot, by eye, be crossed.
**The situation.** A navigation skill-challenge built on the tundra's defining trick: ordinary navigation
*fails by the fog's intent*, so the party must navigate by the **fixed points the fog can't move** —
counting-stones, the anchor-cairn, herd-folk wayfinding song, the feel of the true line. A puzzle of
*method*, not just rolls. Failure loops them deeper toward the fog-walker (Hazard 16).
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [16]** to navigate by fixed anchors instead of sight; Investigation **DC [14]** to
identify which landmarks are *true* (don't move) vs. fog-shifted; Performance/History **DC [13]** to use a
herd-folk wayfinding song. 3 successes before 2 failures.
**Develops into.** Crossing the lying fog by its *true* anchors is how the herd-folk survive (Arc D) — and a
rehearsal for the **void**, where "distance and direction come loose" and the same trick (fix on the true
thing) is the only way through to the **forest** (Arc A).

### 23. The Fog-Walker's Counting-Rule — *Puzzle · any tier*
**Read-aloud.** "It takes one. Always one." But *which* one, and *why*, and *when* — that's the thing no one
says straight. The counting-stones tally the pattern (Discovery 11). The herder's warnings circle it. The
fog-drowned camps show what breaking it costs (Discovery 12). Somewhere in all of it is the fog-walker's
*counting-rule* — the law of who it can take and who it can't — and knowing it is the difference between a
guest and a tally-mark.
**The situation.** A folk-horror deduction puzzle (Bible: "its counting-rules"): assemble the fog-walker's
*rule* — it takes one who leaves the light; it cannot take a guest at a fire; a freely-given gift pays the
night; grudging doubles the debt; it must be *answered*, not ignored. Deduce the rule from stones, lore, and
camps, and the party can *game* the bargain — even use it (against a foe, to save a taken friend).
**Foes/DCs.** Investigation **DC [15]** to extract the pattern from the counting-stones; History/Religion
**DC [16]** to assemble the full rule; Insight **DC [13]** to interpret the herder's oblique hints. Assemble
3+ clues to derive the rule.
**Develops into.** The counting-rule is the **Tithe's rule** in miniature (Arc A / Bible Thread 9) — and the
fog-walker's ledger is kin to the **Collector's** (Arc E). Knowing it lets the party argue the forest's
bargain (Arc A) the way they learned to argue the fog's.

### 24. Recovering the Taken — *Puzzle · T2+*
**Read-aloud.** The fog took one of yours — walked them out into the white on a voice they couldn't refuse —
and they are not dead, not yet, only *taken*, somewhere out in the fog-walker's reach, dreaming or counted or
both. The herder shakes his head: most don't try. But the rule cuts both ways. If you understand what it
takes and *why*, you might understand how to ask for one back.
**The situation.** A rescue skill-challenge that *uses the fog-walker's own rules* (Puzzle 21/23): to recover
a taken companion (or NPC — a herd-folk, a **Smiling Drift** dreamer), the party must navigate the lying fog
(Puzzle 22), find where the taken are *kept* (the counting-stones, the moving-ground heart), and *honour the
rule* to ask one back — a freely-given gift, the right words, no grudging. Knowing the law is the rescue.
**Foes/DCs.** Survival **DC [15]** to reach the taken through the fog (Puzzle 22 applies); Religion/Insight
**DC [16]** to perform the asking-back rite; a *freely given* substitute-gift (roleplay) is the price — and
the fog-walker honours a glad exchange, refuses a stingy one.
**Develops into.** Recovering the taken by *honouring the rule* (not fighting it) is Arc A's thesis proven
(give gladly, and even the fog gives back). It foreshadows the **forest's** bargain — and whoever the fog
took may have been the **One Who Follows** all along (Arc F / Bible Thread 9).
